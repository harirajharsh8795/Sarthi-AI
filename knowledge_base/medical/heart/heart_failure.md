# Heart Failure (Congestive Heart Failure) ki Pehchan, Lakshan aur Upachar (Heart Failure and Ejection Fraction Guide)

## Overview

### English
Heart Failure, also known as Congestive Heart Failure (CHF), is a chronic, progressive cardiovascular condition characterized by the inability of the heart muscle to pump blood efficiently enough to meet the body's metabolic needs. It is classified by **Ejection Fraction (EF)** into Heart Failure with Reduced EF (HFrEF, EF $\le 40\%$, systolic failure) and Heart Failure with Preserved EF (HFpEF, EF $\ge 50\%$, diastolic stiffness). A key consequence is fluid retention, causing edema in the legs and backing up fluid into the lungs, leading to shortness of breath (dyspnea), especially when lying flat (orthopnea).

## Quick Summary
Heart failure is the heart's inability to pump blood efficiently. Key indicators are low Ejection Fraction (EF $< 40\%$) and high BNP. Symptoms include shortness of breath (especially when lying flat), swelling (edema) in the ankles, and chronic fatigue. Diagnostics use Echocardiography and BNP blood tests. Management involves limiting salt/fluids, taking diuretics (water pills), and using ACE inhibitors.

---

## Definition
* **Heart Failure:** A complex clinical syndrome resulting from any structural or functional impairment of ventricular filling or ejection of blood.
* **Ejection Fraction (EF):** The percentage of blood leaving the left ventricle each time the heart contracts. Normal EF is **50% to 70%**.
* **Orthopnea:** Shortness of breath (dyspnea) that occurs when lying flat, forcing the patient to sleep propped up on multiple pillows to breathe comfortably.

---

## Pathology of Fluid Build-up (Congestive Heart Failure)
When the pump fails, blood backs up in the venous system:
1. **Left-Sided Failure (Pulmonary Congestion):** The left ventricle cannot pump blood forward. Blood backs up into the left atrium and pulmonary veins. High pressure forces fluid into the lung alveoli, causing **Pulmonary Edema** (fluid in lungs) and breathing difficulty.
2. **Right-Sided Failure (Systemic Congestion):** The right ventricle cannot pump blood to the lungs. Blood backs up into the right atrium and vena cava. High venous pressure forces fluid into tissues, causing swelling in the ankles/feet (peripheral edema) and fluid in the abdomen (ascites).

## Symptoms of Heart Failure

### English
* Shortness of breath (dyspnea) during walking or at rest; waking up at night gasping for air (Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea - PND); inability to sleep flat without 2-3 pillows (orthopnea); swelling (edema) in the ankles, feet, legs, or abdomen; rapid weight gain (due to fluid buildup); chronic fatigue and cough with pink, frothy sputum.

## Emergency Protocol for Acute Pulmonary Edema (Fluid in Lungs)

### English
1. **Sit the patient completely upright** with their legs hanging over the side of the bed. This reduces blood return to the heart, clearing fluid from the lungs. **Do NOT let them lie flat.**
2. **Provide continuous high-flow oxygen** if available.
3. Call **108** for emergency cardiac transport.

1. **मरीज को बिल्कुल सीधा बैठाएं:** पैरों को बिस्तर से नीचे लटकाएं। उन्हें कभी भी सीधा न लेटाएं।
2. **ऑक्सीजन दें:** यदि उपलब्ध हो, तो तुरंत ऑक्सीजन लगाएं।
3. तुरंत 108 बुलाएं।

2. **Oxygen lagayein:** Aaspas oxygen cylinder ho to immediately high-flow oxygen shuru karein.
3. Immediate **108 ambulance** call karein.

## Diagnostic Evaluation
* **Echocardiogram (Echo):** The gold standard diagnostic scan. It utilizes ultrasound to check heart muscle movement, valve health, and calculate the **Ejection Fraction (EF)**.
* **Brain Natriuretic Peptide (BNP) Test:** A blood test checking levels of BNP hormone, which is released by stretched, failing heart muscles. **High BNP ($> 100$ pg/mL) confirms heart failure.**
* **Chest X-Ray:** Shows an enlarged heart shadow (cardiomegaly) and fluid accumulation in the lung fields (pulmonary venous congestion).

---

## Medicines
*Saarthi AI does not prescribe medicines. The following describes clinical drug categories used to treat heart failure:*
1. **Diuretics (Water Pills):** Force kidneys to excrete sodium and water, reducing body swelling and lung fluid (e.g., Furosemide, Torsemide).
2. **ACE Inhibitors / ARBs:** Relax blood vessels to lower heart workload (e.g., Ramipril, Telmisartan).
3. **ARNI (Angiotensin Receptor-Neprilysin Inhibitors):** Advanced heart failure drug combining Sacubitril and Valsartan.
4. **Beta-Blockers:** Protect heart muscle from adrenaline strain (e.g., Carvedilol, Metoprolol Succinate).
5. **Aldosterone Antagonists:** Weak diuretics that prevent heart muscle scarring (e.g., Spironolactone).
6. **SGLT2 Inhibitors:** Diabetes drugs proven to reduce heart failure hospitalizations (e.g., Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin).

---

## Lifestyle Restrictions (Critical Care)
* **Fluid Restriction:** Strictly limit daily fluid intake (water, tea, milk, dal, soup) to **1.0 to 1.5 Liters/day** (under cardiologist guidance) to prevent fluid overload.
* **Low Sodium Diet:** Limit salt intake to **$< 2-3$ grams/day**. Avoid papad, pickles, chips, and processed foods.
* **Weight Monitoring:** Weigh yourself daily in the morning. **A sudden weight gain of 1.5 to 2 kg in 48 hours indicates rapid fluid retention**, requiring an immediate increase in diuretic dose (consult doctor).

---

## Possible Complications of Heart Failure
* **Acute Pulmonary Edema:** Fluid completely filling the lungs, causing acute suffocation and respiratory failure.
* **Renal Failure (Cardiorenal Syndrome):** Poor heart pumping reducing blood supply to kidneys, causing kidney failure.
* **Fatal Ventricular Arrhythmias:** Damaged heart muscles triggering sudden cardiac arrest.

---

## Emergency Warning Signs
Go to the nearest emergency department immediately or call 108 if you observe:
* **Severe difficulty breathing, gasping for air, and coughing up pink, frothy sputum (Pulmonary Edema emergency).**
* **The patient feels dizzy, collapses, and goes into cardiac arrest.**
* **Chest pain accompanied by severe breathlessness and sweating.**

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is heart failure?**
   Heart failure is a chronic condition where the heart muscle becomes too weak or stiff to pump blood efficiently to meet the body's needs, leading to fluid backup and fatigue.

2. **What is Ejection Fraction (EF) in my report?**
   EF is the percentage of blood pumped out of the heart’s left ventricle with each heartbeat. A normal EF is between 50% and 70%. An EF below 40% indicates heart failure.

3. **Why do heart failure patients get swelling in their feet?**
   When the heart pumps poorly, blood backs up in the veins. This high pressure forces fluid out of the blood vessels and into the surrounding tissues, causing swelling (edema) in the ankles and feet.

4. **What is orthopnea?**
   Orthopnea is difficulty breathing when lying flat. Gravity spreads pooled fluid across the lungs when flat. Sleeping propped up on 2-3 pillows keeps the fluid down, making breathing easier.

5. **Why must heart failure patients limit their water intake?**
   Weak hearts cannot pump excess fluid. If they drink too much water, the extra fluid overloads the blood vessels, leaks into the lungs, and causes acute suffocation (pulmonary edema).

6. **What is a BNP blood test?**
   BNP (Brain Natriuretic Peptide) is a hormone released by heart cells when they are stretched due to fluid pressure. A high BNP level confirms active heart failure.

7. **Where can BPL cardholders get free heart failure ICU care in India?**
   Under the Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY scheme, eligible BPL cardholders can get free cashless ICU stays, ventilator support, and surgeries at all empaneled hospitals.

8. **What is a "water pill" (diuretic)?**
   Diuretics (like Furosemide) are medicines that force the kidneys to remove excess water and salt from the body through urine, reducing leg swelling and lung fluid.

9. **Why is salt restricted in heart failure?**
   Salt contains sodium. Sodium acts like a sponge, retaining water in the blood. This raises blood volume, putting extra work on the weak heart and worsening swelling.

10. **What is the difference between a heart attack and heart failure?**
    A heart attack is a sudden blockage of blood supply to the heart muscle. Heart failure is a chronic, long-term weakness of the heart's pumping power, often caused by past heart attacks.

11. **What is ARNI (Sacubitril-Valsartan)?**
    ARNI is a modern combination medicine that relaxes blood vessels, improves blood flow, and reduces strain on the heart, significantly reducing hospital stays.

12. **Why do heart failure patients need to weigh themselves daily?**
    Sudden weight gain (like 2 kg in 2 days) is not fat; it is a sign of rapid water retention. Catching this early allows the doctor to adjust diuretic doses before breathing problems start.

13. **Can high blood pressure cause heart failure?**
    Yes. High blood pressure forces the heart to pump harder against tight blood vessels. Over years, this constant strain causes the heart muscle to thicken and eventually stiffen and fail.

14. **What is PND (Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea)?**
    PND is a sudden attack of severe shortness of breath and coughing that wakes a patient from sleep, forcing them to sit upright at the edge of the bed to catch their breath.

15. **What is the price of generic Furosemide (Lasix) in India?**
    Generic Furosemide (40mg) tablets are available at PM Jan Aushadhi Kendras for only ₹3 to ₹5 per strip of 10 tablets.

16. **Why does heart failure cause a cough with pink, frothy sputum?**
    When fluid fills the lung sacs (alveoli), it mixes with air and tiny amounts of leaked red blood cells. Coughing up this mixture produces pink, foamy spit, a sign of severe pulmonary edema.

17. **What is CRT (Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy)?**
    CRT is a specialized pacemaker that sends electrical impulses to both ventricles, forcing them to contract in perfect harmony, improving pumping efficiency in severe heart failure.

18. **Can diabetes raise heart failure risk?**
    Yes. Diabetes causes microvascular damage to heart tissue and is associated with diabetic cardiomyopathy, which weakens the heart muscle directly.

19. **What is the difference between systolic and diastolic heart failure?**
    Systolic failure (HFrEF) means the heart muscle is weak and cannot squeeze blood out. Diastolic failure (HFpEF) means the heart muscle is thick and stiff, unable to relax and fill with blood.

20. **Does the Ayushman Bharat card cover heart valve replacement surgeries?**
    Yes, the PM-JAY scheme covers cashless packages for heart valve repair or replacement surgeries, ICU charges, and post-operative medications.

## Common Myths vs Facts

* **Myth:** Heart failure means the heart has completely stopped beating and the patient is dead.
  * **Fact:** Heart failure means the heart is still working, but not pumping blood efficiently. A stopped heart is called cardiac arrest.
* **Myth:** Drinking 5 liters of water daily is excellent to flush out toxins for heart failure patients.
  * **Fact:** Drinking excess water is highly dangerous for heart failure patients. It will overload the weak heart and fill the lungs with fluid. Restrict fluids as advised.
* **Myth:** Rock salt (sendha namak) is perfectly safe and does not cause fluid retention.
  * **Fact:** Rock salt contains sodium. It causes the same amount of water retention and must be restricted to under 1 teaspoon daily, just like table salt.
* **Myth:** Heart failure is a normal, unavoidable part of getting old.
  * **Fact:** While risk rises with age, heart failure is a disease caused by uncontrolled BP, past heart attacks, or diabetes. It can be prevented with proper care.
* **Myth:** Once you have heart failure, you can never do any exercise again.
  * **Fact:** Strenuous lifting is restricted, but mild daily walking (cardiac rehab) is highly recommended to strengthen the heart and improve blood flow.
* **Myth:** You should lie down flat if you feel severe shortness of breath.
  * **Fact:** Lying down flat spreads fluid across the lungs, making breathing worse. Always sit the patient upright with legs hanging down during breathing distress.
* **Myth:** Branded heart tonics are essential to repair weak heart muscles.
  * **Fact:** OTC heart tonics have no scientific proof. Only clinical medications (diuretics, ARNI, beta-blockers) are proven to prolong life.
* **Myth:** Heart failure patients only need to take medicines when they feel sick.
  * **Fact:** Heart failure requires lifelong, daily medication to prevent fluid buildup and protect the heart muscle, even if the patient feels completely healthy.
* **Myth:** A low Ejection Fraction (EF 30%) means you will die within weeks.
  * **Fact:** Many patients with an EF of 25-30% live active, comfortable lives for decades with modern drug therapies (like ARNI, SGLT2 inhibitors) and pacemakers.
* **Myth:** Heart failure only affects the heart.
  * **Fact:** Untreated heart failure affects the entire body: causing kidney congestion (renal failure), liver swelling, muscle wasting, and chronic fatigue.
