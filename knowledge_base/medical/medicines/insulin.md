# Insulin (Insulin) ki Pehchan, Types aur Hypoglycemia Care (Insulin Therapy and Low Blood Sugar Guide)

## Overview

### English
Insulin is a vital pancreatic hormone that regulates carbohydrate, fat, and protein metabolism by promoting the cellular uptake of glucose from the blood into skeletal muscle and fat tissues. In **Type 1 Diabetes**, absolute insulin deficiency mandates lifelong subcutaneous insulin replacement. In **Type 2 Diabetes**, insulin is indicated when oral hypoglycemic agents fail to maintain target HbA1c, or during acute stress (surgery, pregnancy [gestational diabetes]). The most critical, acute complication of insulin therapy is **Hypoglycemia** (blood sugar dropping below **$70\text{ mg/dL}$**), which can trigger seizures, coma, and death if not treated immediately with the **15-15 Rule**.

## Quick Summary
Insulin regulates cellular glucose uptake. Key classifications are rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, and long-acting. The primary emergency is hypoglycemia (blood sugar $< 70\text{ mg/dL}$). First aid requires immediate ingestion of 15g fast-acting sugar (Rule of 15). Diagnostics monitor blood glucose. Management uses basal-bolus schedules.

---

## Definition
* **Insulin Therapy:** The subcutaneous administration of recombinant human insulin or insulin analogs to manage diabetes mellitus.
* **Hypoglycemia:** A clinical state where blood glucose falls below $70\text{ mg/dL}$ ($3.9\text{ mmol/L}$), triggering autonomic and neuroglycopenic symptoms.
* **Basal-Bolus Regimen:** An advanced insulin schedule combining long-acting insulin (basal - once daily for background control) and rapid-acting insulin (bolus - before meals to control sugar spikes).

## Types of Insulin & Action Timelines

Different types of insulin are selected based on how fast they work and how long they last:

| Insulin Type | Clinical Example | Onset of Action | Peak Effect | Duration of Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rapid-Acting** | Insulin Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine | $15\text{ mins}$ | $1 - 2\text{ hours}$ | $3 - 5\text{ hours}$ |
| **Short-Acting (Regular)** | Actrapid, Humulin R | $30\text{ mins}$ | $2 - 3\text{ hours}$ | $5 - 8\text{ hours}$ |

## Symptoms of Hypoglycemia (Low Blood Sugar Emergency)

### English
* **Autonomic (Adrenaline release):** Sudden profuse sweating and cold shivers; rapid heartbeat (palpitations) and hand tremors; intense hunger and anxiety/panic.
* **Neuroglycopenic (Brain lacking glucose):** Dizziness, severe headache, and blurred vision; slurred speech and confusion/irrational behavior; extreme drowsiness, seizures, and loss of consciousness (insulin shock/coma).

### Hindi
* **शारीरिक लक्षण:** अचानक अत्यधिक ठंडा पसीना आना; दिल की धड़कन तेज होना और हाथ कांपना; तेज भूख लगना और घबराहट होना।
* **मस्तिष्क के लक्षण:** चक्कर आना, धुंधला दिखना, भ्रम (confusion) होना, और बेहोश होना (insulin coma)।

### Hinglish
* **Body Symptoms:** Achanak dher sara thanda pasina aana; heart beat tez hona aur haath kaapna; tej bhookh lagna aur ghabrahat hona.
* **Brain Symptoms:** Chakkar aana, aankhon ke aage andhera/blurred vision, bolne me ladkhadahat, confusion hona aur behosh hokar girna (insulin shock/coma).

## First Aid & Hypoglycemia Management (The 15-15 Rule)

### Immediate Action for Conscious Patient (Sugar $< 70\text{ mg/dL}$)

#### English
1. **Apply the 15-15 Rule immediately:**
   * **Step 1 (Give 15g Fast Sugar):** Give the patient **15 grams of fast-acting sugar** (e.g., 3-4 teaspoons of glucose powder/sugar dissolved in water, OR half a cup of fruit juice, OR 1 tablespoon of honey). *Do NOT give chocolate, biscuits, or milk, as the fat in them delays sugar absorption in the stomach.*
   * **Step 2 (Wait 15 mins):** Let the patient rest. Do not inject insulin.
   * **Step 3 (Re-check sugar):** Test blood sugar with a glucometer after 15 minutes.
   * **Step 4 (Repeat if needed):** If blood sugar is still $< 70\text{ mg/dL}$, repeat Step 1 (give another 15g sugar) and re-check after 15 minutes.
2. Once the sugar rises above $70\text{ mg/dL}$, feed the patient a complex carbohydrate meal (roti, sandwich, or khichdi) to prevent it from dropping again.

#### Hindi
1. **15-15 का नियम (Rule of 15) अपनाएं:**
   * **स्टेप 1:** तुरंत **15 ग्राम ग्लूकोज या चीनी** पानी में घोलकर पिलाएं (या आधा कप जूस)। चॉकलेट या बिस्कुट न दें (इनमें मौजूद वसा शुगर सोखने में देरी करती है)।
   * **स्टेप 2:** 15 मिनट तक आराम करने दें।
   * **स्टेप 3:** 15 मिनट बाद ग्लूकोमीटर से शुगर नापें।
   * **स्टेप 4:** यदि शुगर अभी भी 70 से कम है, तो स्टेप 1 दोबारा दोहराएं।
2. शुगर 70 से ऊपर आने पर मरीज को रोटी या भोजन खिलाएं।

#### Hinglish
1. **Rule of 15 (15-15 ka Niyam) apply karein:**
   * **Step 1 (15g Sugar):** Patient ko turant **15 grams fast sugar** (3-4 chhamach glucose/chini paani me ghol kar, ya 1 glass fruit juice, ya 1 chhamach honey) khilayein. *Chocolate, biscuits ya milk na dein kyunki inme fat hota hai jo sugar absorb hone me late karta hai.*
   * **Step 2 (Wait 15 mins):** Patient ko rest karne dein.
   * **Step 3 (Check Sugar):** 15 mins baad digital **Glucometer** se blood sugar dobara check karein.
   * **Step 4 (Repeat):** Agar sugar abhi bhi < 70 mg/dL hai, to dobara 15g sugar dein aur 15 mins baad check karein.
2. Sugar 70 se upar aane par patient ko normal food (roti/sandwich) khilayein taaki sugar dobara drop na ho.

### Immediate Action for Unconscious Patient

#### English
* **Do NOT try to pour liquids or food into the mouth of an unconscious patient.** This will cause choking and aspiration into the lungs, leading to death.
* **Rub glucose gel or honey inside their cheeks** (buccal mucosa) so it can be absorbed directly through the skin, while calling 108.
* **Inject Glucagon (1mg Subcutaneous/IM)** if available at home. Otherwise, transport immediately to an emergency room for **IV Dextrose (25% or 50%)** infusion.

#### Hindi
* **बेहोश मरीज के मुंह में पानी या चीनी न डालें:** इससे फेफड़ों में पानी (choking) जा सकता है।
* **गालों के भीतर शहद या ग्लूकोज जेल रगड़ें।**
* **तुरंत अस्पताल ले जाएं:** ड्रिप द्वारा डेक्सट्रोज (IV Dextrose) चढ़वाना आवश्यक है।

#### Hinglish
* **Behosh patient ke muh me paani/chini na dalein:** Agar patient behosh hai, to unke muh me paani ya sugar syrup na dalein warna wo saans ki nali me phans kar suffocation (choking) kar dega.
* **Cheeks me honey lagayein:** Muh ke side me (buccal mucosa) honey ya glucose gel rub karein taaki skin se absorb ho.
* **108 call karein:** Immediate emergency hospital lekar bhagein jahan doctor unhe **IV Dextrose (glucose drip)** lagayenge.

## Correct Insulin Injection Technique
To ensure proper absorption and avoid complications:
1. **Injection Route:** Subcutaneous (fat layer under skin), not intramuscular.
2. **Injection Sites:** Abdomen (at least 2 inches away from the belly button), thighs, outer arms, or buttocks.
3. **Site Rotation:** Rotate injection sites daily. Injecting in the exact same spot repeatedly causes **Lipohypertrophy** (accumulation of hard fat lumps under the skin), which stops insulin from absorbing normally, causing erratic blood sugar spikes and drops.
4. **Storage:** Keep unopened insulin vials in the refrigerator ($2^\circ\text{C} - 8^\circ\text{C}$). Once opened, a vial can be kept at room temperature (below $30^\circ\text{C}$) for up to 28 days. Do not freeze insulin.

## Prevention of Hypoglycemia
* **Never Skip Meals:** Always eat your meal within 15-30 minutes after injecting short/rapid-acting insulin.
* **Monitor Sugar Regularly:** Check blood glucose before and after exercise, and before bed.
* **Adjust Dose for Exercise:** Intense exercise burns glucose; lower the insulin dose or eat a snack before working out, under medical advice.
* **Carry Fast Sugar:** Always carry glucose packets or sugar candies in your pocket.

---

## Possible Complications of Insulin Misuse
* **Severe Hypoglycemic Coma (Insulin Shock):** Irreversible brain damage from lack of glucose.
* **Lipodystrophy (Lipohypertrophy):** Hard fat lumps at injection sites.
* **Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA):** Occurs in Type 1 diabetics if they miss their insulin doses, causing fatal blood acid buildup.

---

## Emergency Warning Signs
Go to the nearest emergency department immediately or call 108 if you observe:
* **A diabetic patient on insulin becomes unresponsive, confused, or goes into a coma.**
* **The patient develops seizures or fits.**
* **Blood sugar remains below $50\text{ mg/dL}$ despite giving sugar/juice twice.**

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is insulin?**
   Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that allows cells to absorb glucose from the blood to be used for energy.

2. **What is Hypoglycemia?**
   Hypoglycemia is a low blood sugar emergency where glucose levels drop below 70 mg/dL, causing sweating, palpitations, and confusion.

3. **What is the 15-15 rule for low blood sugar?**
   Eat 15g of fast-acting sugar, wait 15 minutes, recheck blood sugar. If still below 70 mg/dL, repeat the process.

4. **Why should I not give chocolate to treat low blood sugar?**
   Chocolate contains fats. Fat slows down digestion and delays glucose absorption in the stomach. Use glucose powder, sugar water, or fruit juice instead.

5. **Where should I inject insulin?**
   Inject into the subcutaneous fat of the abdomen (avoiding the belly button), thighs, or outer arms.

6. **What is Lipohypertrophy?**
   It is the buildup of hard fat lumps under the skin caused by injecting insulin in the exact same spot repeatedly. It prevents insulin from absorbing properly.

7. **How do I store insulin?**
   Keep unopened vials in the refrigerator ($2^\circ\text{C}-8^\circ\text{C}$). Opened vials can be kept at room temperature for up to 28 days. Never freeze insulin.

8. **Where can I get cheap insulin in India?**
   High-quality generic insulin vials (like Human Mixard 30/70) are available at PM Jan Aushadhi Kendras for only 20-30% of their private market price.

9. **Can a pregnant diabetic take insulin?**
   Yes. Insulin is the safest and preferred drug for Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM) during pregnancy, as it does not cross the placenta to affect the baby.

10. **What is the difference between basal and bolus insulin?**
    Basal insulin is long-acting (e.g., Glargine) providing background control for 24 hours. Bolus insulin is rapid-acting (e.g., Aspart) taken before meals to control sugar spikes.

11. **Why does exercise cause low blood sugar in insulin users?**
    Muscles consume glucose rapidly during exercise. If you have active insulin in your body, it accelerates glucose uptake, causing sugar to drop. Eat a snack before exercise.

12. **Why should I never inject insulin directly into a muscle?**
    Intramuscular injection causes insulin to be absorbed too quickly, leading to a rapid, dangerous drop in blood sugar (severe hypoglycemia).

13. **What is Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)?**
    DKA is a life-threatening emergency in Type 1 diabetics. When they miss insulin, the body burns fat for fuel, producing acidic ketones that poison the blood.

14. **Why do I sweat and shake when my blood sugar is low?**
    Low sugar triggers the brain to release adrenaline (epinephrine) to force the liver to release stored glucose. Adrenaline causes sweating, rapid heartbeat, and tremors.

15. **What is the price of generic insulin syringes at Jan Aushadhi Kendras?**
    Generic disposable insulin syringes are available at PM Jan Aushadhi Kendras for only ₹3 to ₹5 per syringe.

16. **What is a glucagon injection?**
    Glucagon is a hormone injection used to treat severe hypoglycemia when the patient is unconscious and cannot take sugar by mouth.

17. **How often should I check my HbA1c?**
    Diabetic patients on insulin should check their HbA1c (3-month average sugar) every 3 to 6 months. Target is usually $< 7\%$.

18. **Can I reuse disposable insulin syringes?**
    No. Reusing syringes dulls the needle, causes skin damage (lipophypertrophy), and increases the risk of bacterial skin infections. Use a new needle every time.

19. **What is the role of the NPCDCS program?**
    It is a national program that assists in early screening of diabetes and provides free insulin support to children with Type 1 Diabetes in rural districts.

20. **Does the Ayushman Bharat card cover DKA ICU admissions?**
    Yes, the PM-JAY scheme covers cashless packages for DKA treatment, pediatric diabetic emergencies, insulin shock coma admissions, and all IV medications.

## Common Myths vs Facts

* **Myth:** If my blood sugar is low and I am feeling dizzy, I should inject extra insulin.
  * **Fact:** Injecting insulin will lower your sugar further, causing immediate coma and death. You need to eat sugar immediately.
* **Myth:** Sucking on a key cures the sweating and shaking of low blood sugar.
  * **Fact:** Sweating and shaking are warning signs of hypoglycemia. Sucking keys is a useless myth. Drink glucose water or juice immediately.
* **Myth:** Insulin is highly addictive, and once you start, your kidneys will fail.
  * **Fact:** Insulin is a natural hormone, not an addictive drug. Taking insulin protects your kidneys from damage (nephropathy) caused by high blood sugar.
* **Myth:** Senda namak (rock salt) regulates blood sugar, so insulin is not needed.
  * **Fact:** Rock salt has no effect on blood glucose or insulin levels. Type 1 diabetics must take daily insulin to survive.
* **Myth:** You can inject insulin anywhere on your body as long as it goes under the skin.
  * **Fact:** Insulin must only be injected in approved subcutaneous fat sites (abdomen, thighs, outer arms). Avoid injecting near joints, scars, or the belly button.
* **Myth:** If you miss a meal, you should still take your bolus (mealtime) insulin dose.
  * **Fact:** If you do not eat, taking mealtime insulin will cause a severe, life-threatening drop in blood sugar. Skip the bolus dose if you skip the meal.
* **Myth:** Type 2 diabetes can only be treated with tablets, never with insulin.
  * **Fact:** Over years, the pancreas in Type 2 diabetics can lose its ability to make insulin. They will require insulin injections to control blood sugar.
* **Myth:** You should massage the injection site after injecting insulin to help it absorb faster.
  * **Fact:** Massaging can cause the insulin to be absorbed too rapidly, risking sudden hypoglycemia. Just pinch, inject, count to 10, and release.
* **Myth:** An unconscious low-sugar patient should be forced to drink sugar syrup.
  * **Fact:** Never pour liquids into an unconscious person's mouth. It will enter the lungs, causing immediate choking and asphyxiation. Call 108.
* **Myth:** Insulin Mixard 30/70 means it contains 30% water and 70% medicine.
  * **Fact:** Mixard 30/70 is a premixed insulin containing 30% fast-acting insulin (for meal cover) and 70% intermediate-acting insulin (for background cover).
