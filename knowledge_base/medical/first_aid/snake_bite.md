# Saamp ke Katne ka Pratham Upachar (Snake Bite First Aid, Big Four, and ASV Guide)

## Overview

### English
A snake bite is a medical emergency that carries a high risk of systemic envenomation by venomous snakes. In India, the "Big Four" medically important venomous snakes are the Spectacled Cobra, Common Krait (neurotoxic - causing respiratory paralysis), Russell's Viper, and Saw-scaled Viper (hemotoxic - causing internal bleeding and acute kidney injury). Immediate first aid requires keeping the patient calm (panic elevates heart rate, spreading venom faster), keeping the bitten limb immobilized at or below the heart level using a splint or sling, and transporting the patient immediately to a hospital stocked with Polyvalent Anti-Snake Venom (ASV). Local incisions (cutting), sucking the wound, or applying tight tourniquets are strictly contraindicated.

## Quick Summary
A snake bite requires urgent ASV treatment. The "Big Four" venomous snakes in India cause neurotoxic paralysis or hemotoxic bleeding. First aid requires keeping the patient calm and immobilizing the limb at or below heart level. Never cut, suck, or tie a tight tourniquet. Diagnostics monitor clotting times (20WBCT). Management utilizes Polyvalent Anti-Snake Venom (ASV).

---

## Definition
* **Big Four Snakes:** The four venomous snake species responsible for the vast majority of snakebite deaths in India: Cobra, Krait, Russell's Viper, and Saw-scaled Viper.
* **Neurotoxic Venom:** Venom that attacks the nervous system, blocking signals to muscles, leading to bilateral ptosis (eyelid drooping), difficulty swallowing, and respiratory failure. (Common in Cobra and Krait bites).
* **Hemotoxic Venom:** Venom that destroys red blood cells, disrupts blood clotting factors, and damages blood vessels, leading to spontaneous bleeding from gums/urine and acute kidney failure. (Common in Viper bites).
* **20WBCT (20-Minute Whole Blood Clotting Test):** A simple, bedside diagnostic test where a patient's blood is kept in a dry glass tube for 20 minutes; if the blood does not clot, it confirms hemotoxic envenomation, indicating immediate need for ASV.

---

## Venom Pathology & Indian Big Four

The clinical presentation varies depending on the snake species:
1. **Spectacled Cobra (*Naja naja*) & Common Krait (*Bungarus caeruleus*):**
   * **Venom Type:** Neurotoxic.
   * **Mechanism:** Blocks acetylcholine receptors at neuromuscular junctions.
   * **Danger:** Causes diaphragm paralysis, leading to sudden respiratory arrest (suffocation). Krait bites often happen at night, are painless, and have barely visible fang marks.
2. **Russell's Viper (*Daboia russelii*) & Saw-scaled Viper (*Echis carinatus*):**
   * **Venom Type:** Hemotoxic.
   * **Mechanism:** Consumes fibrinogen, leading to disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).
   * **Danger:** Spontaneous bleeding, local tissue necrosis (tissue rotting), and acute kidney injury (AKI) due to myoglobinuria.

## First Aid Action Plan (WHO & National Guidelines)

### Do's (What to do)

#### English
* **Keep the patient absolutely calm and still.** Reassure them that most snakebites are from non-venomous snakes or are "dry bites" (no venom injected). Panic speeds up blood circulation, spreading venom faster.
* **Immobilize the bitten limb** immediately using a splint (piece of wood or cardboard) and keep it at or slightly below the heart level. Do not let the patient walk or run.
* **Remove rings, bracelets, or tight clothing** from the bitten limb, as severe swelling will occur and cut off blood flow.
* **Wash the bite gently** with clean water (do not scrub).
* **Transport the patient immediately** to the nearest government hospital that has Anti-Snake Venom (ASV) and ventilator support.

#### Hindi
* **मरीज को शांत रखें:** घबराहट से दिल की धड़कन बढ़ती है, जिससे जहर तेजी से फैलता है।
* **अंग को स्थिर करें:** काटे गए हाथ-पैर को लकड़ी या पट्टी (splint) से बांधें ताकि वह हिले नहीं।
* **तंग चीजें हटाएं:** अंगूठियां, घड़ियां तुरंत निकालें (सूजन आने से पहले)।

#### Hinglish
* **Patient ko calm (shaant) rakhein:** Reassure karein ki darna nahi hai. Ghabrahat se pulse badhti hai, jisse zeher body me tezi se phailta hai.
* **Limb ko immobilize karein:** Katne wale area ke niche lakdi (wood) ya cardbord rakh kar loose kapde se baandh dein (Splint) taaki wo hil na sake. Patient ko chalne na dein.
* **Rings aur jewelry nikal dein:** Swelling aane se pehle hath ki rings, watch aur tight kapde nikal dein, warna blood circulation block ho jayega.
* **Hospital transport:** Turant nearest government hospital ya primary health center (PHC) lekar bhagein jahan ASV available ho.

### Don'ts (What NOT to do)

#### English
* **Do NOT tie a tight tourniquet (rassi/cloth).** Tying a tight band cuts off arterial blood supply completely, trapping venom locally, which causes rapid gangrene (tissue death) and leads to limb amputation.
* **Do NOT cut or slash the bite wound.** Cutting does not release venom; it only causes severe bleeding, tendon damage, and deep bacterial infection.
* **Do NOT suck the venom out with your mouth.** You cannot extract venom, and the rescuer risks poisoning through tiny cuts in their mouth.
* **Do NOT apply ice, herbal pastes, chemicals, or cow dung.** They worsen tissue rotting (necrosis).
* **Do NOT try to catch or kill the snake.** This waste precious time and increases the risk of a second bite.

#### Hindi
* **टूर्निकेट (कसी हुई रस्सी) न बांधें:** इससे रक्त संचार पूरी तरह बंद हो जाता है, जिससे अंग सड़ सकता है और उसे काटना पड़ सकता है।
* **घाव पर चीरा न लगाएं:** ब्लेड से काटना नस काटने और संक्रमण का कारण बनता है।
* **मुंह से जहर न चूसें:** यह पूरी तरह से अप्रभावी और खतरनाक है।
* **सांप पकड़ने में समय बर्बाद न करें।**

#### Hinglish
* **Tight rassi (tourniquet) bilkul na bandhein:** Padosi aksar tight belt ya rassi bandh dete hain. Isse blood flow poori tarah ruk jata hai, jisse hath-pair sadne lagta hai (gangrene) aur use kaatna pad sakta hai.
* **Ghaw par blade se cut na lagayein:** Cut lagane se zeher nahi nikalta, balki arteries aur tendons cut jati hain aur severe infection ho jata hai.
* **Muh se zeher na choosein:** Muh se zeher choosna ek dangerous myth hai. Isse rescuer ki jaan bhi khatre me aa jati hai.
* **Ice ya Jadi-buti na lagayein:** Barf lagane se tissue rotting (necrosis) badhti hai.
* **Saamp ko pakadne me time waste na karein.**

## Medical Management in Hospital (Anti-Snake Venom - ASV)

### Defibrination & ASV Protocol

#### English
* If the patient shows signs of envenomation (neurotoxic paralysis or non-clotting blood in 20WBCT), **Polyvalent Anti-Snake Venom (ASV)** is administered intravenously.
* Polyvalent ASV in India neutralizes all "Big Four" venoms. The initial dose is usually 10 vials diluted in normal saline, infused over 1 hour. Repeat doses are given if symptoms persist.

#### Hindi
* कोबरा, करैत, वाइपर के जहर को बेअसर करने के लिए **पॉलीवेलेंट एंटी-स्नेक वेनम (ASV)** की ड्रिप (IV) चढ़ाना। प्रारंभिक खुराक 10 वायल (vials) होती है।

#### Hinglish

### Respiratory Support

#### English
* For neurotoxic bites (Krait/Cobra) causing diaphragmatic paralysis, the patient requires emergency endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilator support in the ICU.

## Medicines
*Saarthi AI does not prescribe medicines. The following describes clinical drug categories used in snakebite emergencies:*
1. **Active Immunotherapy (ASV):** Polyvalent Anti-Snake Venom (lyophilized powder reconstituted with sterile water, administered IV).
2. **Anticholinesterases:** Administered clinically in neurotoxic bites to temporarily improve muscle strength (e.g., Neostigmine injection, always given with Atropine to prevent side effects).
3. **Anticonvulsants:** Used if venom causes systemic seizures.
4. **Intravenous Fluids:** Maintain hydration and support kidneys in hemotoxic bites.
5. **Tetanus Toxoid Vaccine:** Routine prophylaxis for all puncture wounds.

## Prevention of Snake Bites
* **Use a Flashlight:** Always carry a torch/flashlight when walking outdoors at night in fields or village paths.
* **Wear Boots:** Wear thick leather boots and long trousers when working in high grass or farming.
* **Clear Vegetation:** Keep grass short around houses and clear piles of wood, bricks, or rubbish where snakes love to hide.
* **Use Mosquito Nets:** Kraits hunt at night and often enter huts, biting sleeping people on the floor. Sleeping on a bed under a tucked-in mosquito net prevents bites.

---

## Possible Complications of Untreated Bites
* **Asphyxial Suffocation:** Neurotoxic Krait/Cobra venom stopping breathing, causing death within 1-2 hours.
* **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI):** Hemotoxic Viper venom clogging kidney tubules with hemoglobin, leading to kidney failure.
* **Local Gangrene / Tissue Necrosis:** Rotting of skin and muscle around the bite site, requiring surgical debridement or amputation.

---

## Emergency Warning Signs
Go to the nearest emergency department immediately or call 108 if you observe:
* **The patient develops drooping eyelids (ptosis), difficulty speaking, or cannot swallow their saliva.**
* **The patient develops sudden breathing difficulty, gasping, or chest muscle retraction.**
* **Spontaneous bleeding starts from the gums, nose, or in urine.**
* **Severe, rapidly spreading pain and dark swelling around the bite site.**

## Government Health Programs (India)
* **National Programme for Prevention and Control of Snakebite:** Promotes training of rural doctors in ASV administration and ensures free availability of ASV vials at all Primary Health Centers (PHCs) and Community Health Centers (CHCs).
* **Ayushman Bharat (AB-PMJAY):** Covers cashless emergency packages for snakebite ICU care, ventilator support, and unlimited ASV administration.
* **ASHA Worker Guidelines:** ASHA workers receive incentives for quickly identifying snakebite victims and arranging transport to the nearest ASV-equipped center.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is the first aid for a snake bite?**
   Keep the patient completely calm, immobilize the bitten limb with a splint at or below heart level, remove jewelry, wash the bite gently, and transport them immediately to a government hospital.

2. **What are the "Big Four" venomous snakes in India?**
   The Spectacled Cobra, Common Krait, Russell's Viper, and Saw-scaled Viper. They cause over 90% of venomous snakebite deaths in India.

3. **Why is tying a tight rope (tourniquet) above the bite dangerous?**
   Tying a tight rope stops all arterial blood flow, trapping the concentrated venom in the limb. This leads to rapid tissue rot (gangrene), requiring the arm or leg to be amputated.

4. **Why is cutting the bite wound with a blade bad?**
   Cutting the wound does not release venom. It only causes dangerous bleeding, damages nerves and tendons, and leads to severe bacterial infections.

5. **What is Anti-Snake Venom (ASV) and how does it work?**
   ASV is a specialized medicine made from horse antibodies that binds to and neutralizes snake venom in the blood. Polyvalent ASV in India works against all Big Four snakes.

6. **What is a "dry bite"?**
   A dry bite is a bite by a venomous snake where no venom is injected. Around 30% to 50% of venomous snake bites are dry bites, but they still require hospital observation.

7. **How do I identify a neurotoxic snake bite?**
   Symptoms include drooping eyelids (ptosis), difficulty swallowing saliva, slurred speech, generalized weakness, and eventually, difficulty breathing.

8. **Where can BPL cardholders get free ASV in India?**
   ASV is provided completely free of cost at all government Primary Health Centers (PHCs), Community Health Centers (CHCs), and civil hospitals under the NHM.

9. **What bedside test is done to confirm viper venom?**
   The 20-Minute Whole Blood Clotting Test (20WBCT). A sample of the patient's blood is left in a dry glass tube; if it does not clot after 20 minutes, it indicates hemotoxic viper venom.

10. **Why are Krait bites so dangerous?**
    Krait bites often happen at night while people sleep on the floor. The bite is painless, causes no local swelling or wound, and the patient may wake up with severe breathing failure.

11. **Should I try to catch the snake to show the doctor?**
    No. Trying to catch or kill the snake wastes precious time and increases the risk of a second bite. Indian ASV is polyvalent, so identifying the snake is not required.

12. **Why does neurotoxic venom cause drooping eyelids?**
    The venom blocks the nerves supplying the muscles that lift the eyelids (levator palpebrae superioris), causing them to droop, which is the earliest sign of muscle paralysis.

13. **How does hemotoxic venom damage the kidneys?**
    Hemotoxic venom destroys blood cells and causes tissue breakdown. The kidneys try to filter these broken proteins (myoglobin), which clog the kidney filters, causing acute kidney failure.

14. **What is Neostigmine used for in snakebites?**
    Neostigmine is an anticholinesterase injection given in neurotoxic bites to temporarily raise acetylcholine levels, improving muscle strength and breathing.

15. **Why should the bitten limb be kept below the heart level?**
    Keeping the limb below heart level uses gravity to slow down the flow of venom from the limb back into the central bloodstream.

16. **Why do farmers get more snake bites during monsoon?**
    Rain fills snake holes with water, forcing them out. Farmers working in fields during monsoon are more likely to step on hidden snakes.

17. **What is the price of generic Anti-Snake Venom vials in India?**
    While it costs ₹400-₹600 per vial in private pharmacies, ASV is supplied completely free of cost at all government health clinics.

18. **Why are mosquito nets recommended to prevent kraits?**
    Kraits seek warmth and hunt rodents inside huts at night. Sleeping under a tucked-in mosquito net creates a physical barrier that prevents kraits from reaching you.

19. **What does local necrosis mean in a viper bite?**
    Viper venom contains digestive enzymes that destroy skin and muscle cells locally, causing the tissue to rot, turn black, and develop foul-smelling ulcers.

20. **Does the Ayushman Bharat card cover mechanical ventilator ICU stays for snakebites?**
    Yes, the PM-JAY scheme covers cashless packages for emergency ICU stays, ventilator support, ASV vials, and wound cleaning surgeries.

## Common Myths vs Facts

* **Myth:** Tie a tight rope (tourniquet) above the bite to stop the venom from reaching the heart.
  * **Fact:** Tight tourniquets trap the venom, cutting off all blood flow, causing rapid gangrene and muscle death, leading to amputation. Use a loose splint instead.
* **Myth:** Use a razor blade to cut an 'X' over the bite to drain the poison.
  * **Fact:** Cutting does not remove venom. It only causes severe blood loss, cuts tendons/nerves, and leads to deep infections. Keep the skin intact.
* **Myth:** Sucking the venom out with a suction cup or mouth is effective.
  * **Fact:** Sucking does not remove venom, which spreads instantly. The rescuer can absorb the venom through mouth cuts, putting two lives at risk.
* **Myth:** TANTRIK (snake charmers) can pull the venom out with mantras.
  * **Fact:** Tantriks cannot cure snake venom. Dry bites (where no venom was injected) heal on their own, making people believe in tantriks. Poisonous bites require immediate ASV.
* **Myth:** If you are bitten by a snake, you must drink hot ghee or alcohol.
  * **Fact:** Ghee and alcohol increase heart rate and blood flow, speeding up the spread of venom. They can also cause choking if the throat is paralyzed.
* **Myth:** Poisonous snakes always have a triangular head.
  * **Fact:** Many non-venomous snakes have triangular heads, and the highly venomous Common Krait has a rounded head. Do not rely on head shape.
* **Myth:** Applying a "snake stone" (laal pathar/black stone) on the bite sucks out the poison.
  * **Fact:** Snake stones have no scientific basis and do not absorb venom. They only delay essential hospital ASV treatment.
* **Myth:** Snakes bite humans to seek revenge.
  * **Fact:** Snakes do not have the brain capacity for memory or revenge. They bite humans only as a defensive reaction when stepped on or threatened.
* **Myth:** Pouring kerosene or acid on the bite neutralizes the venom.
  * **Fact:** Acid and kerosene cause severe chemical burns and destroy skin tissue but do not neutralize the venom in the bloodstream.
* **Myth:** You cannot give ASV if you do not know which snake bit the patient.
  * **Fact:** Indian anti-venom is Polyvalent, meaning a single injection contains antibodies against all Big Four snakes. Identifying the snake is not necessary.
