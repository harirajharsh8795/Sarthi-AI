# KFT (Kidney Function Test) ki Pehchan, Range aur Parikshan (Kidney Function Panel and Creatinine Guide)

## Overview

### English
A Kidney Function Test (KFT), also known as a Renal Function Test (RFT), is a group of blood and urine biochemistries performed to evaluate how well the kidneys filter metabolic waste from the blood. The primary blood markers include Serum Creatinine (waste from muscle metabolism) and Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN, waste from protein breakdown). These values are used to calculate the estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR), which determines the stage of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD). A KFT is essential before contrast scans (CT/MRI) and for monitoring patients with diabetes, hypertension, or taking nephrotoxic medications.

## Quick Summary
A KFT measures creatinine, urea, and electrolytes. Key diagnostic values define normal Creatinine ($0.6 - 1.2$ mg/dL) and normal eGFR ($> 90$ mL/min). Symptoms of kidney dysfunction include fluid swelling (edema) in legs and face, decreased urination, and nausea. Diagnostics calculate filtration rates. Management involves limiting salt/protein, avoiding pain relievers, and dialysis.

---

## Definition
* **Kidney Function Test (KFT):** A profile of tests including serum creatinine, blood urea, uric acid, and electrolytes (sodium, potassium) used to evaluate renal health.
* **eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate):** An estimate of how much blood passes through the glomeruli (kidney filters) each minute, calculated using serum creatinine, age, gender, and race.
* **Uremia:** A clinical syndrome characterized by high levels of urea and other nitrogenous waste compounds in the blood, secondary to kidney failure.

---

## Clinical Parameters & eGFR Stages
The KFT evaluates major renal filtration markers:
1. **Serum Creatinine:** The most reliable marker. Creatinine is produced at a constant rate by muscle breakdown and excreted solely by the kidneys. Elevated levels indicate reduced filtration.
2. **Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) / Blood Urea:** Urea is a protein waste product from the liver. Elevated levels can indicate kidney failure, dehydration, or high protein intake.
3. **Serum Uric Acid:** High levels can lead to kidney stones or gout.
4. **Serum Electrolytes (Sodium, Potassium):** Kidneys maintain fluid-electrolyte balance. High potassium (hyperkalemia) is dangerous for heart rhythm.
5. **eGFR Stages of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD):**
   * **Stage 1:** eGFR $\ge 90$ mL/min (Normal kidney function with protein leakage).
   * **Stage 2:** eGFR 60 - 89 mL/min (Mild decrease).
   * **Stage 3:** eGFR 30 - 59 mL/min (Moderate decrease; require nephrology care).
   * **Stage 4:** eGFR 15 - 29 mL/min (Severe decrease).
   * **Stage 5:** eGFR $< 15$ mL/min (Kidney Failure; require dialysis or transplant).

## Normal Reference Ranges (Adults)

| Parameter | Normal Range | Clinical Significance of Elevated Level |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Serum Creatinine** | $0.6 - 1.2$ mg/dL | Kidney damage, dehydration, muscle injury |
| **Blood Urea** | $15 - 40$ mg/dL | Kidney failure, dehydration, gastrointestinal bleed |
| **eGFR** | $> 90$ mL/min | Low eGFR ($<60$) indicates chronic kidney disease |
| **Serum Potassium** | $3.5 - 5.0$ mEq/L | Hyperkalemia; risks cardiac arrest |
| **Serum Sodium** | $135 - 145$ mEq/L | Hyponatremia (low sodium) causes confusion/seizures|

## Symptoms of Abnormal Kidney Function

### English
* Swelling (edema) in the ankles, feet, legs, or puffiness around the eyes; decreased urine output (oliguria); chronic nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite; persistent fatigue and metallic taste in the mouth; shortness of breath (fluid buildup in lungs); muscle cramps.

## Test Preparation & Lab Protocol
* **Preparation:** No strict fasting is required, but it is recommended to avoid eating heavy cooked meat (which temporarily spikes creatinine) 24 hours before the test.
* **Procedure:** A venous blood sample is drawn into a gold-capped (gel separator) or red-capped chemistry tube. A spot urine sample may also be requested to check for protein/albumin leakage (microalbuminuria).

---

## Medical Management of Results

### For Stage 5 Kidney Failure (eGFR < 15 / Creatinine > 6.0)

#### English
* Emergency nephrologist consultation for hemodialysis or planning a kidney transplant. Restrict daily fluids (usually $< 1$ Liter/day) and completely avoid high-potassium foods (bananas, coconut water).

#### Hindi
* हेमोडायलिसिस (hemodialysis) या गुर्दा प्रत्यारोपण (kidney transplant)। कम पोटैशियम वाला आहार लें और तरल पदार्थ सीमित करें।

#### Hinglish
* Urgent dialyis ya kidney transplant ki planning karein. Daily paani/liquids ka intake 1 Liter se kam karein aur potassium rich food (kela, nariyal paani) bilkul band karein.

### For Mild Kidney Strain (Creatinine 1.3 - 1.8)

#### English
* Identify the cause (mostly uncontrolled diabetes or hypertension). Stop taking NSAID painkillers (e.g., Diclofenac, Ibuprofen) immediately. Drink adequate water if caused by simple dehydration.

#### Hindi
* दर्द निवारक दवाएं (NSAIDs) तुरंत बंद करें। शुगर और बीपी नियंत्रित करें।

#### Hinglish
* Painkiller tablets (NSAIDs) turant band karein. Apni sugar aur high BP ki medicines regular lekar pressure control rakhein. Paani saaf aur paryapt piyein.

## Medicines
*Saarthi AI does not prescribe medicines. The following describes clinical drug categories related to renal management:*
1. **Phosphate Binders:** Prevent phosphorus buildup in blood in advanced kidney disease (e.g., Calcium Acetate, Sevelamer).
2. **Erythropoietin Injections:** Treat anemia caused by reduced renal hormone production (e.g., Darbepoetin Alfa).
3. **Potassium Binders:** Treat dangerous hyperkalemia in emergency (e.g., Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate, Lokelma).
4. **Diuretics (Loop Diuretics):** Force the kidneys to excrete water and sodium, reducing body swelling (e.g., Furosemide).
5. **Sodium Bicarbonate Tablets:** Treat metabolic acidosis (acid buildup) caused by failing kidneys.

## Prevention of Kidney Dysfunction
* **Control BP and Sugar:** Keep blood pressure $< 130/80$ mmHg and HbA1c $< 7.0\%$.
* **Avoid NSAID Painkillers:** Do not self-medicate with strong painkillers daily, as they constrict renal blood flow, causing chronic damage.
* **Stay Hydrated:** Drink 2 to 3 liters of clean water daily (unless restricted due to heart/kidney disease).
* **Limit Dietary Salt:** Sodium retention damages kidney filters. Limit salt to $< 5$ grams/day.

---

## Possible Complications of Untreated Renal Failure
* **Pulmonary Edema:** Fluid backing up into the lungs, causing acute suffocation and respiratory failure.
* **Hyperkalemic Cardiac Arrest:** High blood potassium stopping the heart muscle from beating.
* **Uremic Encephalopathy:** High urea levels poisoning brain cells, leading to confusion, seizures, coma, and death.

---

## Emergency Warning Signs
Go to the nearest emergency department immediately or call 108 if you observe:
* **The patient completely stops passing urine (anuria) for over 12-24 hours, accompanied by severe swelling.**
* **Severe difficulty breathing, gasping, or coughing up pink frothy fluid.**
* **Extreme confusion, slurred speech, muscle twitching, or seizures in a patient with kidney failure.**

## Government Health Programs (India)
* **Pradhan Mantri National Dialysis Program (PMNDP):** Provides completely free hemodialysis services to BPL patients at all government district hospitals and community health centers.
* **Ayushman Bharat (AB-PMJAY):** Covers cashless packages for peritoneal dialysis, hemodialysis sessions, nephrologist consults, and kidney transplant surgery costs.
* **Jan Aushadhi Kendras:** Offers generic renal medicines, phosphate binders, and generic blood pressure tablets at highly subsidized rates.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is a Kidney Function Test (KFT)?**
   KFT is a blood and urine panel checking creatinine, urea, and electrolyte levels to check how well your kidneys filter blood wastes.

2. **What is Serum Creatinine?**
   Creatinine is a waste product from normal muscle breakdown. Healthy kidneys filter it completely out of the blood. Elevated levels indicate kidney damage.

3. **What is eGFR and how is it calculated?**
   eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate) is a calculation checking kidney filtration speed based on blood creatinine, age, gender, and weight. Normal is $>90$ mL/min.

4. **Why is high potassium dangerous in kidney failure?**
   Failing kidneys cannot filter excess potassium. High blood potassium (hyperkalemia) disrupts the heart's electrical wiring, risking cardiac arrest.

5. **Do I need to fast before a KFT test?**
   Strict fasting is not required, but avoiding large amounts of cooked red meat for 24 hours before the blood draw prevents temporary creatinine spikes.

6. **What creatinine level indicates kidney failure?**
   A serum creatinine level consistently above 4.0 to 5.0 mg/dL (or eGFR $< 15$) indicates severe kidney failure, often requiring dialysis.

7. **How does high blood pressure damage the kidneys?**
   High blood pressure stiffens and narrows the tiny blood vessels in the kidneys, reducing blood supply and destroying the filtering units (nephrons).

8. **Where can BPL cardholders get free dialysis in India?**
   Eligible patients can get free hemodialysis services at government district civil hospitals under the Pradhan Mantri National Dialysis Program (PMNDP).

9. **Why do doctors check creatinine before a contrast CT scan?**
   Contrast dye can be toxic to the kidneys. Checking creatinine ensures the kidneys are healthy enough to clear the dye safely.

10. **Why should kidney patients avoid taking painkillers like Diclofenac?**
    NSAID painkillers block prostaglandins, narrowing the blood vessels that supply the kidneys. This acute drop in blood flow can cause sudden kidney failure.

11. **What is the difference between acute kidney injury (AKI) and chronic kidney disease (CKD)?**
    AKI is a sudden, temporary loss of kidney function (reversible with treatment). CKD is slow, progressive, and permanent kidney damage lasting over 3 months.

12. **Why does kidney disease cause swelling in the feet?**
    When kidneys fail, they cannot remove excess fluid and sodium. Gravity causes this extra fluid to pool in the ankles and feet (peripheral edema).

13. **What is microalbuminuria?**
    It is the leakage of tiny amounts of albumin protein in the urine, representing the earliest warning sign of diabetic kidney damage.

14. **How does diabetes lead to kidney failure?**
    High blood sugar damages the kidneys' delicate filtering capillaries (glomeruli) over years, leading to progressive diabetic nephropathy.

15. **What is Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)?**
    BUN measures the amount of nitrogen in your blood that comes from the waste product urea, produced when the liver breaks down proteins.

16. **Why do kidney patients become anemic?**
    Healthy kidneys produce erythropoietin, a hormone that stimulates red blood cell production. Damaged kidneys make less of this hormone, causing low hemoglobin.

17. **What is hemodialysis?**
    Hemodialysis is a medical treatment where a machine filters waste, toxins, and excess fluid from the patient's blood when their kidneys can no longer do so.

18. **Can drinking beer dissolve kidney stones?**
    No. Alcohol causes dehydration, which raises uric acid levels and makes kidney stones worse. Drink plain water instead.

19. **What does a low sodium level (hyponatremia) cause?**
    Low sodium causes brain cells to swell with fluid, leading to headaches, confusion, drowsiness, and seizures if critical.

20. **Does the Ayushman Bharat card cover kidney transplant surgery?**
    Yes, the PM-JAY scheme covers cashless surgery packages for kidney transplants, donor evaluations, ICU stays, and post-operative medications.

## Common Myths vs Facts

* **Myth:** If you are passing normal amounts of urine, your kidneys are 100% healthy.
  * **Fact:** In the early stages of kidney disease, kidneys can still produce urine but fail to filter toxic waste. High creatinine can exist with normal urine volumes.
* **Myth:** Drinking coconut water daily is excellent for all kidney patients.
  * **Fact:** Coconut water is rich in potassium. While healthy for normal kidneys, it is highly dangerous for advanced kidney failure patients who cannot filter potassium.
* **Myth:** Kidney failure is always painful.
  * **Fact:** Chronic kidney disease is a silent illness. It causes no physical pain in the back or abdomen until the final stages of kidney failure.
* **Myth:** Drinking 10 liters of water daily will cure chronic kidney disease.
  * **Fact:** Drinking excess water cannot repair scarred kidneys. In advanced kidney failure, drinking too much fluid actually overloads the heart and lungs.
* **Myth:** Dialysis is a permanent cure for kidney disease.
  * **Fact:** Dialysis does not cure kidneys; it only filters blood mechanically to keep the patient alive. A kidney transplant is the only long-term replacement.
* **Myth:** Once you start dialysis, your life expectancy is only a few months.
  * **Fact:** Many patients live active, productive lives on regular dialysis for 10-15 years or more, especially when preparing for a transplant.
* **Myth:** Kidney damage only happens to alcoholics.
  * **Fact:** The leading causes of kidney failure worldwide and in India are uncontrolled diabetes and high blood pressure, not alcohol.
* **Myth:** A single high creatinine reading means you need immediate transplant.
  * **Fact:** A high reading can be caused by dehydration or temporary illness (AKI), which is reversible. CKD is diagnosed only after 3 months of low eGFR.
* **Myth:** Kidney stones always lead to kidney failure.
  * **Fact:** Most kidney stones pass without causing permanent damage. stones only cause failure if they block both kidneys' urine flow for a long period.
* **Myth:** Painkillers are completely safe for kidneys if taken with a glass of milk.
  * **Fact:** Milk does not stop the chemical action of NSAIDs on kidney blood vessels. Avoid self-medicating with painkillers.
