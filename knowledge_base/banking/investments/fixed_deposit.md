# Fixed Deposit (FD) - Comprehensive Guide

## Overview

**English:** A Fixed Deposit (FD) is a financial instrument provided by banks and Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs) which provides investors a higher rate of interest than a regular savings account, until the given maturity date. It may or may not require the creation of a separate account.

**Hindi:** फिक्स्ड डिपॉजिट (FD) या सावधि जमा एक वित्तीय साधन है जो बैंकों और गैर-बैंकिंग वित्तीय कंपनियों (NBFCs) द्वारा प्रदान किया जाता है। यह निवेशकों को एक निश्चित परिपक्वता तिथि तक नियमित बचत खाते की तुलना में उच्च ब्याज दर प्रदान करता है।

**Hinglish:** Fixed Deposit (FD) ek aisi investment scheme hai jo banks aur NBFCs offer karte hain. Isme aapko ek fixed time period ke liye apna paisa jama karna hota hai, jis par aapko normal savings account se zyada interest rate milta hai.

## Quick Summary
- **Primary Purpose:** Wealth preservation, fixed and guaranteed returns, capital protection.
- **Tenure:** Ranges from 7 days to 10 years.
- **Interest Payouts:** Monthly, Quarterly, Half-yearly, Annually, or at Maturity (Cumulative).
- **Insurance:** Covered up to ₹5 Lakhs by DICGC.
- **Premature Withdrawal:** Allowed in most FDs, subject to a small penalty (usually 0.5% to 1%). Tax-saving FDs have a lock-in of 5 years.

## Definition
A Fixed Deposit is a secured investment tool where a lump sum amount is deposited with a financial institution for a pre-determined period at a fixed interest rate. The interest rate remains constant throughout the tenure of the deposit, safeguarding the investor against market fluctuations and ensuring a guaranteed return on maturity. 

## Why It Matters
Fixed Deposits are the cornerstone of conservative financial planning in India. They matter because they provide immense financial security, predictability, and liquidity. For senior citizens, they act as a steady source of income (via monthly or quarterly interest payouts). For risk-averse investors, FDs eliminate market volatility risks. Moreover, tax-saving FDs offer deductions under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act, making them an essential instrument for holistic tax planning.

## Eligibility
- **Resident Indians:** Any individual resident in India can open an FD.
- **Non-Resident Indians (NRIs):** Can open NRO, NRE, and FCNR fixed deposits.
- **Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs):** Eligible to open FD accounts in the name of the HUF.
- **Minors:** FDs can be opened in the name of a minor by a natural or legal guardian.
- **Senior Citizens:** Individuals aged 60 years and above are eligible for special senior citizen FD rates (usually 0.50% higher).
- **Entities:** Proprietorships, Partnerships, Limited Liability Partnerships (LLPs), Companies, Trusts, and Societies are eligible.

## Required Documents
- **Identity Proof:** Aadhaar Card, PAN Card, Passport, Voter ID, Driving License.
- **Address Proof:** Aadhaar Card, Passport, Utility Bills (Electricity, Water, Gas), Bank Statement (not older than 3 months).
- **Photographs:** Passport size photographs of the applicant(s).
- **PAN Card:** Mandatory for FDs exceeding ₹50,000 or if the total interest earned exceeds the TDS threshold. Form 15G/15H can be submitted for non-deduction of TDS if eligible.
- **Entity Documents:** For non-individuals, documents like Certificate of Incorporation, Partnership Deed, Board Resolution, Trust Deed, etc., are required.

## Features & Benefits
1. **Guaranteed Returns:** The primary feature of an FD is the assurance of returns. Unlike equity or mutual funds, the interest rate agreed upon at the time of opening the FD remains unchanged, regardless of how the RBI repo rate or market conditions fluctuate during the tenure. This makes financial planning extremely reliable.
2. **Flexible Tenures:** FDs offer massive flexibility in terms of the duration of the deposit. Investors can choose short-term deposits starting from just 7 days to park temporary surplus funds, or go for long-term deposits up to 10 years for long-term goals like retirement or children's education.
3. **Loan Against FD (Overdraft Facility):** In times of emergency, investors don't necessarily have to break their FD and lose out on interest or pay premature withdrawal penalties. They can avail of a loan or overdraft facility up to 85%-90% of the FD amount. The interest charged on this loan is typically 1% to 2% higher than the FD interest rate.
4. **DICGC Insurance Cover:** To protect depositors' interests, the Reserve Bank of India, through the Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation (DICGC), provides insurance cover of up to ₹5 Lakhs per depositor per bank. This covers both principal and interest amounts, ensuring peace of mind for the investor.
5. **Tax Benefits (Tax-Saver FDs):** A specific category of Fixed Deposits, known as Tax-Saving FDs, comes with a mandatory lock-in period of 5 years. The principal amount invested in these FDs, up to ₹1.5 Lakhs per financial year, is eligible for deduction under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act, 1961.

## Risks
- **Financial Risk:**
  - **Inflation Risk:** If the inflation rate in the economy is higher than the post-tax interest rate earned on the FD, the real rate of return is negative. This means the purchasing power of the money actually decreases over time despite the nominal interest earned.
  - **Reinvestment Risk:** When an FD matures during a low-interest-rate regime, the investor will have to reinvest the maturity proceeds at a lower interest rate, thereby reducing future income.
- **Technical Risk:**
  - Glitches in net banking or mobile banking applications can occasionally delay the instant opening or closure of an FD. However, the core banking system reliably maintains the transaction ledgers.
- **Legal Risk:**
  - If PAN is not provided or KYC is incomplete, the bank may deduct TDS at a higher penal rate (usually 20%). Furthermore, disputes in joint accounts without clear mandate instructions (e.g., Either or Survivor) can lead to legal hassles during premature closure or death claims.
- **Cyber Risk:**
  - Phishing attacks where fraudsters trick customers into sharing OTPs to prematurely break the FD and transfer the funds to unauthorized accounts. The FD itself is safe on the bank's servers, but the linked savings account must be protected.

## Charges & Fees
- **Premature Withdrawal Penalty:** Typically ranges from 0.5% to 1.0% on the applicable interest rate for the period the deposit has run. (E.g., If you booked an FD for 5 years at 7%, but withdrew after 1 year, and the 1-year rate is 6%, the bank will pay you 6% minus 0.5% = 5.5% for that 1 year).
- **TDS (Tax Deducted at Source):** The bank deducts 10% TDS if the interest earned across all branches of the bank exceeds ₹40,000 (₹50,000 for senior citizens) in a financial year. If PAN is not submitted, TDS is deducted at 20%.
- **No Processing Fee:** There is zero processing fee or account opening charge for initiating or maintaining a fixed deposit.

## RBI / Government Rules
- **DICGC Act, 1961:** Mandates that every bank depositor is insured up to a maximum of ₹5,00,000 (Rupees Five Lakhs) for both principal and interest amount held by them in the same right and same capacity.
- **Section 194A of Income Tax Act:** Governs the rules for the deduction of tax at source (TDS) on interest other than interest on securities (i.e., FD interest).
- **RBI Master Direction - Reserve Bank of India (Interest Rate on Deposits) Directions, 2016:** Empowers banks to offer differential rates of interest on bulk deposits, gives them the freedom to formulate penal interest policies for premature withdrawal, and outlines the mandate for offering higher rates to senior citizens.

## Step-by-Step Process
**Process for Opening an FD Online:**
1. Log in to your Bank's Net Banking or Mobile Banking app.
2. Navigate to the 'Deposits' or 'Investments' section and select 'Open Fixed Deposit'.
3. Enter the deposit amount and select the desired tenure (months/days/years).
4. Choose the payout option: Cumulative (interest at maturity) or Non-Cumulative (monthly/quarterly interest payout).
5. Ensure auto-renewal instructions are set according to your preference (Auto-renew principal, Auto-renew principal + interest, or Do not auto-renew).
6. Nominate a beneficiary (mandatory or highly recommended).
7. Confirm the terms and conditions and authenticate via OTP or transaction PIN.
8. The amount is debited from the savings account, and an FD advice/receipt is generated instantly.

**Process for Premature Closure:**
1. Go to the 'Deposits' section in Net Banking.
2. Select the specific FD you wish to break.
3. Choose the option 'Premature Withdrawal' or 'Close FD'.
4. Review the penalty details and final payout amount.
5. Authenticate the transaction. The funds will be credited to the linked savings account immediately.

## Safety Tips
- **Never share OTPs:** Banks will never call you asking for an OTP to "secure" your FD or "upgrade" your interest rate.
- **Form 15G/15H:** Submit these forms at the beginning of the financial year to prevent TDS deduction if your total income is below the taxable limit.
- **Check Auto-Renewal:** Always verify the auto-renewal status of your FD. Sometimes banks auto-renew at the prevailing rate, which might be lower, or for a tenure you didn't intend.
- **Update Nominee:** Always ensure that your FD has an updated nominee to avoid legal complexities for your family in case of an unfortunate event.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ignoring Post-Tax Returns:** Many investors look at the 7-8% headline interest rate and ignore the fact that FD interest is fully taxable as per their income tax slab. For someone in the 30% bracket, a 7% FD yields only around 4.9% post-tax.
2. **Locking all funds in one single FD:** If an emergency arises, breaking a large FD incurs a penalty on the whole amount. It is better to use "FD Laddering" (breaking the amount into smaller FDs of different tenures).
3. **Forgetting to submit Form 15G/15H:** Investors often forget this, resulting in the bank deducting TDS. While they can claim it back during ITR filing, the money gets unnecessarily blocked.
4. **Overlooking the Premature Penalty:** While chasing a slightly higher interest rate for a 5-year tenure, investors might ignore the liquidity aspect and face high penalties when they need the money in year 2.
5. **Selecting the wrong payout option:** An individual seeking regular income might mistakenly select a 'Cumulative' FD, locking their interest until maturity, defeating their purpose.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: What happens if I withdraw my FD before maturity?**
A1: You can withdraw your FD before maturity, but the bank will usually charge a penalty of 0.5% to 1% on the interest rate applicable for the period the FD remained with the bank. The adjusted principal and interest will be credited to your account. Tax-saving FDs cannot be withdrawn prematurely.

**Q2: Can I add funds to an existing FD?**
A2: No, you cannot add more money to an existing Fixed Deposit. A fixed deposit is a one-time lump sum investment. If you have additional funds, you need to open a new, separate FD account at the current prevailing interest rates.

**Q3: Is the interest rate locked for the entire tenure?**
A3: Yes, once you book an FD, the interest rate is locked in for the entire tenure. Even if the RBI changes repo rates or the bank changes its FD rates later, your existing FD will continue to earn the rate agreed upon at the time of booking.

**Q4: How is TDS calculated on FD?**
A4: If the total interest earned from all your FDs in a specific bank exceeds ₹40,000 in a financial year (₹50,000 for senior citizens), the bank deducts 10% TDS. If you haven't provided your PAN card, the TDS deduction rate jumps to 20%.

**Q5: What is a Tax-Saving FD?**
A5: A tax-saving FD is a special type of deposit that comes with a strict 5-year lock-in period. The amount invested, up to ₹1.5 Lakhs in a financial year, qualifies for deduction under Section 80C. Premature withdrawal or loans against this FD are not permitted.

**Q6: What is the difference between cumulative and non-cumulative FD?**
A6: In a cumulative FD, the interest is compounded and paid out along with the principal at the end of the maturity period. In a non-cumulative FD, the interest is paid out periodically (monthly, quarterly, etc.) to provide a regular income stream.

**Q7: Can a minor open an FD?**
A7: Yes, an FD can be opened in the name of a minor, but it must be operated by a natural guardian (parents) or a legal court-appointed guardian until the minor turns 18. Once the minor attains majority, the account is converted to a regular individual account.

**Q8: Can I get a loan against my FD?**
A8: Yes, most banks offer a loan or overdraft facility against an FD. You can usually borrow up to 85%-90% of your deposit amount. The interest rate on this loan is typically 1% to 2% higher than the rate you are earning on the FD.

**Q9: What happens if the account holder passes away?**
A9: If the FD has a nominee, the bank will pay the maturity proceeds (or premature closure amount without penalty) to the nominee upon submission of the death certificate. For joint accounts with "Either or Survivor" mandate, the survivor takes control of the FD.

**Q10: Is FD interest taxable if I don't fall in the taxable income bracket?**
A10: The interest is still considered income, but if your total income is below the taxable limit (e.g., ₹2.5 Lakhs / ₹3 Lakhs as per the regime), you have zero tax liability. To prevent the bank from deducting TDS, you must submit Form 15G (or 15H for senior citizens).

**Q11: Are Corporate/NBFC FDs safe?**
A11: Corporate FDs often offer higher interest rates than bank FDs, but they carry higher risk as they are not covered by the ₹5 Lakh DICGC insurance. It is crucial to check the credit rating (like CRISIL AAA or ICRA MAAA) before investing in corporate FDs.

**Q12: Can NRIs open Fixed Deposits in India?**
A12: Yes, NRIs can open fixed deposits in India through NRO (Non-Resident Ordinary), NRE (Non-Resident External), and FCNR (Foreign Currency Non-Resident) accounts. The taxability and repatriability rules vary for each type.

**Q13: How is FD interest calculated?**
A13: For cumulative FDs, interest is usually compounded quarterly. The formula used is A = P(1 + r/n)^(n*t), where P is principal, r is annual interest rate, n is compounding frequency per year (usually 4), and t is time in years. 

**Q14: What is auto-renewal of an FD?**
A14: If you select auto-renewal, the bank will automatically renew your FD for the same tenure at the prevailing interest rate on the date of maturity. You can choose to renew just the principal or the principal plus the accumulated interest.

**Q15: Does my credit score affect my FD?**
A15: No, your credit score (CIBIL score) has absolutely no impact on your ability to open a fixed deposit or the interest rate you receive. FDs are an asset/investment, not a credit facility, so past credit behavior is irrelevant.

**Q16: Can I change the nominee of an FD later?**
A16: Yes, you can change, add, or remove the nominee for your fixed deposit at any time during its tenure by submitting a nomination modification form to the bank branch or updating it via net banking.

**Q17: What is sweeping-in and sweeping-out facility?**
A17: Many banks offer a 'Flexi FD' or 'Sweep-in' facility. When your savings account balance exceeds a certain threshold, the excess is automatically 'swept out' into an FD to earn higher interest. If your savings balance falls low, funds are 'swept in' from the FD to clear transactions.

**Q18: Can I open a joint FD with my spouse?**
A18: Yes, you can open a joint FD. The primary account holder's PAN will be considered for TDS deduction. You can set the operational mandate as 'Jointly', 'Either or Survivor', 'Former or Survivor', etc.

**Q19: Are there any age limits for senior citizen FDs?**
A19: To qualify for the senior citizen FD interest rates, the primary account holder must have completed 60 years of age on the date of opening the deposit. Super senior citizens (80+ years) sometimes get even higher rates in select banks.

**Q20: What is the minimum amount required to open an FD?**
A20: The minimum amount varies across banks. While public sector and private banks generally require a minimum of ₹1,000 to ₹10,000 to open an FD, some digital banks and post offices allow you to start an FD with even lower amounts.

## Common Myths vs Facts

1. **Myth:** FD returns are completely tax-free.
   **Fact:** FD interest is fully taxable according to your income tax slab. Only the principal up to ₹1.5L in a 5-year tax-saving FD qualifies for an 80C deduction.
   
2. **Myth:** If a bank fails, all my FD money is lost forever.
   **Fact:** Your deposits (Savings + FD + RD) are insured up to ₹5,00,000 per bank by the DICGC, an RBI subsidiary.

3. **Myth:** You cannot withdraw a Tax-Saving FD under any circumstances.
   **Fact:** While generally locked for 5 years, in the tragic event of the primary account holder's death, the nominee or legal heir can withdraw the tax-saving FD prematurely.

4. **Myth:** You can add extra money to your ongoing FD to increase returns.
   **Fact:** An FD is a strictly one-time lump-sum deposit. For regular additions, you should open a Recurring Deposit (RD).

5. **Myth:** TDS is the total tax you have to pay on FD interest.
   **Fact:** TDS is just a 10% advance tax collected by the bank. If you fall in the 30% tax bracket, you still have to pay the remaining 20% as self-assessment tax while filing your ITR.

6. **Myth:** I don't need a nominee if I have a joint account.
   **Fact:** Adding a nominee is crucial even for joint accounts, as it dictates who gets the funds if both joint holders pass away simultaneously.

7. **Myth:** Senior citizen rates apply automatically when the account matures.
   **Fact:** Senior citizen rates are determined on the day the FD is booked or renewed. If you turn 60 during an ongoing FD tenure, the rate doesn't jump automatically mid-tenure.

8. **Myth:** Corporate FDs are just as secure as Bank FDs.
   **Fact:** Corporate FDs carry higher credit risk and do not possess the ₹5 Lakh DICGC insurance blanket that Bank FDs enjoy.

9. **Myth:** It's always best to book the longest tenure FD for highest rates.
   **Fact:** Banks often offer their highest interest rates for specific medium-term durations (e.g., 400 days, or 15-18 months) rather than standard 5 or 10-year periods.

10. **Myth:** Online FDs aren't as safe as getting a physical paper receipt from the branch.
    **Fact:** Digital FDs are perfectly safe and instantly recorded in the bank's core system. E-receipts carry the same legal weight as a physical stamp paper receipt.

## Government Services
- **DICGC:** The Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation guarantees the safety of FDs up to ₹5 Lakhs across commercial banks, small finance banks, and co-operative banks.
- **DigiLocker:** Depositors can link their PAN/Aadhaar and pull digital FD receipts and TDS certificates (Form 16A) directly from participating banks into their DigiLocker.
- **ITR Portals (AIS/TIS):** The interest earned on FDs is proactively tracked and mapped to the PAN card. It automatically appears in the Annual Information Statement (AIS) of the Income Tax e-filing portal, ensuring transparency.
