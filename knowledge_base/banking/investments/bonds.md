# Complete Guide to Bonds (Government & Corporate)

## Overview

**English**: A bond is a fixed-income investment representing a loan made by an investor to a borrower (typically corporate or governmental). Bonds are used by companies, municipalities, states, and sovereign governments to finance projects and operations. When you buy a bond, you are lending your money to the issuer for a specific period, in return for regular interest payments (coupons) and the return of the principal amount at maturity.

## Quick Summary
- **Instrument Type**: Debt/Fixed-Income Security.
- **Risk Level**: Low to Moderate (Government bonds are virtually risk-free; corporate bonds carry credit risk).
- **Return Rate**: Generally higher than savings accounts; varies based on credit rating and tenure (Typically 6% to 10% annually).
- **Tenure**: Short-term (1-3 years), Medium-term (4-10 years), Long-term (10-40 years).
- **Liquidity**: Moderate to High (Can be traded in the secondary market).
- **Taxation**: Interest is generally taxed as per your income slab, except for specific Tax-Free Bonds.

## Definition
A bond is essentially an I.O.U. between a lender and borrower that includes the details of the loan and its payments. Bonds are issued by organizations generally for a period of more than one year to raise money by borrowing. The face value (principal) is returned to the investor on the maturity date, while interest is paid at regular intervals (semi-annually or annually). Key terminologies include:
- **Face Value (Par Value)**: The principal amount of the bond that will be paid back at maturity.
- **Coupon Rate**: The interest rate the bond issuer will pay on the face value.
- **Maturity Date**: The exact date when the issuer must repay the principal amount.
- **Yield to Maturity (YTM)**: The total return anticipated on a bond if it is held until the end of its lifetime.

---

## Why It Matters
For investors, bonds offer a predictable income stream and are considered a crucial element of a diversified investment portfolio. They act as a shock absorber during equity market volatility, preserving capital while generating steady returns. For retirees or those seeking regular income, bonds provide semi-annual or annual payouts that can fund living expenses. From a macroeconomic perspective, bonds allow governments to build infrastructure without immediately raising taxes and enable corporations to expand operations, driving economic growth.

## How It Works

```text
+-------------------+        1. Investor buys Bond         +-------------------+
|                   | -----------------------------------> |                   |
|                   |        (Pays Principal Amount)       |                   |
|     INVESTOR      |                                      |    BOND ISSUER    |
| (Lender of Money) | <----------------------------------- | (Govt/Corporation)|
|                   |        2. Issuer pays regular        |                   |
|                   |        Interest (Coupon) to Investor |                   |
+-------------------+                                      +-------------------+
          |                                                          |
          |                  3. At Maturity Date                     |
          |<---------------------------------------------------------|
          |       Issuer returns Face Value (Principal) to Investor  |
```

**Process Flow:**
1. **Issuance**: Government or Corporate entity issues a bond to raise capital.
2. **Subscription**: Investors purchase the bonds at Face Value during the primary issue.
3. **Holding Period**: The investor receives periodic interest (coupon) payments (e.g., 7% annually).
4. **Secondary Market (Optional)**: If the investor needs money before maturity, they can sell the bond on the stock exchange to another investor. The price depends on prevailing interest rates.
5. **Maturity**: On the maturity date, the current holder of the bond receives the final interest payment and the original Face Value.

## Eligibility
- **Resident Individuals**: Any Indian citizen over the age of 18 can invest in bonds. Minors can invest through a legal guardian.
- **Non-Resident Indians (NRIs)**: NRIs are eligible to invest in most government and corporate bonds subject to FEMA regulations. Certain specific bonds may have restrictions for NRIs or Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs).
- **Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs)**: Eligible to invest in their name.
- **Corporate Entities, Trusts, and Societies**: Institutional investors make up a large portion of the bond market.
- **Demat Account**: To hold bonds electronically (especially for corporate bonds and secondary market G-Secs), a functioning Demat account and a linked trading account are mandatory.
- **Not Applicable**: Foreign nationals without a registered Indian footprint/FPI status cannot directly participate in domestic retail bonds.

## Required Documents
To invest in bonds directly or via a brokerage account, the following documents are essential:
1. **PAN Card**: Mandatory for all financial transactions and investments in India.
2. **Aadhaar Card**: Required for KYC (Know Your Customer) compliance.
3. **Active Demat and Trading Account**: Statements/details required to receive the bond units.
4. **Bank Account Proof**: Cancelled cheque or bank statement to link for coupon (interest) and principal payouts (mandates NEFT/RTGS details).
5. **Address Proof**: Voter ID, Passport, or Utility Bills (if Aadhaar is not used as address proof).
6. **FATCA/CRS Declaration**: Mandatory for NRIs and citizens having tax residency in other countries.

## Features & Benefits
1. **Capital Preservation and Safety**: Government Securities (G-Secs) are backed by the sovereign guarantee of the Government of India, making them practically default-free. High-rated corporate bonds (AAA) also offer a very high degree of safety for the principal amount compared to equities.
2. **Regular Income Stream**: Bonds provide a fixed, predictable source of income through coupon payments. This is highly beneficial for retirees or conservative investors who depend on periodic cash flows to manage their living expenses, outperforming standard savings accounts.
3. **Portfolio Diversification**: Bonds have a historically low correlation with the stock market. When equity markets crash, bond prices often remain stable or even rise as investors seek safe havens. This diversification reduces the overall volatility of an investment portfolio.
4. **Tax Efficiency**: Certain instruments, like Public Sector Undertaking (PSU) Tax-Free Bonds (e.g., NHAI, REC, PFC bonds), offer interest income that is completely exempt from Income Tax under Section 10(15)(iv)(h). This dramatically improves the post-tax yield for investors in the 30% tax bracket.
5. **Liquidity through Secondary Market**: Unlike Fixed Deposits that charge a penalty for premature withdrawal, many bonds are listed on stock exchanges (BSE/NSE). Investors can sell their bonds at the prevailing market price if they urgently need liquidity, though they are subject to market risks.

---

## Risks
- **Interest Rate Risk**: This is the risk that bond prices will fall if market interest rates rise. Bond prices and interest rates have an inverse relationship. If you buy a bond paying 6% and market rates rise to 8%, new bonds are more attractive, causing the price of your 6% bond to drop in the secondary market.
- **Credit (Default) Risk**: The risk that the corporate issuer may go bankrupt and fail to pay the interest or return the principal. This is why checking credit ratings (AAA, AA, A, BBB) from agencies like CRISIL, ICRA, or CARE is critical. Government bonds have zero credit risk.
- **Inflation Risk**: Also known as purchasing power risk. If the inflation rate is higher than the bond's coupon rate, the real return on investment becomes negative. For instance, a 6% bond return is effectively losing value if inflation is at 7%.
- **Liquidity Risk**: While bonds are listed on exchanges, the secondary market for corporate bonds in India is not as deep or active as the equity market. You may not find buyers easily if you want to sell a low-rated bond urgently, forcing you to sell at a steep discount.
- **Call Risk**: Some bonds are "callable," meaning the issuer can repay the principal early if interest rates fall, forcing the investor to reinvest the money at the new, lower interest rates.

## Charges & Fees
- **Brokerage Charges**: If buying through a stockbroker in the secondary market, a standard equity brokerage fee applies (often Rs. 10 to Rs. 20 per executed order, depending on the discount broker).
- **Demat AMC (Annual Maintenance Charges)**: Rs. 300 to Rs. 800 per year charged by the Depository Participant (DP) for holding securities, including bonds.
- **RBI Retail Direct Scheme**: Opening and maintaining an RDG (Retail Direct Gilt) account directly with the RBI incurs absolutely zero fees or maintenance charges.
- **Taxes on Transactions**: GST at 18% is applicable on the brokerage fee. Securities Transaction Tax (STT) is currently generally exempt for debt segment trades on exchanges.
- **Stamp Duty**: A minuscule stamp duty (typically 0.0001%) is applicable on the purchase of bonds on the exchange.

---

## RBI / Government Rules
- **RBI Retail Direct Scheme (2021)**: The Reserve Bank of India launched this scheme to allow retail investors to open Retail Direct Gilt (RDG) accounts directly with the RBI to buy and sell Government Securities (Treasury Bills, Dated G-Secs, State Development Loans, and SGBs) without any intermediaries.
- **SEBI Regulations for Corporate Bonds**: The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) mandates that all public issues of non-convertible debentures (NCDs) must be credit-rated and listed on recognized stock exchanges. Issuers must create a Debenture Redemption Reserve (DRR) to protect investors.
- **Taxation (Income Tax Act, 1961)**:
  - **Interest Income**: Taxed as per the investor's marginal income tax slab under the head "Income from Other Sources".
  - **TDS (Tax Deducted at Source)**: Under Section 193, TDS at 10% is applicable on corporate bonds if the interest exceeds Rs. 5,000 in a year. No TDS on G-Secs.
  - **Capital Gains**: If bonds are sold before maturity, Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) apply (taxed at slab rate if held < 12 months for listed, < 36 months for unlisted). Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) tax rules are subject to the latest Finance Act provisions (historically 10% without indexation for listed bonds, recently adjusted; always check latest union budget rules).

## Step-by-Step Process

### Process 1: Buying Government Bonds via RBI Retail Direct
1. **Registration**: Visit the RBI Retail Direct portal (rbiretaildirect.org.in).
2. **Application**: Fill out the online form using PAN, Aadhaar, email, and mobile number.
3. **Verification**: Authenticate via OTP and complete the Video KYC process.
4. **Bank Linking**: Link your primary bank account for funding and receiving maturity/interest payouts.
5. **Bidding/Buying**: Once the RDG account is active, log in and view the Primary Market (NDS-OM) auctions. Select the G-Sec, T-Bill, or SDL you wish to buy.
6. **Payment**: Transfer funds using UPI or Net Banking. On allotment, bonds reflect in your RDG account.

### Process 2: Buying Corporate Bonds via Demat Account
1. **Login to Broker**: Open your stockbroker app (e.g., Zerodha, Upstox, ICICI Direct).
2. **Navigate to Bonds/NCDs**: Go to the specific section for primary bond issues (IPOs of NCDs) or search for the bond's ticker in the secondary market.
3. **Evaluate**: Check the credit rating, coupon rate, YTM, and maturity date.
4. **Place Order**: Enter the number of units you wish to purchase and place a buy order.
5. **Settlement**: Funds are deducted from your trading account, and bonds are credited to your Demat account in T+1 or T+2 days.

## Safety Tips
1. **Always Check Credit Ratings**: Only invest in AAA or AA-rated corporate bonds. Avoid BBB or lower ("junk" bonds) unless you deeply understand the risk, as they have a high probability of default.
2. **Diversify Issuers**: Do not put all your fixed-income capital into a single company's bond. Spread it across PSUs, Government Securities, and top-tier private corporates.
3. **Beware of Phishing Scams**: Only use official platforms (like RBI Retail Direct or SEBI-registered brokers) to buy bonds. Never transfer money to an unknown individual promising guaranteed 15-20% bond returns.
4. **Understand YTM vs Coupon**: Don't just look at the coupon rate. If buying from the secondary market, look at the Yield To Maturity (YTM), which tells you your actual annualized return based on the price you paid.
5. **Secure Your Demat**: Ensure two-factor authentication (2FA/TOTP) is active on your broker's platform to prevent unauthorized trading of your bond holdings.

## Common Mistakes
1. **Ignoring Interest Rate Dynamics**: Many retail investors do not realize that if they buy a 10-year bond and interest rates rise drastically next year, the market value of their bond will fall. Selling at this point results in capital loss.
2. **Chasing Unrealistic Yields**: Investing in low-rated corporate bonds or cooperative society deposits simply because they offer 2-3% higher interest than the market average. This often results in a total loss of principal if the company defaults.
3. **Misunderstanding Tax Implications**: Assuming all bond interest is tax-free. Only specific "Tax-Free Bonds" are exempt. Regular corporate bond interest is added to your income, which can severely reduce real returns if you are in the 30% slab.
4. **Ignoring Liquidity Needs**: Investing emergency funds into 10-year illiquid corporate bonds. When an emergency strikes, selling these in the secondary market can result in huge discount losses due to low trading volumes.
5. **Confusing NCDs with Bank FDs**: Assuming corporate bonds (NCDs) have DICGC insurance like bank FDs (up to Rs. 5 Lakhs). Bonds have NO such insurance; if the company defaults, you lose the money.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is the difference between a bond and a stock?**
A stock represents ownership (equity) in a company, while a bond represents a loan (debt) you give to the issuer. Bondholders get fixed interest and priority in liquidation, whereas stockholders get dividends and capital appreciation but take on higher risk.

2. **Can I lose my principal amount in bonds?**
Yes, if you invest in corporate bonds and the issuing company defaults or goes bankrupt, you can lose your principal. Government bonds (G-Secs), however, carry practically zero risk of default.

3. **What does Yield to Maturity (YTM) mean?**
YTM is the total anticipated return on a bond if it is held until maturity. It accounts for the coupon rate, the price paid for the bond in the secondary market, and the time remaining until maturity.

4. **Are government bonds completely tax-free?**
No, the interest earned on standard government bonds (G-Secs) is fully taxable as per your income tax slab. Only specific bonds designated as "Tax-Free Bonds" (like NHAI/REC bonds) offer tax-free interest.

5. **How frequently is interest paid on bonds?**
Interest (coupon) payment frequency varies by the bond's terms. Most government and corporate bonds in India pay interest on a semi-annual (twice a year) or annual basis.

6. **Can I sell a bond before its maturity date?**
Yes, if the bond is listed on a stock exchange and held in your Demat account, you can sell it in the secondary market. However, the price you get will depend on current interest rates and market demand.

7. **What is a Zero-Coupon Bond?**
A zero-coupon bond does not pay periodic interest. Instead, it is issued at a deep discount to its face value and redeemed at full face value upon maturity. The difference is the investor's profit.

8. **What are Tax-Free Bonds?**
Tax-free bonds are issued by government-backed entities like NHAI, PFC, REC, IRFC, HUDCO. The interest income generated from these bonds is entirely exempt from income tax under Section 10(15)(iv)(h) of the Income Tax Act.

9. **What happens if interest rates in the economy go up?**
Bond prices have an inverse relationship with interest rates. If the RBI increases the repo rate, the prices of existing bonds in the secondary market will fall, because new bonds will be issued at the higher interest rates.

10. **Is TDS applicable on bond interest?**
For corporate bonds, TDS is deducted at 10% under Section 193 if the interest paid exceeds Rs. 5,000 in a financial year. However, no TDS is deducted on Government Securities (though the income is still taxable).

11. **What is the RBI Retail Direct Scheme?**
It is a platform provided by the Reserve Bank of India that allows individual retail investors to open a Retail Direct Gilt (RDG) account and directly buy/sell Government securities without any intermediary fees.

12. **Can Non-Resident Indians (NRIs) invest in Indian bonds?**
Yes, NRIs can invest in most government and corporate bonds on a repatriable or non-repatriable basis, subject to FEMA guidelines. They require NRE or NRO accounts and appropriate KYC/FATCA declarations.

13. **What is a Debenture? Is it different from a Bond?**
In India, the terms are often used interchangeably. Technically, a bond is usually issued by the government or public entities, and debentures by private corporations. Debentures can be secured (backed by assets) or unsecured.

14. **How do credit ratings affect bonds?**
Credit ratings (like AAA, AA, BBB) indicate the financial health of the issuer. A 'AAA' rating means the highest safety and lowest default risk, meaning they offer slightly lower interest rates. Lower ratings offer higher interest but carry higher default risk.

15. **What is a Callable Bond?**
A callable bond includes a clause that allows the issuer to redeem (pay back) the bond before its maturity date. Issuers usually do this when market interest rates fall, allowing them to reissue new debt at a lower cost.

16. **Why do bond prices fluctuate in the secondary market?**
Bond prices change daily based on prevailing market interest rates, the issuer's changing credit rating, macroeconomic indicators, and overall supply and demand on the exchange.

17. **Can I pledge bonds to get a loan?**
Yes, bonds held in Demat form, especially Government Securities and highly rated corporate bonds, are considered excellent collateral. Banks and NBFCs readily provide Loans Against Securities (LAS) using bonds as a pledge.

18. **Are bonds covered by DICGC deposit insurance?**
No. DICGC insurance (up to Rs. 5 Lakhs) strictly applies to bank deposits (FDs, RDs, Savings). Bonds and Non-Convertible Debentures (NCDs) are market-linked debt instruments and have no DICGC insurance.

19. **What is a Sovereign Gold Bond (SGB)?**
SGBs are government securities denominated in grams of gold. They are issued by the RBI on behalf of the Government of India. They offer capital appreciation linked to gold prices plus a fixed interest of 2.5% per annum.

20. **Should a young investor buy bonds?**
Yes, even aggressive young investors should have a portion of their portfolio in bonds (e.g., 10-20%) for diversification, stability during equity market crashes, and to maintain an emergency liquid reserve.

## Common Myths vs Facts

1. **Myth**: Bonds are completely risk-free investments.
   **Fact**: While G-Secs have negligible default risk, corporate bonds carry credit risk. All bonds carry interest rate risk and inflation risk.

2. **Myth**: Bond interest and FD interest are taxed exactly the same way.
   **Fact**: While both are taxed at slab rates, corporate bonds have different TDS thresholds, and specific Tax-Free PSU bonds are completely exempt from tax.

3. **Myth**: You cannot access your money in a bond until maturity.
   **Fact**: Most bonds are listed on stock exchanges. You can sell them in the secondary market at any time, subject to finding a buyer and prevailing market prices.

4. **Myth**: Only rich institutional investors can buy government bonds.
   **Fact**: Through the RBI Retail Direct Scheme, any retail investor can buy government bonds for as little as Rs. 10,000.

5. **Myth**: High Yield (junk) bonds are the best way to earn money.
   **Fact**: High yield reflects high risk. The company may default, resulting in a 100% loss of your principal amount. Safety of principal should precede high returns.

6. **Myth**: Falling interest rates are bad for bondholders.
   **Fact**: Falling interest rates are actually *good* for existing bondholders because the price of their higher-yielding bonds will increase in the secondary market.

7. **Myth**: Bonds guarantee that you will beat inflation.
   **Fact**: Bonds offer fixed returns. If inflation spikes heavily (e.g., to 8%) and your bond pays 6%, you are experiencing a negative real return.

8. **Myth**: NRIs cannot invest in Indian Tax-Free bonds.
   **Fact**: NRIs are allowed to invest in many Indian Tax-Free bonds through their NRE/NRO accounts, though they must check the specific prospectus of the issue.

9. **Myth**: A stock broker is strictly required to buy any bond.
   **Fact**: You do not need a broker for G-Secs if you use the RBI Retail Direct portal. You also don't need a broker for Sovereign Gold Bonds if bought via net banking.

10. **Myth**: Callable bonds are better because they offer higher interest.
    **Fact**: Callable bonds offer higher interest to compensate you for the risk that the issuer might return your money early when interest rates drop, leaving you with lower reinvestment options.

## Conversation Examples

**Dialogue 1: Understanding Basics**
*User*: FDs aur Bonds mein kya farq hai?
*Agent*: Sir, FD aap bank mein open karte hain aur bank DICGC se 5 lakh tak insured hota hai. Bonds mein aap apna paisa sarkar ya private company ko directly loan par dete hain. Bonds stock market mein trade kiye ja sakte hain, aur Government bonds (G-Secs) mein default ka risk zero hota hai, jabki FDs ke premature withdrawal par penalty lagti hai.

**Dialogue 2: Safe Investments**
*User*: I want an investment that is 100% safe but better than an FD.
*Agent*: You can consider Government Securities (G-Secs) through the RBI Retail Direct portal. Because they are issued by the Government of India, they carry sovereign guarantees—meaning zero default risk. Their yields are often slightly better than top bank FDs, and you can hold them in a zero-fee RDG account.

**Dialogue 3: Taxation on Bonds**
*User*: Kya bonds se milne wala interest tax-free hota hai?
*Agent*: Aam taur par nahi, sir. Corporate bonds aur regular G-Secs ka interest aapke income tax slab ke anusaar taxable hota hai. Lekin, agar aap NHAI, REC ya PFC ke "Tax-Free Bonds" secondary market se kharidte hain, toh unka interest income tax se poori tarah mukt (exempt) hota hai.

**Dialogue 4: Premature Withdrawal**
*User*: Can I get my money back from a 10-year bond after 2 years?
*Agent*: Yes, you can. Unlike FDs, bonds are held in your Demat account and listed on the stock exchange. You can sell your bond in the secondary market to another investor. However, the selling price will depend on current market interest rates. If rates have gone up, you might have to sell at a discount.

**Dialogue 5: Credit Ratings**
*User*: Ye AAA aur BBB rating kya hoti hai bonds mein?
*Agent*: Sir, credit rating agencies (jaise CRISIL ya ICRA) company ki financial health check karti hain. AAA sabse safe rating hai jiska matlab company aapka paisa aasaani se wapas kar degi. BBB ya usse neeche ki rating high risk maani jaati hai, jisme default (paisa doobne) ka khatra zyada hota hai. Hamesha AAA ya AA rated bonds hi chunein.

**Dialogue 6: Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs)**
*User*: Is SGB better than physical gold?
*Agent*: Absolutely. Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) track the market price of gold exactly like physical gold, but without making charges or purity concerns. Additionally, the RBI pays you a 2.5% fixed interest every year on your investment value, and if you hold it till maturity (8 years), the capital gains are completely tax-free!

**Dialogue 7: TDS on Corporate Bonds**
*User*: Mujhe ek company ka bond lena hai, kya usme TDS katega?
*Agent*: Ji sir, agar aap corporate bond (NCD) lete hain, aur aapka saalana interest Rs. 5,000 se zyada hota hai, toh company Section 193 ke tehat 10% TDS deduct karegi. Aap apna tax bachaane ke liye ITR file karte waqt ye TDS claim kar sakte hain. Note: G-Secs par koi TDS nahi lagta.

**Dialogue 8: RBI Retail Direct**
*User*: How do I buy government bonds without paying broker commissions?
*Agent*: You can use the RBI Retail Direct Scheme. You just need to visit rbiretaildirect.org.in, register using your PAN, Aadhaar, and a savings bank account, and complete the Video KYC. Once opened, you can bid directly in primary government bond auctions with zero commission or account maintenance fees.

**Dialogue 9: Interest Rate Risk**
*User*: RBI is raising interest rates. How does this affect my existing bonds?
*Agent*: When RBI raises interest rates, the prices of existing bonds in the secondary market fall. This happens because newer bonds will be issued at the higher, more attractive rates. If you hold your bond until maturity, you won't lose money, but if you sell it now, you might sell at a loss.

**Dialogue 10: Junk Bonds**
*User*: A company is offering 14% interest on their bond while banks give 7%. Should I invest?
*Agent*: I would advise extreme caution. A 14% interest rate usually indicates a very low credit rating (junk bond). The company is forced to offer high rates because they have a high risk of going bankrupt. If they default, you could lose your entire principal amount. Please check the CRISIL/ICRA rating first.

**Dialogue 11: NRI Investment**
*User*: I am an NRI based in Dubai. Can I invest in Indian government bonds?
*Agent*: Yes, NRIs can invest in Indian Government Securities and corporate bonds. You can route your investments through your NRE or NRO account. The RBI Fully Accessible Route (FAR) allows non-residents to invest in specific government bonds without any upper limit.

**Dialogue 12: Zero Coupon Bonds**
*User*: Ye Zero Coupon bond me return kaise milta hai jab interest nahi hota?
*Agent*: Zero Coupon Bonds aapko discount par milte hain. Maan lijiye bond ki Face Value ₹1000 hai, par aaj wo aapko ₹700 mein milega. Beech ke saalo mein koi interest (coupon) nahi milega, lekin maturity date par aapko poore ₹1000 wapas milenge. Ye ₹300 ka difference hi aapka profit hota hai.

**Dialogue 13: Demat Account Requirement**
*User*: Do I strictly need a Demat account for bonds?
*Agent*: Yes, to hold corporate bonds or to trade any bond in the secondary market, a Demat account is mandatory. However, for Sovereign Gold Bonds and G-Secs bought via RBI Retail Direct, they can be held in an RBI RDG account or a Certificate of Holding without a traditional broker Demat.

**Dialogue 14: Regular Income for Retirees**
*User*: I am retiring next month. Are bonds a good way to get a monthly pension?
*Agent*: Bonds are excellent for retirees, but they usually pay interest semi-annually (every 6 months) or annually, not monthly. To simulate a monthly income, you can build a "Bond Ladder"—buying multiple bonds that have coupon payment dates spread across different months of the year.

**Dialogue 15: Tax-Free Bonds Liquidity**
*User*: Can I buy NHAI tax-free bonds right now?
*Agent*: The government has not issued new tax-free bonds in the primary market for several years. However, you can still buy previously issued NHAI tax-free bonds from the secondary market via your stock broker. Just be aware that their prices might be trading at a premium, which lowers your effective Yield to Maturity.
