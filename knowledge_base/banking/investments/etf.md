# Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs)

## Overview

**English:** An Exchange Traded Fund (ETF) is an investment fund traded on stock exchanges, much like individual stocks. It holds assets such as stocks, commodities, or bonds and generally operates with an arbitrage mechanism designed to keep it trading close to its net asset value, although deviations can occasionally occur. ETFs offer the diversification of a mutual fund with the real-time liquidity and trading flexibility of a stock.

## Quick Summary
- **Traded Like Stocks:** Bought and sold on standard stock exchanges (NSE/BSE) during market hours.
- **Underlying Assets:** Can track equity indices (like Nifty 50), sectors, commodities (like Gold, Silver), or debt instruments.

- **Real-time Pricing:** Unlike Mutual Funds which allocate NAV at the end of the day, ETF prices fluctuate throughout the trading day.
- **Demat Mandatory:** You absolutely need a Demat and Trading account to invest in ETFs.

## Definition
An Exchange Traded Fund (ETF) is a pooled investment vehicle that passively tracks an underlying index, commodity, or basket of assets. Structurally, it is created by an Asset Management Company (AMC) which issues shares of the fund in exchange for the underlying securities (a process handled by Authorized Participants or APs). Retail investors then buy and sell these ETF shares on the secondary market (stock exchanges) at the prevailing market price, which is closely aligned with the Net Asset Value (NAV) of the underlying assets.

---

## Why It Matters
ETFs represent a democratization of asset management. They allow retail investors to buy a fully diversified portfolio or a specific asset class (like Gold) in a single transaction, avoiding the hassle of managing 50 different stocks or physically storing gold. Because they are passively managed, they remove the "fund manager risk" and significantly reduce management fees, allowing long-term compounding to work more efficiently for the investor without a heavy fee drag.

## How It Works

```text
+---------------------+         +------------------------+         +-----------------------+
|  Asset Management   |         | Authorized Participant |         |   Stock Exchanges     |
|   Company (AMC)     | <-----> |   (Large Institutions) | <-----> |      (NSE / BSE)      |
|  (Creates the ETF)  |         | (Create/Redeem Units)  |         | (Secondary Market)    |
+---------------------+         +------------------------+         +-----------------------+
                                                                             ^
                                                                             |
                                                                             v
                                                                   +-----------------------+
                                                                   |   Retail Investors    |
                                                                   |  (Buy/Sell via Demat) |
                                                                   +-----------------------+
```

1. **Creation:** AMC decides to launch an ETF tracking Nifty 50.
2. **Basket Collection:** Authorized Participants (APs) buy all 50 stocks in the exact proportion of the index and hand them to the AMC.
3. **Unit Issuance:** AMC gives the AP large blocks of ETF shares (Creation Units) in return.
4. **Market Trading:** APs list these ETF units on the NSE/BSE. Retail investors buy/sell them through their brokers.
5. **Arbitrage:** If ETF price strays from the actual NAV, APs step in to buy/sell the underlying assets and ETF units, bridging the gap and keeping prices aligned.

## Eligibility
To invest in an ETF in India, an individual or entity must meet the following criteria:
- **Age limit:** Must be 18 years or older for an independent account. Minors can invest via a natural/court-appointed guardian operating a minor Demat account.
- **Demat & Trading Account:** Must hold a valid, active Demat and Trading account with a SEBI-registered Depository Participant (DP) and stockbroker.
- **KYC Compliance:** Full KYC verification (KRA compliant) is strictly mandatory, including Aadhaar and PAN linkage.
- **Bank Account:** A valid Indian bank account linked to the trading account for fund transfers.
- **Residency:** Resident Indians, NRIs (subject to FEMA/RBI guidelines via NRE/NRO accounts), HUFs, and Corporate entities are eligible.

## Features & Benefits

### 1. Real-Time Trading and Liquidity
Unlike mutual funds where transactions happen at an end-of-day NAV, ETFs can be bought and sold continuously throughout the trading day. This provides investors with the flexibility to react to intraday market news, enter at specific price points, and set limit orders or stop-loss orders just like individual stocks. High liquidity in popular ETFs means entering and exiting positions is seamless.

### 2. Extremely Low Expense Ratios
ETFs are inherently passively managed. They simply mirror an index without requiring expensive fund management teams to research and pick stocks. Because of this, the Total Expense Ratio (TER) of ETFs can be as low as 0.05% per annum, compared to 1% to 2% for active mutual funds. Over a 10 to 20-year horizon, this massive savings in fees dramatically boosts the compounding effect of the investor's portfolio.

### 3. Complete Portfolio Transparency
Mutual funds disclose their full portfolios on a monthly basis, meaning investors don't know exactly what the fund holds day-to-day. ETFs, however, track transparent indices (like Nifty 50 or Bank Nifty) or asset classes whose constituents are public knowledge. Therefore, investors know precisely what underlying assets they hold in real-time, preventing unexpected portfolio drift or hidden risks.

### 4. Efficient Diversification
A single ETF unit can provide exposure to an entire basket of securities. For instance, buying one unit of a Nifty 50 ETF instantly gives the investor proportional ownership in the top 50 companies in India. This reduces the unsystematic risk associated with individual stock picking. There are ETFs for diverse assets, including equity, debt (e.g., Bharat Bond ETF), and commodities (e.g., Gold BeES).

### 5. Absence of Minimum Investment Constraints
While mutual funds often have a minimum lumpsum requirement of ₹500 or ₹1,000, the minimum investment for an ETF is simply the price of one single unit. For some ETFs, this can be as low as ₹10 to ₹50. This fractional-style exposure makes it exceptionally accessible for beginners and those looking to invest micro-savings dynamically into capital markets.

## Risks

### Financial Risk
The primary financial risk is Market Risk (Systematic Risk). Since the ETF mirrors an index, if the entire market or the specific sector index crashes, the ETF value will correspondingly plummet. Additionally, there is **Tracking Error Risk**—the difference between the ETF's performance and the index's performance. High tracking errors negate the very purpose of an index ETF. **Liquidity Risk** is also significant in niche or newly launched ETFs where low trading volumes can result in wider bid-ask spreads, forcing investors to buy at a premium or sell at a discount to NAV.

### Technical Risk
Since ETFs are traded on stock exchanges through brokers, they are susceptible to technical glitches. Broker downtimes, API failures, or exchange halts can prevent an investor from exiting a position during severe market volatility. Inaccurate ticking of the iNAV (Indicative Net Asset Value) due to data feed errors can mislead investors into placing incorrect limit orders.

### Legal & Regulatory Risk
Investments in ETFs are heavily regulated by SEBI and exchange authorities. Changes in taxation rules (like the recent adjustments to capital gains tax for debt mutual funds and ETFs), modifications in index constituent rules, or suspension of a specific ETF by the AMC due to regulatory non-compliance can impact the investor. Cross-border ETFs (like Nasdaq 100 ETFs) carry additional regulatory risks related to RBI's foreign investment limits.

### Cyber Security Risk
Since ETF investments require a digitized Demat and Trading ecosystem, cyber security is a critical risk factor. Phishing attacks, unauthorized access to the trading terminal, or SIM cloning can lead to unauthorized liquidation of ETF holdings. Attackers might sell the highly liquid ETFs to buy illiquid penny stocks in a coordinated pump-and-dump scheme, draining the investor's wealth without directly transferring money out of the bank.

## Charges & Fees

1. **Brokerage Charges:** Charged by the stockbroker for executing the buy/sell trade. Many discount brokers charge zero brokerage on delivery trades, but others may charge a flat fee (e.g., ₹20) or a percentage.
2. **Total Expense Ratio (TER):** Charged by the AMC for managing the ETF. Deducted directly from the NAV on a daily prorated basis. Typically ranges from 0.05% to 1.00%.
3. **Securities Transaction Tax (STT):** Applicable on equity ETFs at the time of buying and selling. Standard rates apply as per government budgets (e.g., 0.1% on delivery transactions).
4. **Demat AMC (Annual Maintenance Charge):** Charged by the Depository Participant (broker) for keeping the Demat account active, ranging from ₹0 to ₹1000 per year.
5. **DP (Depository Participant) Charges:** A flat fee (usually ₹13.5 to ₹20 + GST) charged by the depository (CDSL/NSDL) and the broker every time ETF units are *debited* (sold) from the Demat account.
6. **Exchange Transaction Charges:** Nominal fees charged by NSE or BSE for executing the trade (around 0.003% to 0.0035%).
7. **GST:** 18% GST applies on brokerage, transaction charges, and DP charges.
8. **Stamp Duty:** Charged by the state government only on the buy side of the transaction (e.g., 0.015%).

## RBI / Government Rules
*Note: Capital markets and ETFs are primarily regulated by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI), not the RBI (unless dealing with foreign/international ETFs where FEMA rules apply).*

- **Tracking Error Limits:** SEBI mandates that AMCs closely monitor and report the tracking error of passive funds. If the tracking error exceeds permissible limits (typically 2% on an annualized basis), the AMC must disclose this and take corrective actions.
- **Creation Unit Size:** SEBI regulates the minimum block size for direct transactions between the AMC and Authorized Participants, ensuring retail investors only trade on the secondary market, protecting them from large-scale liquidity crunches.
- **iNAV Disclosures:** SEBI requires AMCs to publish the intraday Indicative Net Asset Value (iNAV) of the ETF continuously during market hours so that investors can make informed trading decisions and avoid large bid-ask premiums.
- **T+1 Settlement:** In line with SEBI's mandate for Indian equity markets, ETF transactions follow a T+1 (Trade + 1 day) rolling settlement cycle. Units are credited to the Demat account one working day after the purchase.
- **Taxation Mandates:** Under the Finance Act, equity ETFs holding over 65% domestic equities are taxed under Equity Capital Gains (LTCG / STCG). Debt, Gold, and International ETFs are taxed as per specific rules (often at marginal income tax slabs depending on recent Finance Bill amendments).

## Step-by-Step Process

### How to Buy an ETF
1. **Open an Account:** Register with a SEBI-registered stockbroker and open a Trading + Demat account. Complete KYC.
2. **Add Funds:** Transfer money from your linked bank account to the trading ledger using UPI, NEFT, or Net Banking.
3. **Search for the ETF:** Log in to your broker's trading terminal or app. In the search bar, type the name or ticker of the ETF (e.g., "NIFTYBEES" for Nippon India Nifty 50 ETF, or "GOLDBEES" for Gold ETF).
4. **Analyze the Price:** Check the current market price and the market depth (bid-ask spread) to ensure liquidity.
5. **Place Order:** Select the "Buy" option. Choose "Delivery" (CNC) for long-term holding. Enter the quantity of units. Choose a "Limit" order to buy at a specific price, or "Market" order to buy immediately.
6. **Execution & Settlement:** Once executed, the funds are deducted. The ETF units will reflect in your Demat account after T+1 days.

### How to Sell an ETF
1. **Locate Holdings:** Go to the "Portfolio" or "Holdings" section of your trading app.
2. **Select ETF:** Click on the ETF you wish to sell. Ensure you have the requisite units free (not pledged or blocked).
3. **Place Sell Order:** Click "Sell". Enter the quantity. Use a Limit order for better price control.
4. **Authorize (CDSL/NSDL):** If you haven't submitted a Power of Attorney (POA) or DDPI, you must verify the sale using a CDSL TPIN and OTP.
5. **Execution:** Once the order matches a buyer, it gets executed.
6. **Fund Credit:** 80% of the funds are available for trading immediately, and 100% can be withdrawn to your bank account after T+1 settlement.

### Can I do SIP in ETFs?
Yes. Unlike mutual funds which have built-in AMC SIPs, ETF SIPs are executed at the broker level. You set an instruction on your trading app to buy a fixed quantity of ETF units or a fixed monetary amount on a specific date every month. The broker automatically fires market orders on that date, provided your trading account has sufficient funds.

## Safety Tips
- **Always Check the Spread:** Highly illiquid ETFs can have a large gap between the buying and selling price. Always use Limit Orders instead of Market Orders to avoid buying at a premium.
- **Secure Your Demat:** Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) / Biometrics on your trading application. Never share your broker password or CDSL TPIN with anyone.
- **Ignore "Assured Returns" Tips:** Beware of Telegram or WhatsApp groups promising guaranteed returns in specific niche ETFs. 
- **Verify Ticker Symbols:** Many ETFs have similar sounding names (e.g., NIFTYBEES vs JUNIORBEES). Always verify the underlying index before hitting buy.
- **Do Not Trade on Public Wi-Fi:** Avoid executing trades or logging into your trading account on open, unsecured networks.

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Using Market Orders for Illiquid ETFs
Many investors place a "Market Order" for an ETF with low trading volume. Because there aren't enough sellers at the actual NAV price, the order gets executed at a bizarrely high price set by a random seller, causing an immediate, massive notional loss. Always use Limit Orders.

### 2. Ignoring the Tracking Error
Beginners assume that if the Nifty goes up by 10%, their Nifty ETF will go up by exactly 10%. However, poor fund management, cash drag, and high expense ratios can cause the ETF to only go up by 9%. Ignoring tracking error leads to suboptimal long-term returns. Investors must compare the historical tracking errors of different AMCs.

### 3. Confusing ETF and Index Mutual Fund
While both track indices, an Index Mutual Fund doesn't need a Demat account and executes at end-of-day NAV. An ETF requires a Demat, has intraday pricing, and incurs DP/Brokerage charges. Paying high Demat AMC and brokerage just to do a monthly ₹500 SIP in an ETF is cost-inefficient compared to an Index Mutual Fund.

### 4. Over-Trading Due to Intraday Ease
Because ETFs trade like stocks and fluctuate second-by-second on the screen, long-term passive investors get tempted to time the market, buy dips, and sell peaks intraday. This leads to higher brokerage costs, STT, and capital gains taxes, destroying the compounding benefit.

### 5. Overlooking the Bid-Ask Spread vs iNAV
The Indicative NAV (iNAV) is the true value of the ETF's holdings. Sometimes, in a volatile market, the trading price of the ETF gets detached from the iNAV. Buying the ETF when it is trading at a significant premium to its iNAV means you are overpaying for the underlying assets.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

**1. What is the main difference between an ETF and a Mutual Fund?**
An ETF trades on a stock exchange like a regular stock, allowing intraday buying and selling at varying prices. A Mutual Fund is bought or sold directly with the AMC, and all transactions for the day happen at a single, end-of-day Net Asset Value (NAV). Additionally, ETFs mandate a Demat account, while Mutual Funds do not.

**2. Is a Demat account strictly compulsory for buying ETFs?**
Yes, it is absolutely mandatory. Because ETF units are traded on stock exchanges and settled in electronic depository formats, you cannot hold or trade an ETF without a valid Demat and Trading account linked to your PAN.

**3. What is Nifty BeES?**
Nifty BeES stands for Nifty Benchmark Exchange Traded Scheme. It was the first ETF launched in India (originally by Benchmark AMC, now Nippon India). It passively tracks the Nifty 50 index and is one of the most liquid ETFs available on the NSE.

**4. Are ETFs safer than individual stocks?**
Yes, generally. A single stock carries company-specific risks (unsystematic risk) and can theoretically go to zero if the company goes bankrupt. An ETF holds a basket of stocks (like 50 top companies), meaning the failure of one company has a minimal impact on the overall portfolio, offering excellent diversification.

**5. How is the price of an ETF determined?**
The price is primarily driven by the real-time Net Asset Value of its underlying basket of assets. However, because it trades on an exchange, demand and supply mechanics can cause slight premiums or discounts. Arbitrageurs (Authorized Participants) continuously work to align the market price with the actual NAV.

**6. Do ETFs pay out dividends?**
Most equity index ETFs in India (like Nifty 50 ETFs) generally reinvest the dividends received from the underlying companies back into the fund, which increases the NAV (Growth option). Very few ETFs pay out regular cash dividends to the investor's bank account.

**7. How are ETFs taxed in India?**
Equity ETFs (holding >65% domestic equity) are taxed like stocks: 12.5% for Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) above ₹1.25 Lakhs (holding > 1 year), and 20% for Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) for holding < 1 year (as per recent Union Budget updates). Debt, Gold, and International ETFs are added to your income and taxed at slab rates regardless of the holding period.

**8. Can I buy fractional shares of an ETF?**
No, in the Indian stock market, you cannot buy fractional shares of ETFs or stocks. You must buy in multiples of 1 whole unit. However, the price of a single unit is often kept low (e.g., ₹200 to ₹500) to ensure affordability.

**9. What happens if the AMC managing the ETF shuts down?**
Your investment is safe. The assets held by the ETF are kept separate from the AMC's own balance sheet, in custody of a SEBI-registered custodian. If the AMC shuts down, SEBI will transfer the management of the ETF to another robust AMC, or the assets will be liquidated and the current NAV will be paid out to unit holders.

**10. What is an Authorized Participant (AP)?**
An AP is usually a large financial institution or market maker appointed by the AMC. They have the exclusive right to create or redeem ETF units directly with the AMC in large blocks. Their main job is to provide liquidity and ensure the ETF market price stays extremely close to its true NAV.

**11. Can I pledge my ETFs for margin trading?**
Yes, most top-tier brokers allow you to pledge highly liquid ETFs (like Nifty BeES, Bank BeES, or Gold BeES) to get margin collateral. You can use this collateral margin to take intraday or positional trades in the F&O (Futures and Options) segment.

**12. What is Tracking Error?**
Tracking error measures how much an ETF's return deviates from the return of the underlying index it is supposed to track. Causes include the expense ratio, cash balances held for liquidity, and transaction costs incurred by the AMC while rebalancing the portfolio. A lower tracking error is always better.

**13. How do Gold ETFs work?**
Gold ETFs invest in physical gold of 99.5% purity. One unit usually represents 1 gram (or a fraction thereof) of physical gold. When you buy a Gold ETF, you get exposure to gold prices without worrying about making charges, secure storage, theft, or wealth tax associated with physical jewelry.

**14. Are there any DP charges when buying an ETF?**
No, Depository Participant (DP) charges are only levied when units are debited (sold or transferred out) from your Demat account. Buying ETFs only incurs brokerage, STT, and standard exchange charges, but no DP charge.

**15. What are Sectoral ETFs?**
Instead of tracking the broad market like Nifty 50, Sectoral ETFs track a specific industry index. Examples include Bank ETFs (tracking Bank Nifty), IT ETFs (tracking Nifty IT index), or Pharma ETFs. They carry higher risk than broad market ETFs because they lack inter-sector diversification.

**16. What is the minimum amount required to invest in an ETF?**
The minimum investment is exactly the market price of one single unit of that ETF plus applicable brokerage and taxes. There is no predefined minimum like ₹500 as seen in mutual funds.

**17. What is an iNAV and why is it important?**
iNAV stands for Indicative Net Asset Value. It is an intraday estimation of the ETF's NAV based on real-time prices of its underlying assets, updated every few seconds. Investors should check the iNAV to ensure they are not buying the ETF at an unjustified premium or selling at a deep discount on the exchange.

**18. Can NRIs invest in Indian ETFs?**
Yes, Non-Resident Indians (NRIs) can invest in Indian ETFs. They need to open a PIS (Portfolio Investment Scheme) or Non-PIS account linked with their NRE/NRO bank account, along with a Demat and Trading account tailored for NRIs. Strict FEMA guidelines apply.

**19. Why should I use a Limit Order for ETFs instead of a Market Order?**
Some ETFs have low trading volumes, leading to wide bid-ask spreads (the gap between highest buyer and lowest seller). A market order might execute at a much higher price than the actual NAV because it hits whatever the lowest seller is demanding. A limit order restricts the maximum price you are willing to pay, protecting you from sudden price slippages.

**20. What is a Smart Beta ETF?**
Unlike traditional ETFs that weight stocks based purely on market capitalization (size), Smart Beta ETFs use specific rules or metrics—like volatility, value, momentum, or dividend yield—to select and weight stocks. They aim to beat the market passively, sitting somewhere between active management and standard passive indexing.

## Common Myths vs Facts

**Myth 1:** ETFs are only for advanced traders and institutional investors.
**Fact:** ETFs are highly suitable for beginners as they provide instant diversification across top companies at a very low cost, requiring no stock-picking skills.

**Myth 2:** ETFs and Mutual Funds are exactly the same thing.
**Fact:** While both pool money, ETFs trade live on the stock exchange during market hours requiring a Demat account. Mutual Funds trade directly with the AMC at end-of-day NAV.

**Myth 3:** You can lose more than your investment in an ETF.
**Fact:** Like stocks, you can only lose up to the capital you have invested. Unless you are using margin funding or derivatives (which is a separate mechanism entirely), your ETF value cannot go negative.

**Myth 4:** ETFs are free to trade and manage.
**Fact:** Although cheaper than mutual funds, ETFs still charge an Expense Ratio. Furthermore, buying and selling them incurs brokerage fees, Demat AMC, DP charges, and STT.

**Myth 5:** The ETF price will exactly match the Index price.
**Fact:** The ETF mirrors the *percentage movement* of the index, not the absolute numerical value. If Nifty is at 25,000, the ETF unit might be priced at ₹250 (a fraction). If Nifty moves 1%, the ETF price will move roughly 1%.

**Myth 6:** All ETFs are highly liquid and easy to sell instantly.
**Fact:** While popular ones like Nifty 50 ETFs are highly liquid, many niche or newly launched sectoral ETFs suffer from severe illiquidity. Selling large volumes of illiquid ETFs can take time or force you to sell at a discount.

**Myth 7:** Gold ETFs are backed by paper contracts, not real gold.
**Fact:** SEBI mandates that Gold ETFs in India must be backed by physical gold of 99.5% purity stored in highly secure, audited vaults managed by custodians.

**Myth 8:** If the stock market crashes, the ETF manager will sell bad stocks to protect me.
**Fact:** Standard ETFs are purely passive. They blindly track the index. If a stock is in the index, the ETF must hold it, even if it is crashing. The manager makes zero active decisions to "protect" the portfolio.

**Myth 9:** You cannot do SIPs in ETFs.
**Fact:** You absolutely can. Most modern stockbrokers provide a "Stock SIP" or "ETF SIP" feature where they automatically buy a specified quantity of ETF units on a fixed date every month.

**Myth 10:** ETFs avoid Capital Gains Tax.
**Fact:** Selling an ETF at a profit triggers Capital Gains Tax exactly like selling individual stocks or mutual funds, complete with STCG and LTCG definitions based on the holding period.

**Agent:** iNAV yaani Indicative NAV, ETF ke underlying assets ki real-time value hoti hai. Exchange pe ETF ki price demand-supply ki wajah se iNAV se thodi upar (premium) ya niche (discount) ho sakti hai. Buy karne se pehle AMC ki website pe iNAV check karein, taaki aap overpay na kar rahe ho.

**Example 8: Tax Implications**
**User (English):** How much tax do I pay if I hold a Nifty ETF for 2 years?
**Agent:** Since a Nifty ETF is an equity-oriented instrument held for more than 1 year, Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) tax will apply. Currently, LTCG up to ₹1.25 Lakhs per financial year is tax-free. Any profit above ₹1.25 Lakhs is taxed at a flat rate of 12.5% (plus applicable cess).

**Example 9: Dividend Queries**
**User (Hindi):** Kya mujhe ETF se dividend ka paisa mere bank account me milega?
**Agent:** India mein zyadatar Equity ETFs apne dividends ko fund mein hi reinvest kar dete hain, jisse ETF ki NAV badh jati hai. Ise Growth option kehte hain. Agar aapko regular income chahiye, toh aapko un specific dividend-yielding ETFs ko dhundhna hoga jo cash payout karte hain, jo ki rare hain.

**Example 10: Demat Mandatory**
**User (Hinglish):** Mere paas Demat account nahi hai, bas regular bank account hai. Kya main ETF le sakta hu?
**Agent:** Nahi. ETF ko stock exchange pe trade kiya jata hai, isliye ise kharidne aur hold karne ke liye ek valid Demat aur Trading account ka hona bilkul anivarya (mandatory) hai. Agar aap bina Demat ke invest karna chahte hain, toh aap Index Mutual Funds dekh sakte hain.

**Example 11: International ETFs**
**User (English):** Can I invest in US tech stocks using Indian ETFs?
**Agent:** Yes, absolutely. AMCs in India offer ETFs that track international indices like the Nasdaq 100 or S&P 500 (e.g., Motilal Oswal Nasdaq 100 ETF). You can buy these directly on the NSE/BSE using your regular Indian Demat account in INR, getting exposure to global tech giants.

**Example 12: AMC Shutting Down**
**User (Hindi):** Agar woh company band ho gayi jisne ETF banaya hai, toh mere paise ka kya hoga?
**Agent:** Aapka paisa puri tarah surakshit hai. SEBI ke niyam anusar, AMC aur ETF ke assets alag rakhe jate hain (custodian ke paas). Agar AMC band hoti hai, toh SEBI us ETF ka management kisi aur company ko de degi, ya fir fund ko band karke aapko us din ki NAV ke hisaab se poora paisa waapas mil jayega.

**Example 13: Fractional Units**
**User (Hinglish):** Nifty ETF ka price ₹250 hai. Mere paas ₹100 hain, kya main adha ETF unit le sakta hu?
**Agent:** Nahi, Indian stock market mein fractional shares yaani hisso mein units kharidne ki anumati nahi hai. Aapko kam se kam 1 poora unit kharidna hoga. Isliye aapko invest karne ke liye kam se kam ₹250 aur brokerage hone tak intezaar karna padega.

**Example 14: Difference between Nifty and Bank Nifty ETF**
**User (English):** Should I buy Nifty ETF or Bank Nifty ETF? 
**Agent:** A Nifty ETF gives you broad exposure across 14 different sectors (IT, Pharma, Auto, Banking, etc.), making it highly diversified and less risky. A Bank Nifty ETF invests ONLY in the top banking stocks. It can offer higher returns during a banking boom but carries concentrated sectoral risk. For beginners, Nifty ETF is safer.

**Example 15: Settling Cycle**
**User (Hindi):** Maine aaj ETF becha hai, paise bank me kab aayenge?
**Agent:** India mein stock market T+1 settlement cycle follow karta hai. Jis din aap ETF bechte hain, 80% paisa turant trading account me dikhta hai jisse aap aur trade kar sakte hain. Par bank account me withdraw karne ke liye aapko agle working day (T+1) ka wait karna padega.

## Government Services & Integration
- **Bharat 22 ETF & CPSE ETF:** These are special ETFs launched by the Government of India as part of its disinvestment program. They track a basket of Central Public Sector Enterprises (CPSEs), Public Sector Banks, and some strategic holdings. Retail investors often get discounts during their New Fund Offers (NFOs).
- **DigiLocker Integration:** When opening a Demat account to trade ETFs, the KRA fetches Aadhaar and PAN verification directly through the government's DigiLocker infrastructure for instantaneous e-KYC.
- **CDSL/NSDL e-DIS:** Depositories managed under SEBI regulations mandate electronic Delivery Instruction Slips (e-DIS) authorized via TPIN and OTP to ensure ETF units are not fraudulently sold by brokers without the investor's express consent.
