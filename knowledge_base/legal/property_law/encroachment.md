# Property Encroachment (संपत्ति अतिक्रमण / Zameen Par Kabza)

## Overview

**English:**
Property encroachment occurs when someone illegally expands their physical boundaries onto the property of another person, or unlawfully occupies a vacant property without the owner's permission. In India, this is a very common dispute, especially concerning agricultural lands, open plots, and shared boundaries in urban housing. Encroachment can be intentional or accidental (due to faulty measurements). The law provides both civil remedies (for eviction and damages) and criminal remedies (for trespass and criminal intimidation).

## Definition

**English:**
Encroachment is defined as an unauthorized physical intrusion onto another person's real property. This intrusion could be structural (building a wall, extending a roof) or purely possessory (fencing someone else's land, parking vehicles permanently). Unlike adverse possession, which is a legal doctrine allowing a trespasser to gain title after 12 years of hostile possession, encroachment is the physical act of intrusion itself, which gives the rightful owner an immediate cause of action.

## Why It Matters

**English:**
Ignoring encroachment can lead to a permanent loss of property rights. Under the Limitation Act, if a squatter or encroacher remains in uninterrupted, open, and hostile possession of a private property for 12 years, they can claim legal ownership under the doctrine of "Adverse Possession." Furthermore, property value diminishes significantly if boundaries are disputed, making it impossible to sell or secure loans against the property.

## Legal Background

*   **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS):**
    *   **Section 329:** Defines Criminal Trespass. Whoever enters into or upon property in the possession of another with intent to commit an offence or to intimidate, insult or annoy any person in possession of such property.
    *   **Section 330:** Defines House-trespass.
    *   **Section 333:** Punishment for house-trespass.
*   **Specific Relief Act, 1963:**
    *   **Section 5:** Recovery of specific immovable property based on title.
    *   **Section 6:** Suit by person dispossessed of immovable property (can be filed within 6 months of dispossession without proving title, just prior possession).
    *   **Section 38:** Perpetual Injunction to prevent the breach of an obligation existing in favor of the plaintiff.
    *   **Section 39:** Mandatory Injunction to compel the performance of certain acts to prevent the breach of an obligation (e.g., order to demolish an illegally built wall).
*   **Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC):**
    *   **Order 39 Rule 1 & 2:** Deals with granting temporary injunctions (commonly known as Stay Orders) to stop further construction or alienation of the encroached property during the pendency of the suit.
*   **Limitation Act, 1963:**
    *   **Article 64:** Suit for possession based on previous possession (Limitation: 12 years from the date of dispossession).
    *   **Article 65:** Suit for possession based on title (Limitation: 12 years from the time the possession of the defendant becomes adverse to the plaintiff).
*   **Constitution of India:**
    *   **Article 300A:** Persons not to be deprived of property save by authority of law. (Constitutional Right to Property).

## Applicability
*   **Private Property:** Full applicability of civil and criminal laws. Owners must act within 12 years.
*   **Public/Government Property:** Adverse possession requires 30 years against the Government (Article 112 of Limitation Act). The Public Premises (Eviction of Unauthorised Occupants) Act applies for rapid eviction from government lands.
*   **Exemptions:** Genuine boundary disputes that are merely measurement errors are usually resolved via Revenue Court surveys rather than criminal trespass charges.

## Rights of Citizens

**English:**
1.  **Right to Self-Help:** The owner has a limited right to use reasonable force to eject a trespasser immediately upon entry (before the trespasser establishes settled possession).
2.  **Right to Injunction:** To obtain a court order stopping any further illegal construction on their land.
3.  **Right to Damages:** To claim financial compensation for any loss of use or physical damage to the property.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure

**English:**
1.  **Gather Evidence:** Collect title deeds (Sale Deed, Gift Deed), updated mutation extracts (Khata/7-12), recent property tax receipts, and time-stamped photographs/videos of the encroachment.
2.  **Revenue Survey (Optional but Recommended):** Apply to the Tehsildar or Inspector of Land Records for an official demarcation/survey of the land boundaries.
3.  **Police Complaint:** File a written complaint to the local SHO for Criminal Trespass (BNS Section 329). Obtain an acknowledgment (NCR or FIR).
4.  **Send a Legal Notice:** Hire an advocate to send a formal legal notice to the encroacher, demanding removal of the encroachment within a stipulated time (e.g., 15 days).
5.  **File a Civil Suit:** If the encroacher does not comply, file a civil suit in the court of appropriate jurisdiction seeking:
    *   Declaration of Title.
    *   Recovery of Possession (Section 5 Specific Relief Act).
    *   Mandatory Injunction to demolish illegal structures (Section 39).
    *   Application for Temporary Injunction (Order 39 Rule 1&2 CPC) to freeze the situation.

## Required Documents
*   **Original Title Deed:** Sale deed, Gift deed, or Will showing absolute ownership.
*   **Revenue Records:** Khata certificate, 7/12 extract, Patta, or Jamabandi.
*   **Tax Receipts:** Up-to-date property tax payment receipts.
*   **Survey Map:** Approved layout plan or official cadastral map (Bhu-naksha).
*   **Proof of Encroachment:** Clear, date-stamped photographs or CCTV footage.
*   **Identity Proof:** Aadhaar Card, PAN Card of the rightful owner.

## Government Authorities
*   **Civil Courts (Junior/Senior Division):** For filing civil suits for injunction and possession.
*   **Police Department:** For registering complaints against criminal trespass and breach of peace.
*   **Revenue Authorities (Tehsildar / Circle Officer / Patwari):** For official land measurement, demarcation, and mutation records.
*   **Municipal Corporations / Development Authorities:** If the encroachment violates building bylaws or is on public pathways.

## Penalties & Punishments
*   **Criminal Trespass (BNS Section 329):** Imprisonment up to 3 months, or fine up to ₹5,000, or both.
*   **House Trespass (BNS Section 330):** Imprisonment up to 1 year, or fine up to ₹5,000, or both.
*   **Contempt of Court:** If the encroacher violates a civil court's Stay Order, their property can be attached, and they may face civil imprisonment.

## Exceptions
*   **Adverse Possession:** If the encroachment has been open, continuous, and hostile to the true owner for more than 12 uninterrupted years (and the owner took no legal action), the encroacher can legally claim title.
*   **Easement Rights:** A neighbor crossing your land for 20+ years may acquire an "easement by prescription" (Right of Way), which is not an encroachment but a legal right of usage.
*   **Tenant Holding Over:** A tenant who refuses to vacate after the lease expires is not a trespasser in the traditional sense; they are a "tenant at sufferance," and standard rent control/eviction laws apply, not just trespass laws.

## Important Timelines
*   **Limitation to file Suit for Possession:** 12 years from the date the possession became adverse or date of dispossession.
*   **Specific Relief Act Sec 6:** A person dispossessed without due process can file a suit to recover possession merely on prior possession (without proving title) within 6 months.
*   **Notice Period:** Standard legal notice usually gives 15 to 30 days to the encroacher to vacate voluntarily.

## Common Legal Mistakes
*   **Delaying Action:** Waiting years to file a case out of fear or wanting to avoid court costs, allowing the clock on "Adverse Possession" to run out.
*   **Relying Only on Police:** Believing the police will evict an established squatter. Police handle crimes; they cannot adjudicate property title disputes—only civil courts can.
*   **Verbal Warnings Only:** Threatening the encroacher verbally but never sending a documented legal notice or filing a formal complaint.
*   **Using Illegal Force:** Hiring local goons to clear the land, which often results in the rightful owner being jailed for assault (BNS Sections for hurt/grievous hurt) and criminal intimidation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is the first thing I should do if my property is encroached?**
   Take photographs, gather your property documents, and immediately file a written complaint at the local police station regarding criminal trespass.
2. **Can the police evict an encroacher?**
   If the trespass is fresh and happening in real-time, police can intervene to stop a crime. However, if the person has settled possession, police will ask you to get a civil court order.
3. **What is a stay order?**
   A stay order (temporary injunction) is a court directive that stops the encroacher from doing any further construction or selling the disputed property while the case is ongoing.
4. **How much time does it take to get a stay order?**
   In urgent cases with strong documentary evidence, a civil court can grant an ex-parte stay order within 3 to 7 days of filing the suit.
5. **My neighbor built a wall on my land by mistake. What now?**
   You should apply for official demarcation via the Tehsildar. If the survey proves the wall is on your land, you can file a civil suit for mandatory injunction to demolish it.
6. **Can I demolish the neighbor's encroaching wall myself?**
   No. Taking the law into your own hands can lead to criminal charges of mischief and damage to property against you. You must obtain a court order.
7. **What happens if I ignore the encroachment for years?**
   If you ignore it for 12 continuous years, the encroacher can claim "Adverse Possession" and become the legal owner, extinguishing your rights.
8. **Does a legal notice stop the 12-year adverse possession clock?**
   A legal notice shows you object, but to legally stop the clock, you MUST file a civil suit for recovery of possession in court before the 12 years expire.
9. **How do I prove my ownership in court?**
   By submitting registered sale deeds, latest encumbrance certificates, updated revenue records (Khata), and property tax receipts.
10. **The encroacher claims they have forged documents. What to do?**
    You must file an FIR for forgery and cheating (BNS sections for cheating and forgery) alongside your civil suit for declaration of title and cancellation of the forged deed.
11. **Is encroachment a criminal offense or civil dispute?**
    It is both. Unlawful entry with intent to annoy/commit an offense is a crime (Criminal Trespass), but recovering the land and proving title is a civil matter.
12. **What if the encroachment is on agricultural land?**
    Revenue authorities (Tehsildar/SDM) have specific fast-track powers under state land revenue codes to measure agricultural land and sometimes order eviction without a long civil suit.
13. **Can I claim rent or damages from the encroacher?**
    Yes, in your civil suit, you can claim "mesne profits," which is compensation for the wrongful use and occupation of your property by the encroacher.
14. **How do I prevent encroachment on an empty plot?**
    Build a boundary wall, install a gate with a lock, put up a notice board, pay property taxes on time, and visit the site regularly.
15. **What if my relative refuses to vacate my house?**
    This is usually handled as a suit for eviction or mandatory injunction against a licensee, not pure encroachment, but the civil process to recover possession is similar.
16. **Can Lok Adalat resolve encroachment disputes?**
    Yes, if both parties agree to a compromise (e.g., the encroacher agrees to buy the encroached strip of land), the matter can be settled quickly in Lok Adalat.
17. **What is an ex-parte injunction?**
    It is an order passed by the judge in your favor without hearing the encroacher, granted only in highly urgent situations where waiting for a hearing would cause irreparable loss.
18. **Can I sell a property that has an ongoing encroachment dispute?**
    It is very difficult to find a buyer, and you are legally bound to disclose the dispute. Furthermore, the doctrine of *lis pendens* applies, meaning the buyer takes the property subject to the court's final decision.
19. **What is the cost of filing a civil suit for possession?**
    You have to pay ad-valorem court fees based on the market value of the property, which varies from state to state (usually between 2% to 7% of property value).
20. **Can I use CCTV footage as evidence?**
    Yes, CCTV footage clearly showing the act of trespassing/construction is admissible as electronic evidence under the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) with a Section 63 certificate.

## Common Myths vs Facts

1.  **Myth:** Police can bulldoze an illegal encroachment immediately.
    **Fact:** Police cannot demolish structures or decide property ownership. A civil court order or municipal action is required for demolition.
2.  **Myth:** If someone lives on my land for 5 years, it becomes theirs.
    **Fact:** The legal period for adverse possession of private property is 12 years of continuous, uninterrupted, and hostile possession.
3.  **Myth:** I don't need a boundary wall because I have the registry papers.
    **Fact:** Registry papers prove title, but a physical boundary prevents physical encroachment. Prevention is cheaper than litigation.
4.  **Myth:** Criminal trespass charges will automatically return my land to me.
    **Fact:** Criminal courts can punish the trespasser (jail/fine) but usually do not execute eviction or hand over possession; you need a civil court decree for that.
5.  **Myth:** Government survey (demarcation) is the final word on ownership.
    **Fact:** A survey only establishes boundaries on the ground based on revenue maps. Title (ownership) disputes must still be decided by civil courts.
6.  **Myth:** I can cut off the electricity and water of the encroacher to force them out.
    **Fact:** Courts frown upon self-help evictions that violate basic human rights. The encroacher might file a case against you.
7.  **Myth:** Sending a police complaint is enough to stop the 12-year limitation period.
    **Fact:** Only instituting a valid civil suit for possession in a competent court stops the clock for adverse possession.
8.  **Myth:** A tenant who doesn't pay rent is an encroacher.
    **Fact:** A tenant is not an encroacher. They entered legally. You must file an eviction suit under rent control or transfer of property laws.
9.  **Myth:** I have to be physically present in India to file an encroachment suit (for NRIs).
    **Fact:** NRIs can execute a Special Power of Attorney (SPA) allowing a trusted person in India to file and manage the lawsuit on their behalf.
10. **Myth:** Encroachment cases take 20 years to resolve.
    **Fact:** While final title disputes can take time, a temporary injunction (stay order) can often be obtained in a matter of days to freeze the situation.

Immediately file a written police complaint for criminal trespass under BNS Section 329. If the police say it's a civil matter, contact a lawyer to send a legal notice and file a civil suit for eviction and a stay order.

*Assistant:* Police often hesitate to intervene in land disputes, terming them "civil in nature." You should hire an advocate and approach the Civil Court immediately to file a suit for injunction (Order 39 Rule 1 & 2 CPC). The civil court can pass a stay order directing the encroacher to stop work, and can also direct the police to enforce the stay.

**Conversation 11: Immediate Trespass**
*Citizen:* Some unknown people have just broken my lock and entered my vacant house today!
*Assistant:* Call 112 immediately! This is an active crime (House Trespass under BNS Section 330). Because the trespass is happening right now, you have the right to private defense of property, and the police have the power to arrest the intruders on the spot and restore your possession.

**Conversation 13: Time Limit to File Suit**
*Citizen:* My land was encroached in 2015. Can I file a case now in 2026?
*Assistant:* Yes, you can. The Limitation Act provides a 12-year window to file a civil suit for the recovery of possession based on title. Since it has been 11 years, you must file the suit immediately before the 12-year period expires in 2027 to prevent them from claiming adverse possession.

**Conversation 15: Agricultural Land Demarcation**
*Citizen:* Is the process different for farming land?
*Assistant:* For agricultural land, disputes regarding boundaries are primarily handled by the Revenue Courts (Tehsildar / SDM) under the State Land Revenue Act. It is often faster than civil courts because the Tehsildar can order official measurement and direct the removal of boundaries on the spot.
