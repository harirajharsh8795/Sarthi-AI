# Lease and Rent Agreements under Indian Law

## Overview
**English:** A lease is a legal contract under which a property owner (lessor) grants another person (lessee) the right to use and occupy their property for a specified period, in exchange for a periodic payment (rent) or a lump-sum premium. In India, leases are primarily governed by the Transfer of Property Act, 1882, along with state-specific rent control laws and the newly proposed Model Tenancy Act. Understanding lease laws is crucial for protecting the rights of both landlords and tenants, ensuring smooth property transactions, and preventing illegal evictions or property disputes.

## Quick Summary
**English:** A lease transfers the right to enjoy an immovable property for a certain time, whereas ownership remains with the landlord. Any lease exceeding 11 months must be compulsorily registered and stamped under Indian law.

## Definition
**English:** Section 105 of the Transfer of Property Act, 1882, defines a lease of immovable property as a transfer of a right to enjoy such property, made for a certain time, express or implied, or in perpetuity, in consideration of a price paid or promised, or of money, a share of crops, service or any other thing of value, to be rendered periodically or on specified occasions to the transferor by the transferee, who accepts the transfer on such terms.
**Hindi (Unicode):** संपत्ति हस्तांतरण अधिनियम, 1882 की धारा 105 के अनुसार, अचल संपत्ति का पट्टा उस संपत्ति का आनंद लेने के अधिकार का हस्तांतरण है, जो एक निश्चित समय के लिए या शाश्वत रूप से, किसी भुगतान या वचनबद्ध मूल्य के बदले में किया जाता है।
**Hinglish:** Transfer of Property Act, 1882 ke Section 105 ke mutabiq, immovable property ka lease us property ko enjoy karne ke right ka transfer hai, jo ek fixed time ya hamesha ke liye, rent ya premium ke badle mein hota hai.

## Why It Matters
**English:** Proper lease agreements prevent unauthorized occupation, clarify maintenance duties, establish clear eviction grounds, and protect tenants from arbitrary rent hikes. They provide legal standing in court in case of disputes.

## Legal Background
### History & Purpose
The concept of leasing was formalised in India through the Transfer of Property Act (TPA) of 1882 to regulate land and property relations established during British rule. The primary purpose is to distinguish between the transfer of ownership (sale) and the transfer of usage rights (lease), providing a statutory framework to balance the interests of landlords and tenants. Later, to prevent tenant exploitation after World War II and partition, various State Rent Control Acts were enacted.

### Applicable Acts
1. **The Transfer of Property Act, 1882** (General law governing leases)
2. **The Registration Act, 1908** (Mandates registration of leases exceeding 11 months)
3. **The Indian Stamp Act, 1899** (Regulates stamp duty on lease deeds)
4. **State-specific Rent Control Acts** (e.g., Delhi Rent Control Act 1958, Maharashtra Rent Control Act 1999)
5. **The Model Tenancy Act, 2021** (Proposed federal framework adopted by various states)

### Relevant Constitutional Articles
- **Article 300A:** Right to Property - No person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law. (Protects landlords from illegal encroachment).
- **Article 21:** Right to Life and Personal Liberty - Courts have interpreted this to include the right to shelter and dignified living for tenants.

### Relevant Sections
- **Section 105, TPA, 1882:** Defines Lease, Lessor, Lessee, Premium, and Rent.
- **Section 106, TPA, 1882:** Duration of certain leases in absence of written contract or local usage (month-to-month for residential, year-to-year for agricultural/manufacturing).
- **Section 107, TPA, 1882:** How leases are made (written and registered for term exceeding one year).
- **Section 108, TPA, 1882:** Rights and liabilities of lessor and lessee.
- **Section 111, TPA, 1882:** Determination (termination) of lease.
- **Section 17(1)(d), Registration Act, 1908:** Compulsory registration of leases of immovable property from year to year, or for any term exceeding one year.

## Applicability
- **Who is covered:** Any individual, company, trust, or legal entity entering into an agreement to rent or lease residential, commercial, or agricultural property.
- **Who is exempt:** Leave and License agreements (governed by the Indian Easements Act, 1882) are not considered leases as they do not transfer interest in the property, only permission to occupy.
- **Special conditions:** Agricultural leases are often governed by state-specific local laws and customs rather than the general provisions of the TPA.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure
**English:**
1. **Drafting the Lease Deed:** Both parties agree on terms (rent, security deposit, duration, notice period) and draft a lease agreement.
2. **Purchasing Stamp Paper:** The agreement is printed on non-judicial stamp paper of appropriate value based on state stamp duty laws.
3. **Signing:** Both landlord and tenant sign all pages of the agreement in the presence of two witnesses.
4. **Registration:** If the lease duration is more than 11 months, both parties and witnesses visit the Sub-Registrar of Assurances office.
5. **Biometrics & Fee Payment:** Registration fees are paid, and biometric data/photographs are captured.
6. **Collection:** The registered lease deed is collected from the Sub-Registrar's office.

## Government Authorities
- **Sub-Registrar of Assurances:** For registration of the lease deed.
- **Rent Control Court / Rent Authority:** Specially appointed courts or authorities under State Rent Control Acts or Model Tenancy Act for dispute resolution.
- **Civil Courts:** For eviction suits and recovery of rent arrears if not covered by a specialized Rent Court.
- **Lok Adalat:** For amicable out-of-court settlement of rent disputes.
- **Police Department:** For tenant police verification (mandatory in many cities) and addressing criminal trespass or harassment.

## Penalties & Punishments
- **Failure to Register:** An unregistered lease deed (exceeding 11 months) cannot be admitted as evidence in a court of law (Section 49 of Registration Act).
- **Eviction due to Default:** Under Section 114 of TPA, if rent is unpaid, the lessor can determine the lease and seek eviction, though courts may grant relief against forfeiture if arrears and interest are paid.
- **Holding Over Penalty:** Under the Model Tenancy Act, if a tenant fails to vacate after the tenancy ends, the landlord is entitled to double the rent for the first two months and four times the rent thereafter.
- **Illegal Eviction by Landlord:** Disconnecting essential services (water, electricity) is a punishable offense under state laws, often leading to fines or orders for immediate restoration.

## Exceptions
- **Month-to-Month Tenancies (11 months or less):** Do not legally require registration, reducing upfront costs, which is why 11-month agreements are standard practice in India.
- **Agricultural Leases:** TPA Chapter V provisions do not strictly apply to agricultural leases unless the State Government explicitly notifies it.
- **Leave and License:** These do not create any property interest for the licensee and can be revoked easily compared to a formal lease.

## Important Timelines
- **Notice Period for Termination:** Under Section 106 TPA (without a written contract), 15 days' notice for residential leases and 6 months' notice for manufacturing/agricultural leases is required. Written contracts define their own notice periods (usually 1-2 months).
- **Registration Timeline:** A lease deed must be registered within 4 months from the date of execution.
- **Limitation for Arrears:** A landlord can file a civil suit to recover unpaid rent only within 3 years from the date the rent became due under the Limitation Act, 1963.

## Common Legal Mistakes
- **Skipping Police Verification:** Landlords often ignore mandatory tenant police verification, which is an offense under Section 188 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) / IPC, leading to potential fines or imprisonment.
- **Verbal Agreements:** Relying solely on verbal rent agreements makes eviction or recovering unpaid rent extremely difficult in court.
- **Ignoring Stamp Duty:** Paying insufficient stamp duty to save money makes the document invalid as evidence until a heavy penalty (up to 10 times the deficit) is paid.
- **Illegal Eviction Tactics:** Landlords locking out tenants or cutting off power without following due legal process.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is the difference between a Lease and a Leave and License Agreement?**
   A lease creates an interest in the property and is harder to terminate, governed by the Transfer of Property Act. A Leave and License grants mere permission to use the property without transferring any interest, governed by the Easements Act. It is easier for landlords to evict under Leave and License.
   
2. **Why are most rent agreements for 11 months?**
   Under the Registration Act, 1908, lease agreements for a period exceeding 11 months require mandatory registration. To avoid the hassle, time, and high stamp duty costs of registration, parties prefer 11-month agreements.

3. **Can a landlord evict a tenant without notice?**
   No, arbitrary eviction is illegal. A landlord must provide a legal notice specifying valid grounds for eviction (like non-payment of rent, breach of terms, or personal need) and follow the legal eviction process through civil courts or rent tribunals.

4. **Is police verification of a tenant mandatory?**
   Yes, in most major cities and states in India, police verification of tenants is legally mandatory. Failure to do so can result in prosecution of the landlord under Section 188 of BNS (formerly IPC).

5. **What happens if an 11-month agreement is not registered?**
   An 11-month agreement does not legally require registration. It is valid and enforceable in court even if it is only notarized or printed on adequate stamp paper.

6. **How much stamp duty is required for a lease agreement?**
   Stamp duty varies by state. It is usually calculated as a percentage of the total annual rent plus the security deposit. For example, in Delhi, it is generally 2% of the average annual rent.

7. **Can a tenant deduct repair costs from the rent?**
   Yes, under Section 108(f) of the TPA, if the landlord fails to make necessary repairs within a reasonable time after notice, the tenant can make the repairs and deduct the cost with interest from the rent.

8. **Is it legal for a landlord to disconnect electricity or water?**
   No, essential services like water and electricity cannot be disconnected by the landlord, even if the tenant defaults on rent. The tenant can approach the rent authority or civil court for immediate restoration.

9. **What is the Model Tenancy Act (MTA)?**
   The MTA is a framework introduced by the central government in 2021 to revamp rent control laws, establish dedicated rent courts, mandate written agreements, and cap security deposits (2 months for residential, 6 months for commercial). States are gradually adopting it.

10. **Can a lease be terminated before the expiry of the term?**
    Yes, either party can terminate the lease prematurely by giving a notice as specified in the termination clause of the written agreement (usually 1 or 2 months' notice).

11. **What is 'Holding Over' by a tenant?**
    If a tenant continues to occupy the property after the lease expires and the landlord accepts rent from them, it creates a tenancy by 'holding over' (Section 116 TPA), renewing the lease on a month-to-month basis.

12. **Are oral lease agreements valid?**
    For leases exceeding one year, oral agreements are strictly invalid. For month-to-month tenancies, oral agreements technically exist but are highly discouraged as they are extremely difficult to prove in court.

13. **Can a tenant sublet the property?**
    Under Section 108(j) of the TPA, a lessee may sublet the property, BUT most modern lease agreements contain an explicit clause prohibiting subletting without the landlord's written consent.

14. **What happens to a lease if the landlord dies?**
    The lease is not automatically terminated upon the death of the landlord. The legal heirs inherit the property along with the rights and liabilities of the lease agreement until its expiration.

15. **What happens to a lease if the property is sold?**
    The new owner steps into the shoes of the old landlord. The existing lease remains valid, and the tenant now owes rent and duties to the new owner.

16. **How to resolve a dispute over the security deposit?**
    If a landlord unjustly withholds the security deposit after the tenant vacates, the tenant can send a legal notice. If unresolved, they can file a complaint with the Rent Authority or file a civil suit for recovery.

17. **Is a notarized rent agreement legally binding?**
    For periods up to 11 months, a notarized rent agreement on appropriate stamp paper is valid. However, for periods exceeding 11 months, notarization is not a substitute for compulsory registration at the Sub-Registrar's office.

18. **Can a tenant claim ownership through adverse possession?**
    A tenant cannot claim ownership through adverse possession as long as their entry and possession were permissive (based on a lease), regardless of how many decades they have lived there. Adverse possession requires "hostile" possession.

19. **What is the Pagdi system?**
    Pagdi is a traditional tenancy system in cities like Mumbai where the tenant pays a lump sum (premium) to the landlord and thereafter pays a nominal rent. The tenant becomes a co-owner of sorts and has strong eviction protections.

20. **Can landlords increase rent arbitrarily every year?**
    Rent increases must be governed by the escalation clause in the lease agreement (e.g., 5% or 10% annual increase). In areas governed by older Rent Control Acts, standard rent laws heavily restrict arbitrary hikes.

## Common Myths vs Facts
1. **Myth:** A tenant can capture the property if they live there for 12 years.
   **Fact:** False. Permissive possession under a tenancy agreement never turns into adverse possession, even after 50 years.
2. **Myth:** Notarized agreements for 5 years are valid.
   **Fact:** False. Any lease over 11 months MUST be registered. A notary seal does not fulfill this legal requirement.
3. **Myth:** Landlords can physically throw out a defaulting tenant.
   **Fact:** False. Landlords cannot take the law into their own hands. They must follow the due legal process of eviction through the courts.
4. **Myth:** 11-month agreements don't need stamp duty.
   **Fact:** False. While they don't need *registration*, they absolutely must be executed on non-judicial stamp paper of appropriate value.
5. **Myth:** A landlord can lock the house if rent is delayed.
   **Fact:** False. Locking a tenant out or denying them entry without a court order is illegal criminal trespass.
6. **Myth:** The tenant is always responsible for all repairs.
   **Fact:** Major structural repairs are the landlord's responsibility; minor day-to-day maintenance is the tenant's responsibility.
7. **Myth:** Landlords can visit the rented property anytime.
   **Fact:** Tenants have a right to privacy. Landlords must give reasonable prior notice before inspecting the property.
8. **Myth:** Leave and license is the exact same as a lease.
   **Fact:** Legally they are distinct. A lease transfers property interest; a license merely grants permission to occupy.
9. **Myth:** If the lease expires, the tenant becomes an illegal squatter instantly.
   **Fact:** If the landlord accepts rent after expiry, the tenant becomes a "tenant holding over." Otherwise, they are a "tenant at sufferance" but still require legal eviction.
10. **Myth:** Police can evict tenants if the landlord complains.
    **Fact:** Police have no jurisdiction over civil tenancy disputes. Eviction orders can only be passed by a civil court or rent tribunal.
