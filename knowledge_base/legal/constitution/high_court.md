# High Courts in India: Powers, Jurisdiction, and Citizen's Guide

## Overview
**English:** The High Court is the highest judicial authority at the state level in India. Established under Article 214 of the Constitution, it exercises vast original, appellate, and writ jurisdictions. It acts as the guardian of fundamental rights within the state and holds the power of superintendence over all subordinate courts and tribunals in its territorial jurisdiction. Unlike the Supreme Court, a High Court's writ jurisdiction extends beyond fundamental rights to any other legal rights.

## Quick Summary
**English:** High Courts enforce Fundamental Rights and other legal rights via Writs (Article 226), hear civil and criminal appeals from District Courts, and monitor the functioning of the lower judiciary (Article 227). Every state is constitutionally mandated to have a High Court, though Parliament has the authority to establish a common High Court for two or more states or Union Territories.

## Definition
**English:** A High Court is the principal civil court of original and appellate jurisdiction in the state, vested with the constitutional power to issue prerogative writs, supervise subordinate courts, and ensure the administration of justice as mandated by the Constitution of India.
**Hindi Unicode:** उच्च न्यायालय राज्य में मूल और अपीलीय क्षेत्राधिकार का प्रमुख दीवानी न्यायालय है, जिसे विशेषाधिकार रिट जारी करने, अधीनस्थ न्यायालयों की निगरानी करने और भारत के संविधान द्वारा अनिवार्य न्याय प्रशासन सुनिश्चित करने की संवैधानिक शक्ति प्राप्त है।
**Hinglish:** High Court state ka main appellate aur original court hai jiske paas writs issue karne, lower courts ko supervise karne aur justice ensure karne ki constitutional power hoti hai.

## Why It Matters
**English:** High Courts are highly accessible to citizens compared to the Supreme Court. They serve as a critical check on executive overreach at the state level. Their ability to issue writs for both fundamental and ordinary legal rights makes them the primary protector of citizen liberties against state action.

## Legal Background
The functioning, powers, and organization of the High Courts are strictly governed by Part VI, Chapter V of the Constitution of India.

*   **Article 214:** Mandates that there shall be a High Court for each State.
*   **Article 215:** Declares every High Court to be a Court of Record and grants it all the powers of such a court, including the power to punish for contempt of itself.
*   **Article 226:** Power of High Courts to issue certain writs (Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Quo Warranto, Certiorari) for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights (Part III) and for any other purpose.
*   **Article 227:** Power of superintendence over all courts and tribunals throughout the territories in relation to which it exercises jurisdiction.
*   **Article 231:** Power of Parliament to establish a common High Court for two or more States.
*   **Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023:** Specifically, Section 528 (equivalent to Section 482 of the old CrPC) provides inherent powers to the High Court to make orders necessary to prevent abuse of the process of any court or to secure the ends of justice.

## Applicability
*   **Who is Covered:** All citizens of India, non-citizens (in cases of fundamental rights like Article 21), artificial legal persons (companies, trusts) seeking relief within the territorial jurisdiction of the respective High Court.
*   **Who is Exempt:** Decisions made by Armed Forces Tribunals (exempted from Article 227 superintendence).
*   **Special Conditions:** For a High Court to entertain a matter, the cause of action (wholly or in part) must arise within its territorial jurisdiction.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure
**English:**
1. **Drafting:** A petition or appeal is drafted by an advocate with all annexures, impugned orders, and an accompanying affidavit.
2. **Filing:** The petition is filed in the High Court Registry. Defect checking (Stamp Reporting) takes place.
3. **Curing Defects:** The advocate cures any formatting or missing document defects pointed out by the registry.
4. **Listing:** The matter is listed before the appropriate bench (Single Judge or Division Bench) for an admission hearing.
5. **Admission/Notice:** The judge hears preliminary arguments. If satisfied, notice is issued to the opposite party.
6. **Reply/Rejoinder:** The opposite party files a Counter Affidavit. The petitioner can file a Rejoinder.
7. **Final Hearing:** Detailed arguments are made on merits.
8. **Judgment:** The court delivers its judgment/order, which can be challenged in the Supreme Court via Special Leave Petition (SLP).

## Required Documents
1. **Writ Petition / Memorandum of Appeal:** The main drafting pleading.
2. **Affidavit:** Sworn statement verifying the contents of the petition.
3. **Vakalatnama:** Authorization given by the client to the advocate.
4. **Impugned Order:** Certified copy of the lower court or authority's order being challenged.
5. **Annexures:** Supporting evidence, letters, notices, or government orders.
6. **Court Fees:** Appropriate stamp duty or court fee as per the High Court Rules.
7. **ID Proof:** Aadhar Card/Voter ID of the petitioner.

## Government Authorities
1. **Chief Justice of the High Court:** Administrative and Judicial head of the state's High Court.
2. **Puisne Judges:** Other sitting judges of the High Court.
3. **Registrar General:** The administrative head of the High Court Registry.
4. **Advocate General:** The highest law officer of the State Government who appears for the State in the High Court.
5. **Standing Counsel:** Advocates appointed by government departments or authorities to represent them.

## Penalties & Punishments
*   **Civil Contempt (Contempt of Courts Act, 1971):** Willful disobedience of a High Court judgment, decree, direction, or order. Punishable with simple imprisonment for a term which may extend to 6 months, or with fine up to ₹2,000, or both.
*   **Criminal Contempt:** Publication of any matter or doing an act which scandalizes or lowers the authority of the High Court, prejudices judicial proceedings, or interferes with the administration of justice. Same punishment as civil contempt.
*   **Perjury:** Filing false affidavits before the High Court is punishable under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) with imprisonment which may extend to 7 years and a fine.
*   **Frivolous Litigation:** The High Court often imposes exemplary costs (monetary fines) on petitioners for wasting the court's time with malicious or baseless petitions.

## Exceptions
*   The High Court's power of superintendence (Article 227) does not extend to any court or tribunal constituted by or under any law relating to the Armed Forces (e.g., Armed Forces Tribunal).
*   Election disputes (Parliamentary or State Legislature) can only be challenged via an Election Petition under the Representation of the People Act, 1951, bypassing general writ jurisdiction initially.

## Important Timelines
*   **Civil Appeal from Lower Court Decree:** 90 days from the date of the decree.
*   **Criminal Appeal (Death Sentence confirmation):** Normally 30 days.
*   **Criminal Appeal against Conviction:** 60 days.
*   **Writ Petitions (Article 226):** No specific statutory time limit, but governed by the doctrine of "Laches". It must be filed within a "reasonable time". Unexplained delays usually lead to dismissal.
*   **Review Petition:** Generally 30 days from the date of the judgment.

## Common Legal Mistakes
1. **Approaching HC directly without exhausting remedies:** Bypassing statutory appeals (like an appeal before a Commissioner or Tribunal) and directly filing a writ. The High Court will likely dismiss it, directing the petitioner to alternative remedies.
2. **Concealment of Material Facts:** Hiding adverse facts in the petition. This leads to immediate dismissal and heavy penalties.
3. **Delay and Laches:** Waiting years to challenge an administrative order without a valid reason for the delay.
4. **Drafting Errors:** Not filing proper certified copies or missing crucial annexures, keeping the case stuck in the registry for months.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **Can I approach the High Court if my fundamental rights are violated?**
   **English:** Yes, under Article 226 of the Constitution, you can file a Writ Petition directly in the High Court for fundamental right violations.
   **Hindi:** हाँ, अनुच्छेद 226 के तहत आप मौलिक अधिकारों के उल्लंघन के लिए सीधे उच्च न्यायालय में रिट याचिका दायर कर सकते हैं।
   **Hinglish:** Haan, Article 226 ke under aap fundamental rights ke violation par direct High Court ja sakte hain.

2. **Is it mandatory to hire an advocate in the High Court?**
   **English:** No, a citizen can argue their own case as a "Party-in-person", but prior permission from the court is required and legal procedures are complex.
   **Hindi:** नहीं, आप 'पार्टी-इन-पर्सन' के रूप में अपना केस खुद लड़ सकते हैं, लेकिन इसके लिए अदालत की अनुमति आवश्यक है।
   **Hinglish:** Nahi, aap apna case khud bhi lad sakte hain as 'Party-in-person', par court se permission leni padti hai aur procedure complex hota hai.

3. **What is the difference between Article 32 and Article 226?**
   **English:** Article 32 is for approaching the Supreme Court ONLY for Fundamental Rights. Article 226 is for approaching High Courts for Fundamental Rights AND other legal rights.
   **Hindi:** अनुच्छेद 32 के तहत केवल मौलिक अधिकारों के लिए सुप्रीम कोर्ट जाया जाता है, जबकि अनुच्छेद 226 के तहत मौलिक और कानूनी अधिकारों के लिए उच्च न्यायालय।
   **Hinglish:** Article 32 sirf Fundamental rights ke liye SC jane ke liye hai, jabki 226 Fundamental aur legal rights dono ke liye HC ke liye hai.

4. **Can a High Court hear cases from multiple states?**

## Common Myths vs Facts

1. **Myth:** The Supreme Court is the only court that can issue Writs.
   **Fact:** High Courts have an even wider writ jurisdiction (Article 226) than the Supreme Court (Article 32) because they can issue writs for fundamental rights as well as ordinary legal rights.

2. **Myth:** You can directly file any appeal in the Supreme Court bypassing the High Court.
   **Fact:** You must generally exhaust the High Court remedy first. The Supreme Court only takes direct appeals in highly exceptional cases or specific statutory matters.

3. **Myth:** A High Court's jurisdiction is limited strictly to its state borders.
   **Fact:** A High Court can issue writs against individuals or authorities outside its state boundaries if the cause of action partially or wholly arises within its territory.

4. **Myth:** High Courts manage the police department.
   **Fact:** High Courts do not manage the police (Executive function), but they can direct police actions, order investigations, and protect citizens from police excesses.

5. **Myth:** Only poor people can file a PIL.
   **Fact:** Any citizen can file a PIL provided the issue affects the public at large, irrespective of the petitioner's financial status.

6. **Myth:** PILs can be filed for personal property disputes.
   **Fact:** PILs are strictly for "Public Interest". Filing a PIL for personal gain or private disputes results in heavy financial penalties.

7. **Myth:** The High Court cannot overturn a government policy.
   **Fact:** The High Court can strike down a state government policy or law if it violates the Constitution or fundamental rights.

8. **Myth:** Proceedings in the High Court are only conducted in English.
   **Fact:** While English is the primary language, the Governor (with the President's consent) can authorize the use of Hindi or the state's official language in High Court proceedings.

9. **Myth:** Quashing of an FIR means you are acquitted.
   **Fact:** Quashing means the FIR itself is invalid or abusive and is erased from the record before a trial concludes. Acquittal happens after a full trial.

10. **Myth:** You must pay a percentage of your claim as court fees in writ petitions.
    **Fact:** Writ petitions usually have a fixed nominal court fee, not an ad valorem (percentage-based) fee like civil recovery suits.

*Assistant:* You can approach the High Court under Section 528 of the BNSS (formerly 482 CrPC) for quashing the FIR. If the court finds the allegations maliciously instituted, it will cancel the FIR. *Assistant:* Since the Sessions Court rejected it, you must urgently file an application for Anticipatory Bail in the High Court. *Citizen:* My civil case has been pending in the district court for 15 years with no progress. Can the High Court help? (मेरा दीवानी मामला 15 साल से जिला अदालत में लंबित है, कोई प्रगति नहीं हुई है। क्या उच्च न्यायालय मदद कर सकता है?)
*Assistant:* Yes. Under Article 227 of the Constitution, the High Court has supervisory power.
