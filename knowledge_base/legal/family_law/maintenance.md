# Maintenance and Alimony in India (Guzara Bhatta)

## Overview

**English:** Maintenance (commonly known as alimony or spousal/child support) is the legal obligation of an individual to provide financial support to their dependent spouse, children, or parents who are unable to maintain themselves. In India, maintenance is a statutory right aimed at preventing destitution and vagrancy. Following the implementation of the new criminal laws, the general secular right to maintenance, previously governed by Section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), is now governed by Section 144 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023. Additional personal and special laws exist to cover specific scenarios.

## Definition

**English:** Maintenance encompasses the provision of basic necessities of life, including food, clothing, shelter, medical attendance, and in the case of children, education expenses. Alimony usually refers specifically to spousal support granted during or after a divorce, but in Indian law, the terms are often used interchangeably under the broader umbrella of "maintenance."

## Why It Matters

**English:** Maintenance laws serve as an instrument of social justice. They ensure that financially weaker family members, particularly women, children, and elderly parents, are not left destitute due to marital breakdowns or familial neglect. It enforces the moral and legal duty of earning members to support their dependents.

## Legal Background

The framework for maintenance in India is broad, encompassing both secular laws applicable to all citizens irrespective of religion, and personal laws based on the applicant's religion.

1. **Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS) - Section 144:**
   - Replaces the erstwhile Section 125 of CrPC.
   - It is a secular provision, applicable to all religions.
   - Provides a quick, summary remedy for wives, children (legitimate or illegitimate), and parents unable to maintain themselves.

2. **Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 (HMA):**
   - **Section 24:** Provides for *Pendente Lite* maintenance (temporary maintenance during the pendency of matrimonial proceedings) and litigation expenses. Notably, either the husband or the wife can claim this if they have no independent income.
   - **Section 25:** Deals with permanent alimony and maintenance granted at the time of passing the decree (e.g., divorce decree) or anytime subsequent thereto.

3. **Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (PWDVA):**
   - **Section 20:** Empowers the Magistrate to direct the Respondent to pay monetary relief to meet the expenses incurred and losses suffered by the aggrieved woman and any child of hers as a result of domestic violence.

4. **Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956 (HAMA):**
   - **Section 18:** Wife's right to maintenance (can live separately without forfeiting her right to maintenance under specific conditions).
   - **Section 20:** Maintenance of children and aged parents.

5. **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986:**
   - Provides for a reasonable and fair provision and maintenance to be made and paid to her within the iddat period by her former husband. However, Supreme Court rulings (like *Danial Latifi* and *Shabana Bano*) have clarified that Muslim women can also claim maintenance beyond the iddat period under the secular CrPC 125 (now BNSS 144).

6. **Special Marriage Act, 1954:**
   - **Section 36:** Alimony pendente lite (for wife).
   - **Section 37:** Permanent alimony and maintenance (for wife).

7. **Maintenance and Welfare of Parents and Senior Citizens Act, 2007:**
   - Specifically tailored for elderly parents and senior citizens to claim maintenance from children or legal heirs via special Tribunals.

## Applicability

**Who is Covered (Who can claim?):**
- A wife unable to maintain herself.
- Minor children (legitimate or illegitimate, married or unmarried - though married minor daughters are complex if their husband has no means).
- Adult children holding physical or mental abnormality/injury making them unable to maintain themselves.
- Father or mother unable to maintain themselves.
- Under HMA (Sec 24/25), a husband unable to maintain himself can also claim maintenance from an earning wife.

**Who is Exempt (When can maintenance be refused?):**
- Under BNSS 144 (CrPC 125), a wife is not entitled to maintenance if:
  1. She is living in adultery.
  2. She refuses to live with her husband without any sufficient reason.
  3. They are living separately by mutual consent.
- A financially independent spouse with sufficient income to maintain their standard of living may be denied maintenance, though comparative income is assessed.

## Rights of Citizens

**English:**
- Right to claim interim maintenance while the main court case is ongoing.
- Right to demand an exact disclosure of the opposing party's assets and income (Rajnesh vs Neha guidelines).
- Right to approach the court for enhancement of maintenance if the cost of living increases or the payer's salary increases.
- Right to seek attachment of salary or property of the defaulter if they fail to pay the court-ordered maintenance.

## Duties & Responsibilities

**English:**
- **Duty to Disclose Truthfully:** Both parties must file accurate Affidavits of Assets and Liabilities. Hiding income can lead to perjury charges.
- **Duty to Pay:** The person ordered to pay maintenance must do so within the stipulated time frame each month.
- **Duty to Inform Court:** If the receiver gets a high-paying job or remarries, they must inform the court, as it affects the maintenance eligibility.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure

**English:**
1. **Filing the Petition:** The applicant files a maintenance petition through a lawyer in the Family Court or Judicial Magistrate's Court (under BNSS 144, HMA 24, or PWDVA 20).
2. **Issuance of Notice:** The court issues a notice/summons to the respondent, directing them to appear in court.
3. **Filing of Affidavits:** Both parties must file an "Affidavit of Disclosure of Assets and Liabilities" as per the Supreme Court format in *Rajnesh v. Neha*. This includes bank statements, income tax returns, salary slips, and expenditure details.
4. **Interim Maintenance Order:** The court evaluates the affidavits and passes an interim order to provide immediate financial relief while the case proceeds.
5. **Evidence and Cross-Examination:** Both parties present their evidence. Witnesses may be cross-examined regarding income, lifestyle, and standard of living.
6. **Final Arguments and Judgment:** The court hears the final arguments and passes a final judgment, determining the permanent monthly maintenance amount or a one-time lump-sum alimony.
7. **Execution (If unpaid):** If the respondent defaults, the applicant files an execution petition. The court can issue a warrant for arrest, attach bank accounts, or attach salary/property.

## Required Documents

To successfully claim or defend a maintenance petition, the following documents are crucial as part of the mandatory Affidavit of Assets and Liabilities:
- Identity Proof (Aadhaar, PAN, Voter ID).
- Address Proof (Rent agreement, utility bills).
- Salary Slips (Last 6 to 12 months).
- Income Tax Returns (ITR) (Last 3 years).
- Bank Account Statements (All active accounts, last 3 to 5 years).
- Proof of Educational Qualifications.
- Proof of Assets (Property papers, vehicle RC, mutual funds, FDs, gold/jewelry records).
- Proof of Liabilities/Loans (Home loan, personal loan EMI statements, credit card bills).
- Dependent Details (School fee receipts for children, medical bills for elderly parents/self).
- Proof of Standard of Living (Credit card statements reflecting luxury expenses, travel history, social media posts showing lifestyle - often used by applicants to prove the respondent's hidden income).

## Government Authorities

1. **Family Courts:** Established under the Family Courts Act, 1984, they are the primary venues for HMA and PWDVA maintenance matters.
2. **Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC):** Handles applications under Section 144 of BNSS (formerly 125 CrPC) and domestic violence (PWDVA) complaints.
3. **Lok Adalat & Mediation Centres:** Attached to Family Courts to encourage amicable lump-sum settlements or mutually agreeable monthly maintenance figures without prolonged litigation.
4. **Maintenance Tribunals:** specifically for Senior Citizens under the Maintenance and Welfare of Parents and Senior Citizens Act.

## Penalties & Punishments

The law takes the non-payment of court-ordered maintenance very seriously.
- **Arrest and Imprisonment:** Under Section 144(3) of BNSS (formerly 125(3) CrPC), if a person fails to pay maintenance without sufficient cause, the Magistrate can issue a warrant for levying the amount. If the amount remains unpaid after warrant execution, the court can sentence the defaulter to imprisonment for a term which may extend to **one month** or until payment is made, *for each month's default*.
- **Attachment of Salary:** The court can order the employer of the defaulter to directly deduct the maintenance amount from their salary and remit it to the court/applicant.
- **Attachment of Property:** The court can attach movable and immovable properties of the defaulter and auction them to recover the arrears.
- **Striking off Defense:** In civil proceedings (like under HMA), if a spouse defaults on interim maintenance, the court may strike off their defense, meaning they will not be allowed to contest the divorce or child custody case further until arrears are cleared.

## Exceptions

- **Mutual Consent:** If the wife and husband agree to live separately by mutual consent, she cannot claim maintenance under BNSS 144.
- **Adultery:** A wife living in adultery is explicitly barred from claiming maintenance under BNSS 144.
- **Refusal to Live Without Cause:** If a wife refuses to live with her husband without sufficient reason (like cruelty or dowry demand), her claim may be rejected.
- **Remarriage:** Once a divorced spouse remarries, their right to claim maintenance from the former spouse extinguishes immediately.
- **Husband's Maintenance:** A husband can only claim maintenance under the Hindu Marriage Act (Sec 24/25) if he can prove he is physically/mentally incapacitated or genuinely unable to earn, while his wife earns well. It is not available to an able-bodied man who voluntarily chooses not to work.

## Important Timelines

- **Interim Maintenance Decision:** The Supreme Court directs that interim maintenance applications should ideally be disposed of within **60 days** of the respondent receiving notice.
- **Limitation for Claiming Arrears:** An application to recover unpaid maintenance (arrears) under BNSS 144(3) must be filed within **one year** from the date the amount became due. Arrears older than one year cannot be recovered through this specific criminal court execution process.
- **Date of Payment:** Based on the landmark judgment in *Rajnesh v. Neha (2020)*, maintenance is universally awarded from the **date of filing the application**, not from the date of the court's order.

## Common Legal Mistakes

1. **Hiding Income:** Filing a false affidavit of income and assets. Opposing lawyers often use social media posts, travel records, and credit card statements to expose hidden wealth, leading to severe penalties and adverse court orders.
2. **Waiting Too Long to File for Arrears:** Allowing maintenance arrears to pile up for over a year before filing an execution petition. Recovery via Magistrate courts is barred for dues older than 12 months.
3. **Assuming Cash Income Can't Be Proven:** Businessmen often claim zero income by showing only IT returns. Courts use the "lifestyle test" (cars owned, foreign trips, children's school fees) to assess true income.
4. **Leaving Jobs to Avoid Payment:** An able-bodied husband resigning from his job to claim he has "no income" to avoid paying maintenance. Courts routinely impute a notional income based on his qualifications and capability.
5. **Filing Multiple Maintenance Petitions:** Filing under CrPC/BNSS, HMA, and PWDVA simultaneously and hiding this fact. You must disclose previous maintenance orders to the new court. The court will adjust/set-off the amounts, not add them up doubly.

## Safety Tips

**English:**
- **Document Everything:** Keep copies of your spouse's ITR, bank statements, and property documents securely *before* relations turn fully hostile.
- **Social Media Caution:** If you claim inability to pay, do not post pictures of expensive holidays or luxury purchases on social media.
- **Use Banking Channels:** Always pay or receive maintenance through bank transfers (NEFT/RTGS/Cheque) to have a clear legal record. Never pay in cash without taking a signed, stamped receipt.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. **Can a working wife claim maintenance?**
   Yes, a working wife can claim maintenance if her income is not sufficient to maintain the standard of living she enjoyed in her matrimonial home. The court will calculate the difference between her income and the required amount.

2. **Can a husband claim maintenance from his wife?**
   Yes, but only under Sections 24 and 25 of the Hindu Marriage Act. He must prove that he is incapable of earning an independent income (e.g., due to disability) and that his wife has sufficient financial means.

3. **Does the new BNSS change how maintenance is calculated?**
   No. Section 144 of the BNSS replaces Section 125 of the CrPC. The legal principles, procedures, and the right to maintenance remain exactly the same as established by decades of jurisprudence.

4. **From which date is maintenance calculated?**
   Following the Supreme Court's mandate in *Rajnesh v. Neha*, maintenance must be paid from the date the application was filed in the court, not from the date the final order is passed.

5. **Is maintenance granted in a mutual consent divorce?**
   In a mutual consent divorce, the parties decide the alimony/maintenance amount themselves. It is usually paid as a full and final one-time settlement, after which neither party can claim maintenance in the future.

6. **What if the husband resigns from his job to avoid paying maintenance?**
   Courts are well aware of this tactic. If an able-bodied, qualified man deliberately leaves his job, the court will presume his "capacity to earn" based on his education and past salary, and order maintenance accordingly.

7. **How is the quantum of maintenance calculated?**
   There is no fixed mathematical formula. Courts consider the husband's net income, the wife's income, dependent parents, children's needs, and the lifestyle enjoyed by the couple during the marriage. A rough thumb rule often used is allocating 1/3rd to 1/4th of the husband's net salary to the wife.

8. **Can children claim maintenance?**
   Yes. Minor children (legitimate or illegitimate) can claim maintenance. Adult children can also claim it if they suffer from a physical or mental abnormality making them unable to support themselves.

9. **Can unmarried adult daughters claim maintenance?**
   Under Section 20 of the Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, an unmarried adult daughter can claim maintenance from her parents until she gets married, provided she cannot maintain herself.

10. **What happens if the maintenance order is ignored?**
    If the payer defaults, the receiver can file an execution petition. The Magistrate can issue an arrest warrant and send the defaulter to jail for up to one month for every month of default, or attach their property/salary.

11. **Does going to jail cancel the unpaid maintenance arrears?**
    No. Going to jail is a punishment for defying the court order, not a replacement for the debt. The arrears remain payable even after serving the jail sentence.

12. **Can parents claim maintenance from their children?**
    Yes. Under BNSS Section 144, HAMA, and the Senior Citizens Act, biological and adoptive parents who cannot maintain themselves can claim maintenance from their earning children (sons and daughters alike).

13. **Is maintenance taxable as income?**
    Monthly maintenance received is treated as a personal receipt and is generally not taxable in the hands of the receiver. A one-time lump-sum alimony is considered a capital receipt and is totally tax-free.

14. **Can maintenance be claimed in live-in relationships?**
    Yes. The Supreme Court has ruled that women in long-term live-in relationships "in the nature of marriage" are entitled to claim maintenance under the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act (PWDVA).

15. **What if the wife is caught in adultery?**
    Under Section 144 of BNSS, a wife "living in adultery" (a continuous course of adulterous conduct, not just a single lapse) is completely debarred from claiming maintenance.

16. **Can a second wife claim maintenance?**
    If the second marriage is legally void (e.g., the husband married her without divorcing his first wife), the second wife generally cannot claim maintenance under BNSS 144, but she can claim it under PWDVA if she was kept in the dark about the first marriage. Children from the second marriage, however, have full rights to maintenance.

17. **Can maintenance be increased in the future?**
    Yes. Under BNSS Section 145 (formerly CrPC 127), the court can increase or decrease the maintenance amount if there is a material change in circumstances, such as inflation, salary hikes, or job loss.

18. **Can maintenance be claimed if both are living under the same roof?**
    Yes. Under PWDVA, a woman facing economic abuse can claim maintenance even while living in the shared household with the husband.

19. **What is an "Affidavit of Assets and Liabilities"?**
    It is a mandatory sworn statement ordered by the Supreme Court (*Rajnesh v. Neha*) where both parties must completely and truthfully disclose all their bank accounts, properties, loans, and income sources to the court.

20. **Can I claim maintenance if my husband lives abroad (NRI)?**
    Yes. You can file for maintenance in India. Serving summons and executing the order can be complex and may require the assistance of the Ministry of External Affairs or invoking the Passport Act to impound his passport if he defaults.

## Common Myths vs Facts

1. **Myth:** Working women cannot get maintenance.
   **Fact:** A working woman can get maintenance if her income is too low to sustain her standard of living compared to her husband's wealth.
2. **Myth:** A husband can never get maintenance.
   **Fact:** Under the Hindu Marriage Act, a husband incapable of earning (e.g., due to disability) can claim maintenance from his earning wife.
3. **Myth:** If the husband has no salary slip, he doesn't have to pay.
   **Fact:** Courts assess "capacity to earn" and lifestyle (cars, businesses, expenses) to fix maintenance even for businessmen claiming zero income.
4. **Myth:** Maintenance is only for wives.
   **Fact:** Minor children, disabled adult children, and elderly parents have equal rights to claim maintenance.
5. **Myth:** Going to jail for a month clears the maintenance debt.
   **Fact:** Jail is a penalty for contempt, not a waiver of the debt. The arrears still stand and can be recovered by attaching property.
6. **Myth:** You can hide bank accounts from the Family Court.
   **Fact:** Deliberately hiding assets in the mandatory Affidavit of Assets and Liabilities amounts to perjury and can lead to criminal prosecution.
7. **Myth:** Divorced Muslim women can only get maintenance for 3 months (Iddat).
   **Fact:** The Supreme Court (*Shabana Bano*, *Danial Latifi*) has ruled that divorced Muslim women can claim maintenance for life or until remarriage under the secular CrPC 125 (now BNSS 144).
8. **Myth:** A husband can transfer his property to his mother to avoid paying maintenance.
   **Fact:** Courts look at the true control of assets. Fraudulent transfers done specifically to defeat maintenance claims can be ignored by the court.
9. **Myth:** Maintenance is calculated from the date of the court's final order.
   **Fact:** It is calculated from the date the application was filed in court, meaning the husband will have to pay lump-sum arrears for the years the trial took.
10. **Myth:** Alimony and Maintenance are exactly the same thing.
    **Fact:** Practically they overlap, but Alimony usually refers to spousal support upon divorce, whereas Maintenance covers basic living expenses for spouse, children, or parents even without divorce.
