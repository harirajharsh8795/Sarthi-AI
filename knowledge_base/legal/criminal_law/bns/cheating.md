# Cheating and Dishonestly Inducing Delivery of Property (BNS Section 318)

## Overview
**English:** Under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023, "Cheating" is a severe criminal offense covered under Section 318 (which incorporates the essence of the former Sections 415 to 420 of the Indian Penal Code). It involves deceiving someone fraudulently or dishonestly, thereby inducing them to deliver any property, consent to retain property, or intentionally causing them to do (or omit to do) something that causes damage to their body, mind, reputation, or property. Cheating undermines the basic fabric of trust in personal, professional, and commercial transactions. 

**Hindi Unicode:** भारतीय न्याय संहिता (BNS) 2023 के तहत, "धोखाधड़ी" (Cheating) धारा 318 के अंतर्गत एक गंभीर आपराधिक अपराध है (जिसमें पूर्व भारतीय दंड संहिता की धारा 415 से 420 का सार शामिल है)। इसमें किसी को धोखे से या बेईमानी से गुमराह करना शामिल है, जिससे उसे कोई संपत्ति सौंपने के लिए प्रेरित किया जाए, या जानबूझकर ऐसा कुछ करने (या न करने) के लिए कहा जाए जिससे उसके शरीर, मन, प्रतिष्ठा या संपत्ति को नुकसान हो। धोखाधड़ी व्यक्तिगत, व्यावसायिक और वाणिज्यिक लेनदेन में विश्वास के मूल ढांचे को कमजोर करती है।

## Definition
**English:** Section 318 of BNS defines cheating as whoever, by deceiving any person, fraudulently or dishonestly induces the person so deceived to deliver any property to any person, or to consent that any person shall retain any property, or intentionally induces the person so deceived to do or omit to do anything which he would not do or omit if he were not so deceived, and which act or omission causes or is likely to cause damage or harm to that person in body, mind, reputation or property, is said to "cheat". 
**Hindi Unicode:** BNS की धारा 318 धोखाधड़ी को परिभाषित करती है: जो कोई भी किसी व्यक्ति को धोखा देकर, कपटपूर्वक या बेईमानी से उस व्यक्ति को किसी अन्य व्यक्ति को कोई संपत्ति सौंपने, या यह सहमति देने के लिए प्रेरित करता है कि कोई व्यक्ति किसी संपत्ति को अपने पास रखे, या जानबूझकर उस व्यक्ति को ऐसा कुछ करने या न करने के लिए प्रेरित करता है जो वह नहीं करता यदि उसे धोखा न दिया गया होता, और जिसके कारण उस व्यक्ति के शरीर, मन, प्रतिष्ठा या संपत्ति को नुकसान होने की संभावना है, उसे "धोखाधड़ी" (cheat) कहा जाता है।
**Hinglish:** BNS Section 318 cheating ko define karta hai: Jo koi bhi kisi insaan ko dhokha dekar, fraudulently ya beimaani se property kisi ko dene par majboor karta hai, ya koi aisi cheez karne ko kehta hai jo wo normally na karta agar use dhokha na diya gaya hota, aur us action se us insaan ke body, mind, izzat ya property ko nuksan hota hai, to use "cheat" karna kehte hain.

## Legal Background
**History:** Under the colonial-era Indian Penal Code (IPC) 1860, cheating was codified mainly under Sections 415 to 420. The term "420" became culturally synonymous with a fraudster in India. 
**Purpose:** With the introduction of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023, the provisions have been consolidated, modernized, and reorganized to handle both traditional cheating and modern electronic/cyber frauds effectively.
**Applicable Acts:** 
- Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023 (Substantive Law)
- Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023 (Procedural Law)
- Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) 2023 (Evidentiary provisions, especially for electronic records)
- Information Technology (IT) Act, 2000 (Applicable in cases of cyber cheating/phishing)
**Relevant Sections:**
- **BNS Section 318(1):** Definition of cheating.
- **BNS Section 318(2):** Punishment for simple cheating (Up to 3 years imprisonment, or fine, or both).
- **BNS Section 318(3):** Cheating with knowledge that wrongful loss may ensue to a person whose interest the offender is bound to protect (Up to 5 years imprisonment, or fine, or both).
- **BNS Section 318(4):** Cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property (Up to 7 years imprisonment and fine) - Equivalent to old IPC 420.
- **BNS Section 319:** Cheating by personation (pretending to be someone else).

## Applicability
**Who is covered:** All individuals, corporate entities, companies, association of persons, or agents operating within the territory of India. It also extends to Indian citizens committing cheating offenses outside India, or any person targeting computer resources in India.
**Who is exempt:** No civilian is exempt. Minor exceptions apply for children under a certain age lacking criminal intent (doli incapax) and persons of unsound mind at the time of the act.
**Special Conditions:** For an act to be classified as cheating, "dishonest intention" (mens rea) must be present at the *very beginning* of the transaction. A mere failure to keep a promise or breach of contract is a civil wrong, not criminal cheating, unless deception existed from day one.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure
**English:**
1. **Preserve Evidence:** Do not delete chats, call logs, emails, or transaction IDs. Take screenshots.
2. **Draft a Complaint:** Write a chronological application detailing the fraud, the persons involved, and the amount/property lost.
3. **File Police Complaint/FIR:** Submit the complaint to the local Police Station or Cyber Cell. Under BNSS, if it's a cognizable offense (like aggravated cheating), the police must register an FIR.
4. **Investigation:** Police gather evidence, track IP addresses or bank trails, and may arrest the accused based on evidence.
5. **Charge-sheet:** Police file a charge-sheet (Challan) in court under BNSS provisions once investigation is complete.
6. **Trial:** The court examines witnesses, scrutinizes electronic records under BSA guidelines, and pronounces the verdict.
7. **Recovery/Restitution:** The court may order the attached properties of the accused to be sold to compensate the victim.

## Required Documents
- Written complaint detailing the chronology of events.
- Bank account statements showing the fraudulent transaction.
- Copies of forged documents (if any).
- Screenshots of WhatsApp/Email/SMS communications.
- Call recordings or voice notes.
- Identity proof of the complainant (Aadhaar, PAN).
- Details of the accused (if known) - phone numbers, bank accounts, social media profiles.

## Government Authorities
- **Local Police Station:** First point of contact for registering an FIR.
- **Cyber Crime Cell / Portal (cybercrime.gov.in):** For all internet, UPI, and telephonic scams.
- **Magistrate Courts (JMFC/CMM):** To hear cases, grant bail/remand, and conduct trials.
- **Economic Offences Wing (EOW):** Specialized branch investigating multi-crore financial frauds and corporate cheating.
- **Enforcement Directorate (ED):** Steps in if the cheating involves massive money laundering (under PMLA).

## Penalties & Punishments
1. **Simple Cheating (BNS 318(2)):** Up to 3 years imprisonment, or fine, or both.
2. **Cheating where offender is bound to protect interest (BNS 318(3)):** Up to 5 years imprisonment, or fine, or both. (E.g., Bank manager cheating a customer).
3. **Cheating & Dishonestly Inducing Delivery of Property (BNS 318(4)):** Up to 7 years imprisonment AND fine. (The most severe form, equivalent to old 420 IPC).
4. **Cheating by Personation (BNS 319):** Up to 5 years imprisonment, or fine, or both.

## Exceptions
- **Breach of Contract:** Merely failing to pay back a loan or failing to deliver goods on time is a civil breach, NOT cheating, if there was no initial intention to deceive.
- **Honest Mistakes:** Accounting errors or genuine business losses where there was no malicious intent (mens rea).
- **Mutual Consent Risks:** Entering high-risk investments where terms clearly stated the risks, and no false promises were explicitly made.

## Important Timelines
- **Reporting:** Immediate reporting is crucial. Cyber frauds should be reported within the "Golden Hour" (first 24-48 hours) by calling 1930 to freeze the fraudulent bank accounts.
- **Limitation Period:** For offenses punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment, the limitation period to take cognizance is 3 years from the date of the offense or the date it became known. For offenses punishable with more than 3 years (like BNS 318(4)), there is no strict limitation period, but unreasonable delay must be explained.
- **Bail Hearings:** Usually happen within a few days of arrest, varying based on case severity and recovery of assets.

## Common Legal Mistakes
1. **Delay in Reporting:** Waiting weeks to report, allowing scammers to withdraw money and destroy evidence.
2. **Filing Civil Suit instead of Criminal:** Treating deliberate fraud as a mere business dispute, or vice-versa.
3. **Deleting Chats in Anger:** Deleting communication logs out of frustration, destroying crucial digital evidence.
4. **Settling without Court Order:** Taking partial money from the fraudster without a formalized legal settlement, which compromises the case.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is the new section for 420 IPC in BNS?**
   **Answer:** Under the new Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023, the offense of cheating, previously known under Section 420 of the IPC, is now covered under Section 318(4). 
2. **Is cheating a bailable or non-bailable offense?**
   **Answer:** Simple cheating (3 years) is generally bailable, but aggravated cheating under Section 318(4) (up to 7 years) is a cognizable and non-bailable offense, meaning bail is at the court's discretion.
3. **Can police refuse to file an FIR for cheating?**
   **Answer:** For cognizable offenses like BNS 318(4), the police are legally bound to register an FIR under BNSS. If they refuse, you can approach the Superintendent of Police or file a complaint directly to the Magistrate.
4. **What is the difference between cheating and breach of trust?**
   **Answer:** In cheating, the deceit is present from the very beginning to obtain the property. In Criminal Breach of Trust, the property is entrusted legally, but the person later misappropriates it with dishonest intent.
5. **My friend took a loan and is not returning it. Is it cheating?**
   **Answer:** Not necessarily. If he intended to return it but suffered losses, it is a civil dispute. If he gave fake documents or never intended to repay from day one, it becomes cheating.
6. **How do I prove intention to cheat?**
   **Answer:** Intention (mens rea) is proved by circumstantial evidence, such as submitting fake documents, absconding, siphoning off funds immediately, or a history of similar frauds.
7. **Is a bounced cheque considered cheating?**
   **Answer:** Cheque bounce is primarily dealt with under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act. However, if it was given with a clear intent to deceive from the start, a cheating case can also be filed alongside.
8. **Can an online matrimonial fraud be booked under BNS 318?**
   **Answer:** Yes. Creating fake profiles, pretending to be wealthy, and extracting money under the pretext of marriage or emergencies is a classic case of cheating by personation and dishonestly inducing delivery of property.
9. **I was cheated out of Rs. 100 on an app. Can I file a police case?**
   **Answer:** Yes, the law does not specify a minimum amount for cheating. However, practically, for small amounts, using the National Cyber Crime portal (1930) is the fastest and most efficient route.
10. **What is 'Cheating by Personation'?**
    **Answer:** It means cheating by pretending to be some other person, or knowingly substituting one person for another. Covered under BNS Section 319, it carries a punishment of up to 5 years.
11. **Do I need original documents to file a cheating FIR?**
    **Answer:** No. Photocopies, digital copies, and bank statements are sufficient to register the FIR. The police will seize the original documents during their investigation.
12. **Can a company be sued for cheating?**
    **Answer:** Yes, a corporate entity can be booked for cheating. The directors, managers, and authorized signatories responsible for the deceptive acts will face criminal proceedings.
13. **Is compromise possible in a cheating case?**
    **Answer:** Yes. Many cheating cases are compoundable with the permission of the court. If the accused returns the money and the victim agrees, the case can be settled and quashed.
14. **What is the role of the Economic Offences Wing (EOW)?**
    **Answer:** EOW handles complex, multi-crore, and high-stakes white-collar crimes. Usually, local police transfer cases exceeding a specific financial threshold (e.g., above 3-5 Crores depending on the state) to the EOW.
15. **Can my bank reverse a fraudulent UPI transaction?**
    **Answer:** If reported immediately to 1930 and your bank within the "Golden Hour", authorities can freeze the scammer's account and the bank can initiate a chargeback or lien.
16. **Is selling a defective product considered cheating?**
    **Answer:** If the seller genuinely didn't know it was defective, it falls under Consumer Protection Law. If the seller knowingly packaged a fake/broken product as genuine, it constitutes cheating.
17. **What is the punishment for cheating?**
    **Answer:** Depending on the subsection of BNS 318, punishment ranges from 3 years for simple cheating to 7 years for dishonestly inducing delivery of property, plus mandatory fines.
18. **Can I file a cheating case if there was no written contract?**
    **Answer:** Yes. Oral agreements, WhatsApp chats, email correspondence, and bank transfers can act as substantial evidence to prove cheating, even without a formal written contract.
19. **What is 'Zero FIR' in cheating cases?**
    **Answer:** A Zero FIR allows a victim to file a police complaint at any police station, irrespective of where the crime occurred. It is then transferred to the police station with the correct jurisdiction.
20. **Can I get my money back during the trial?**
    **Answer:** Yes. The court has powers under BNSS to order interim release of seized funds to the victim or order compensation from the fines imposed on the accused after conviction.

## Common Myths vs Facts
1. **Myth:** Cheating means marital infidelity. 
   **Fact:** In legal terms under BNS, cheating strictly refers to financial, property, or document-related fraud and deception, not adultery or relationship infidelity.
2. **Myth:** A bounced cheque is an automatic 420 (BNS 318) case.
   **Fact:** Cheque bounce falls under Section 138 of the NI Act. BNS 318 is only applied if initial fraudulent intent is explicitly proven.
3. **Myth:** You cannot file a case without a written agreement.
   **Fact:** Bank transactions, witness testimonies, and digital logs like WhatsApp chats are perfectly valid to prove deception.
4. **Myth:** Any failure to repay a loan is cheating.
   **Fact:** Mere inability to repay due to financial hardship is a civil default. Fraudulent intent at the time of borrowing is required for it to be a crime.
5. **Myth:** Police will help recover the money immediately.
   **Fact:** Police can freeze accounts and arrest the accused, but the final order to return funds must come from a Magistrate or Court.
6. **Myth:** Cyber frauds cannot be traced.
   **Fact:** Cyber cells use advanced digital forensics, IP tracking, and banking nodal officers to trace the money trail across multiple wallets and accounts.
7. **Myth:** BNS 318 is bailable, so the fraudster won't go to jail.
   **Fact:** BNS 318(4) (inducing delivery of property) is non-bailable. The accused can be sent to judicial custody for weeks or months.
8. **Myth:** You must file the FIR where the fraudster lives.
   **Fact:** You can file the complaint where you reside, where the money was sent from, or via a Zero FIR anywhere in India.
9. **Myth:** Cheating cases run for decades with no result.
   **Fact:** The new BNSS guidelines have established stricter timelines for trials, charge-sheet filings, and fast-track procedures for electronic evidence.
10. **Myth:** Only poor uneducated people get cheated.
    **Fact:** Highly educated professionals, corporations, and banks regularly fall victim to sophisticated cyber scams and corporate frauds.

*Assistant:* Yeh BNS Section 318(4) (purana IPC 420) ke tehat dhokhadhadi ka ek serious mamla hai. Aap turant apne bank statements, fake papers aur us aadmi ki details lekar nazdiki police station ya EOW mein FIR darj karayein.

**Example 2:**
*Citizen:* I received an SMS saying my electricity will be cut off, I clicked the link, and Rs. 50,000 was deducted! 
*Assistant:* This is a cyber-cheating case. Immediately call 1930 (National Cyber Crime Helpline) to freeze the transaction. After that, register a formal complaint on cybercrime.gov.in with the SMS screenshot and bank statement.

**Example 3:**
*Citizen:* Mere dost ne business ke naam par paise liye aur ab loss ka bahaana bana raha hai. Kya ye cheating hai?
*Assistant:* Agar usne shuru se hi aapko dhokha dene ke irade se paise liye the aur business fake tha, tab ye BNS 318 ki cheating hai. Par agar business sach mein tha aur loss hua hai, toh ye civil dispute (paise ki len-den) ka matter hoga.

**Example 4:**
*Citizen:* A company promised me a job abroad, took my passport and fees, and disappeared. What do I do?
*Assistant:* This falls under cheating and forgery. File a police complaint under BNS Section 318(4) and report the loss of your passport immediately so it cannot be misused. 

**Example 5:**
*Citizen:* Kisi ne mera Facebook profile hack karke mere doston se paise maange. (Someone hacked my Facebook and asked my friends for money.)
*Assistant:* Yeh BNS Section 319 (Cheating by personation) aur IT Act ke tehat cyber fraud hai. Apne doston ko turant alert karein aur cybercrime portal par complain file karein.

**Example 6:**
*Citizen:* Can I withdraw a cheating case if the accused returns my money?
*Assistant:* Yes. Cheating under BNS Section 318 is a compoundable offense with the permission of the Court. If you reach a settlement and recover your assets, you can file a plea to quash the FIR.

**Example 7:**
*Citizen:* Maine ek car kharidi, par seller ne meter peeche kar diya tha. (I bought a car, but the seller rolled back the odometer.)
*Assistant:* Yeh bilkul dhokhadhadi (cheating) hai kyunki usne jaan-बूझकर facts chupaye aur aapko nuksan pahunchaya. Aap Consumer Court me bhi ja sakte hain aur Police me BNS 318 ki complaint bhi de sakte hain.

**Example 8:**
*Citizen:* Do I need a lawyer to file a cheating FIR?
*Assistant:* No, you do not need a lawyer to simply file a police complaint or an FIR. You only need to provide a clear written application with your evidence. However, a lawyer is helpful during the court trial phase.

**Example 9:**
*Citizen:* My accountant siphoned money from my company over three years. Is this Section 318?
*Assistant:* This usually attracts both Cheating (BNS 318) and Criminal Breach of Trust by a clerk or servant (BNS 316), as the accountant abused the trust placed in them. 

**Example 10:**
*Citizen:* Mujhe police station walo ne kaha ki online fraud ke liye yahan FIR nahi likhi jayegi.
*Assistant:* Police Zero FIR likhne se mana nahi kar sakti. Agar wo mana karte hain, toh aap unke Senior (SP/DCP) ko online ya speed post ke zariye apni shikayat bhej sakte hain.

**Example 11:**
*Citizen:* Are WhatsApp chats valid evidence in a cheating case?
*Assistant:* Yes. Under the new Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) 2023, electronic records like WhatsApp chats are fully admissible as primary evidence, provided you give the required Section 63 certificate.

**Example 12:**
*Citizen:* Ek matrimonial site par kisi ne khud ko NRI doctor bata kar mujhse paise ainth liye. (Someone on a matrimonial site posed as an NRI doctor and extorted money.)
*Assistant:* Yeh cheating by personation (BNS 319) aur BNS 318(4) ka case hai. Iski shikyat Cyber Crime Cell me karein. Us fake profile ki URL, chat history, aur bank transfer receipt as proof use hogi.

**Example 13:**
*Citizen:* If the fraudster lives in Dubai, can the Indian police do anything?
*Assistant:* Yes. If the crime targets an Indian citizen or Indian computer system, Indian police have jurisdiction. They can issue Look Out Circulars (LOC) or work with Interpol (Red Corner Notice) to extradite the accused.

**Example 14:**
*Citizen:* Mera case court me 5 saal se chal raha hai cheating ka, accused hazir nahi ho raha.
*Assistant:* Aap apne lawyer ke zariye court me Non-Bailable Warrant (NBW) issue karne aur uski property attach (kurk) karne ke liye BNSS section 84 ke tehat application laga sakte hain.

**Example 15:**
*Citizen:* I transferred money by mistake to a wrong account. Is the receiver cheating me if they don't return it?
*Assistant:* The initial mistake is yours, so it's not cheating upfront. However, if they refuse to return it after being informed by the bank, it becomes criminal misappropriation of property.
