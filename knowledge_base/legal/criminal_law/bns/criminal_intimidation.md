# Criminal Intimidation under Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)

## Overview

**English**: Criminal intimidation involves intentionally threatening another person with injury to their person, reputation, or property, with the intent to cause alarm or to force them to do something they are not legally bound to do, or to omit doing something they are legally entitled to do. Under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023, this offense is treated with strict seriousness to ensure the psychological and physical safety of citizens.

## Definition

**English**: Section 351 of BNS defines criminal intimidation as threatening another with any injury to their person, reputation, or property, or to the person or reputation of anyone in whom that person is interested, with the intent to cause alarm to that person, or to cause that person to do any act which they are not legally bound to do.

## Legal Background

- **History & Purpose:** Historically governed by Sections 503 and 506 of the Indian Penal Code (IPC), 1860. The primary purpose is to criminalize the act of threatening, which assaults a person's peace of mind, even if no physical harm is immediately inflicted. 
- **Applicable Acts:** Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023; Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023; Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023.
- **Relevant Constitutional Articles:** Article 21 (Protection of Life and Personal Liberty) - Ensures a life free from fear and coercion.
- **Relevant Sections in BNS:**
  - **Section 351(1) BNS:** Defines the core elements of Criminal Intimidation.
  - **Section 351(2) BNS:** Prescribes the general punishment (imprisonment up to 2 years, fine, or both).
  - **Section 351(3) BNS:** Deals with severe threats (death, grievous hurt, destruction of property by fire, offenses punishable with death or life imprisonment, or imputing unchastity to a woman). The punishment here is up to 7 years imprisonment, or fine, or both.
  - **Section 351(4) BNS:** Threat by anonymous communication or by concealing identity (punishment up to 2 years in addition to the punishment for the offense).

## Applicability

- **Who is covered:** All individuals residing within the territory of India, regardless of citizenship, who issue or receive a criminal threat.
- **Who is exempt:** No civilian is exempt. Law enforcement officers performing their duty (e.g., warning a suspect of legal consequences) are exempt, provided the threat is of lawful action.
- **Special Conditions:** The threat must be intended to cause alarm. A mere outburst of temper without the actual intention to execute the threat or cause alarm may sometimes not qualify, depending on judicial interpretation.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure

**English**:
1. **Preserve Evidence:** Save audio, video, text messages, or get contact details of eyewitnesses.
2. **File an NCR / FIR:** Visit the local police station. For general intimidation, the police will file a Non-Cognizable Report (NCR) and direct you to the Magistrate. For threats of death/grievous hurt, they must file an FIR.
3. **Approach the Magistrate:** If an NCR is filed, you must file a private complaint before the Judicial Magistrate under the relevant BNSS provisions.
4. **Investigation:** The Magistrate orders an investigation or summons the accused based on the evidence presented.
5. **Trial & Sentencing:** The trial proceeds in court. If proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt, the accused is sentenced under BNS Section 351.

## Required Documents
- Identity Proof of the Complainant (Aadhaar, Voter ID, Passport).
- Written Complaint detailing the exact words used in the threat, time, date, and location.
- Evidence of Intimidation: Screenshots of WhatsApp/SMS, Call Recordings, Emails, CCTV footage, audio clips.
- List of Witnesses (if any) and their contact information.
- Medical Reports (if the intimidation was accompanied by physical assault, even minor).

## Government Authorities
- **Local Police Station:** The first point of contact for filing complaints.
- **Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in):** If the intimidation is done online (social media, email).
- **Judicial Magistrate Court:** For filing private complaints if the police do not file an FIR.
- **National/State Women’s Commission:** If the threat is directed at a woman, specifically related to her modesty or safety.

## Penalties & Punishments
Under Section 351 of BNS:
- **General Criminal Intimidation (Section 351(2) BNS):** Imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to 2 years, or with fine, or with both.
- **Aggravated Criminal Intimidation (Section 351(3) BNS):** If the threat is to cause death or grievous hurt, or destruction of property by fire, or to commit an offense punishable with death or life imprisonment, or to impute unchastity to a woman, the punishment is imprisonment up to 7 years, or fine, or both.
- **Anonymous Threat (Section 351(4) BNS):** An additional punishment extending up to 2 years.

## Exceptions
- **Empty Threats in a Fit of Anger:** If a person says something in extreme anger with absolutely no intent to follow through or cause alarm, and the victim knows it's a hollow threat, courts sometimes do not classify it as criminal intimidation.
- **Lawful Threats:** Threatening someone with a lawsuit (e.g., "I will take you to court if you don't pay my debt") is the exercise of a legal right and does NOT constitute criminal intimidation.

## Important Timelines
- **Limitation Period:** For general intimidation (punishable up to 2 years), a complaint must generally be filed within 3 years of the incident under BNSS. For severe threats (punishable up to 7 years), there is theoretically no strict limitation period, but unreasonable delay can weaken the case.
- **Police Action:** The police are expected to register an FIR immediately for aggravated threats. For NCRs, the complainant should approach the court without delay (preferably within weeks).

## Common Legal Mistakes
- **Deleting the evidence:** Out of fear, victims often delete the threatening messages. This destroys the case.
- **Delaying the complaint:** Waiting too long to report makes the court suspicious of the motive behind the complaint.
- **Exaggerating the facts:** Adding false claims of assault when only a verbal threat occurred. This damages the complainant's credibility.
- **Retaliation:** Threatening the accused back, which makes it a cross-case and weakens the victim's position.

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **What is the BNS Section for Criminal Intimidation?**
   It is covered under Section 351 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023. This replaces the old IPC Sections 503 and 506.
2. **Is criminal intimidation a bailable offence?**
   Under general circumstances (Section 351(2) BNS), it is a bailable and non-cognizable offence. However, in states where amendments were made to the old IPC 506, or under severe threats, it may be treated differently by local police protocols.
3. **Can I go to jail for threatening to sue someone?**
   No. Threatening to take lawful legal action, like filing a lawsuit for unpaid dues, is a legal right, not criminal intimidation.
4. **What if the threat is made anonymously?**
   Section 351(4) BNS covers anonymous threats. It adds up to 2 years of extra punishment because concealing identity increases the alarm and malicious intent.
5. **My neighbor threatened to kill my dog. Is this criminal intimidation?**
   Yes. The definition includes threats to injure the property of a person. Pets are considered property under the law, and threatening to harm them to cause alarm is an offence.
6. **Will the police file an FIR for a simple verbal threat?**
   Typically, for a simple threat without a weapon or severe intent, the police will register a Non-Cognizable Report (NCR). You will need to take the NCR to a Magistrate to start proceedings.
7. **What happens if someone threatens me on Facebook or Instagram?**
   Online threats are fully covered. You can file a complaint with the cyber crime cell or online at cybercrime.gov.in. The electronic record will serve as primary evidence.
8. **What is the maximum punishment for threatening to kill someone?**
   Threatening to cause death or grievous hurt falls under aggravated criminal intimidation (Section 351(3) BNS), carrying a maximum punishment of 7 years in prison.
9. **How do I prove a verbal threat if there are no witnesses?**
   It is challenging. You would rely on circumstantial evidence, past history of hostility, immediate reporting to the police (which shows your state of alarm), and any CCTV footage that shows aggressive body language.
10. **Does a threat have to be made directly to me?**
    No. The law covers threats made to someone you are interested in (e.g., your child, spouse, or friend) to cause you alarm.
11. **Can a fake online profile be traced if they send threats?**
    Yes. Cyber crime authorities can trace IP addresses, device IDs, and ISP logs to identify the person behind a fake profile.
12. **Is black magic a form of criminal intimidation?**
    If a person threatens to perform "black magic" or "divine displeasure" to cause alarm or extort something, it falls under specific legal provisions regarding inducing belief of divine displeasure.
13. **What if the threat was just a joke?**
    For it to be a crime, there must be an intent to cause alarm. If it was clearly a joke understood by both parties, it is not intimidation. However, courts evaluate the context strictly.
14. **How long does a criminal intimidation case take in court?**
    Depending on the court's backlog, a trial could take anywhere from 1 to 5 years. Gathering strong evidence early speeds up the process.
15. **Can a woman claim a harsher punishment for threats?**
    Yes. Under Section 351(3) BNS, if the threat is to impute unchastity to a woman (threatening to ruin her reputation regarding her character), the punishment extends up to 7 years.
16. **Is a threat letter admissible in court?**
    Absolutely. A physical letter, an email, or a WhatsApp message are all admissible pieces of evidence under the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA).
17. **What should I do if the police refuse to register an FIR for a death threat?**
    You can send your complaint in writing via registered post to the Superintendent of Police (SP) or Commissioner. If they still don't act, you can file a complaint directly before a Magistrate under BNSS.
18. **Can the complaint be withdrawn later?**
    General criminal intimidation is a compoundable offence, meaning the victim and the accused can reach a compromise outside court, and the case can be withdrawn with the court's permission.
19. **If I owe someone money and they threaten to break my legs, what do I do?**
    Owing money does not give anyone the right to threaten physical violence. You must report the threat to the police immediately.
20. **Does the threat have to be executed to be a crime?**
    No. The mere act of making the threat with the intent to cause alarm completes the offence of criminal intimidation. The threat does not need to be carried out.

## Common Myths vs Facts

1. **Myth:** A threat is only illegal if they show a weapon.
   **Fact:** Words alone, written or spoken, can constitute criminal intimidation if the intent is to cause alarm.
2. **Myth:** You can't be punished if you delete the threatening WhatsApp message.
   **Fact:** Deleted messages can often be retrieved through cyber forensics or from the recipient's phone/cloud backups.
3. **Myth:** Threatening to file a police complaint is blackmail.
   **Fact:** Threatening to take lawful legal action is not criminal intimidation or blackmail.
4. **Myth:** The police will always arrest someone immediately for a threat.
   **Fact:** For general threats (punishable up to 2 years), an arrest without a warrant is generally not permissible as it is a non-cognizable offence.
5. **Myth:** Anonymous callers cannot be caught.
   **Fact:** Telecom providers and cyber police can trace anonymous or private numbers easily.
6. **Myth:** If I hit someone who threatened me, it is self-defense.
   **Fact:** Pre-emptive physical violence against a mere verbal threat is generally not protected under self-defense unless you face imminent physical attack.
7. **Myth:** You need at least two witnesses to prove a threat.
   **Fact:** Even audio recordings or text messages are sufficient evidence; eyewitnesses are helpful but not strictly mandatory if digital evidence exists.
8. **Myth:** Threatening a person's reputation is not a crime; only physical threats are.
   **Fact:** Section 351 BNS explicitly includes threats to a person's reputation.
9. **Myth:** It's not a threat if I didn't mean it.
   **Fact:** If the victim felt alarmed and the context shows malicious intent, the law may still consider it an offence regardless of your hidden internal intentions.
10. **Myth:** Only the person threatened can file a case.
    **Fact:** If the threat is to harm a relative or friend of a person to cause that person alarm, that person can file the complaint.

## Government Services
- **eCourts Services (https://ecourts.gov.in/):** To track the status of your case once it goes to trial.
- **National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (https://cybercrime.gov.in/):** For filing complaints regarding threats received online, via email, or social media.
- **Police Emergency Number (112):** Pan-India single emergency number for immediate physical threats.
- **NALSA (National Legal Services Authority):** Provides free legal aid to marginalized citizens facing threats who cannot afford a lawyer.
