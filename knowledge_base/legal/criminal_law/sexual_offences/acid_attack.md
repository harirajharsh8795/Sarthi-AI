# Acid Attack Laws and Procedures in India (BNS Section 124)

## Overview
**English:** An acid attack is a horrific crime involving the throwing of acid or a similar corrosive substance onto someone's body with the intent to disfigure, maim, torture, or kill. Under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) of 2023, stringent provisions are laid down to punish the perpetrators and provide robust compensation and immediate medical care rights for the survivors. The law recognizes the immense physical, psychological, and social trauma inflicted by such acts and mandates a zero-tolerance approach.

## Quick Summary
**English:** Acid attacks are classified as severe, non-bailable, and cognizable offences. Section 124 of the BNS deals specifically with voluntarily causing grievous hurt by use of acid, replacing the older IPC Section 326A. The minimum punishment is 10 years of rigorous imprisonment, which may extend to life imprisonment, along with a heavy fine that must directly go to the victim for medical expenses. Attempted acid attacks are covered under Section 124(2) of the BNS.

## Definition
**English:** Under BNS, "acid" includes any substance which has acidic or corrosive character or burning nature, capable of causing bodily injury leading to scars or disfigurement or temporary/permanent disability. An "acid attack" involves voluntarily causing permanent or partial damage, deformity, burns, maiming, disfigurement, or disability to any part of the body of a person by administering acid or by using any other means.

## Legal Background
**History:** Before 2013, there were no specific sections in the IPC for acid attacks; they were treated merely as grievous hurt under IPC 326. Following the landmark Nirbhaya case and the Criminal Law (Amendment) Act 2013, Sections 326A and 326B were inserted into the IPC. Furthermore, in the *Laxmi v. Union of India* case, the Supreme Court banned the over-the-counter unregulated sale of acid and mandated free medical treatment.
**Applicable Acts:** Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023, Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023, The Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016.
**Relevant Constitutional Articles:** Article 21 (Right to Life and Personal Liberty).
**Relevant Sections:**
- **BNS Section 124(1):** Voluntarily causing grievous hurt by use of acid (Minimum 10 years to Life Imprisonment).
- **BNS Section 124(2):** Voluntarily throwing or attempting to throw acid (Minimum 5 years to 7 years).
- **BNSS Section 396:** Victim Compensation Scheme (Minimum 3 Lakhs as per NALSA/SC guidelines).
- **BNS Section 115:** (If death occurs, it is treated as Murder).

## Applicability
**Who is covered:** Any individual (regardless of gender) who is a victim of an acid attack or attempted acid attack. Although historically most victims have been women, the law is gender-neutral regarding the victim.
**Who is exempt:** No exemptions. Minors committing the act will be tried under the Juvenile Justice Act, but depending on age (16-18) and heinous nature, they can be tried as adults.
**Special conditions:** The fine imposed on the perpetrator must be just and reasonable to meet the medical expenses of the treatment of the victim and shall be paid directly to the victim.

## Rights of Citizens
**English:**
1. Right to Free Medical Treatment: Every hospital (public or private) is legally bound to provide free first aid and medical treatment.
2. Right to Compensation: Guaranteed minimum compensation of ₹3,00,000 from the State Government, with an upfront payment of ₹1,00,000 within 15 days of the incident.
3. Right to Privacy: The identity of the victim should not be disclosed in public media without consent.
4. Disability Rights: Acid attack survivors are recognized as Persons with Disabilities under the 2016 Act, entitling them to reservations and special schemes.

## Duties & Responsibilities
**English:**
- **Citizens:** Must immediately call an ambulance and the police. Provide first aid by washing the affected area with copious amounts of clean, running water (not neutralizing agents unless medically trained).
- **Hospitals:** Must not wait for an FIR or police clearance before starting life-saving treatment. Must issue a medical certificate free of cost to the victim.
- **Police:** Must register a Zero FIR immediately, irrespective of jurisdiction, and ensure the victim's statement is recorded safely, preferably by a female officer or a Magistrate.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure
**English:**
1. **Immediate Medical Aid:** Rush the victim to the nearest hospital. The hospital must start treatment immediately.
2. **Police Intimation:** The hospital informs the police (Medico-Legal Case - MLC). Citizens can also dial 112.
3. **FIR Registration:** Police record the FIR under BNS Section 124(1) or 124(2). A Zero FIR must be filed if outside jurisdiction.
4. **Statement Recording:** The victim's statement is recorded under BNSS Section 183 by a Judicial Magistrate, ensuring evidentiary value.
5. **Investigation & Forensics:** Police seize the clothes, acid container, and collect chemical forensic evidence from the scene.
6. **Arrest & Charge Sheet:** The accused is arrested (non-bailable). Police must file the charge sheet within 60 to 90 days.
7. **Trial:** A fast-track court conducts the trial on a day-to-day basis.
8. **Compensation Claim:** The District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) processes the compensation claim concurrently with the trial.

1. Medico-Legal Certificate (MLC) provided by the hospital.
2. Copy of the FIR.
3. Photographs of injuries (taken by forensic experts or medical staff).
4. Victim's recorded statement.
5. Identity Proof for filing compensation claims (Aadhaar, Voter ID).

## Government Authorities
- **Police Department:** For immediate rescue, FIR, and criminal investigation.
- **District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) / NALSA:** For processing victim compensation and providing free legal aid.
- **Criminal Courts / Fast Track Courts:** For prosecution and trial of the accused.
- **State Health Department:** To monitor hospital compliance regarding free treatment.
- **Sub-Divisional Magistrate (SDM):** Regulates the sale of acid in the district.

## Penalties & Punishments
- **BNS Section 124(1) (Causing Grievous Hurt by Acid):** Rigorous imprisonment for not less than 10 years, which may extend to imprisonment for life, AND a fine.
- **BNS Section 124(2) (Attempt to throw Acid):** Imprisonment of either description for a term not less than 5 years, which may extend to 7 years, AND a fine.
- **Fine Guidelines:** The fine MUST directly cover the victim's medical expenses.
- **Non-compliance by Hospital (BNS Section 125/226):** If a hospital refuses free immediate treatment, the administration can be prosecuted and punished with imprisonment up to 1 year or a fine.

## Exceptions
There are strictly no exceptions or "provocation" defenses acceptable in cases of acid attacks. It is always considered a pre-meditated, brutal crime. Settlement or compromise between parties is generally not recognized to quash the FIR due to the heinous nature of the crime.

## Important Timelines
- **First-Aid:** Must be immediate (Golden Hour).
- **Interim Compensation:** ₹1 Lakh must be paid within 15 days of bringing the matter to the notice of DLSA.
- **Remaining Compensation:** Balance to be paid as expeditiously as possible, usually within 2 months.
- **Charge Sheet:** Must be filed by the police within 60-90 days.
- **Trial Completion:** Expected to be completed within 2 to 6 months in fast-track courts.

## Common Legal Mistakes
1. **Washing with wrong chemicals:** Public trying to neutralize acid with milk or other chemicals; always use only plain running water.
2. **Waiting for Police at the Hospital:** Doctors waiting for police arrival before starting treatment (this is illegal).
3. **Delay in FIR:** Family members prioritizing treatment without informing the police. Dial 112 while the victim is being treated.
4. **Throwing away evidence:** Discarding burnt clothes or the acid container; these are critical forensic evidence.

## Common Myths vs Facts

**Myth 1:** Acid attacks only happen to women.
**Fact:** While predominantly targeted at women, men and children are also victims of acid attacks. The law protects all victims equally regardless of gender.

**Myth 2:** You must wait for the police to arrive before treating an acid burn.
**Fact:** ABSOLUTELY FALSE. Medical treatment must begin immediately. The "Golden Hour" is critical. Hospitals cannot demand an FIR before treatment.

**Myth 3:** Throwing milk or baking soda neutralizes the acid best.
**Fact:** Attempting chemical neutralization without expert medical knowledge can cause severe exothermic (heat) reactions, worsening the burn. Always use ONLY copious amounts of clean running water.

**Myth 4:** Bail is easily available if the attacker belongs to a rich family.
**Fact:** Acid attack is a non-bailable and heinous offence. Courts uniformly deny bail to perpetrators, regardless of their background, due to Supreme Court precedents.

**Myth 5:** Only government hospitals offer free treatment.
**Fact:** The Supreme Court has mandated that BOTH public and private hospitals must provide free, immediate medical treatment to acid attack victims.

**Myth 6:** The maximum compensation is only 3 Lakhs.
**Fact:** 3 Lakhs is the MINIMUM compensation. Courts and DLSAs often grant much higher amounts (e.g., 7-10 Lakhs) depending on the severity of the injuries.

**Myth 7:** Compromise outside court can close the case.
**Fact:** Acid attacks are non-compoundable offences. You cannot settle the matter out of court, and the state will prosecute the attacker even if the victim is coerced to withdraw.

**Myth 8:** Attempting to throw acid but missing is not a crime.
**Fact:** Attempted acid attack is a distinct crime under BNS 124(2) and carries a severe punishment of 5 to 7 years imprisonment.

**Myth 9:** Acid is completely banned in India.
**Fact:** It is not entirely banned but highly regulated. It cannot be sold openly over the counter; buyers must provide IDs and sellers must maintain logs.

**Myth 10:** The victim's medical expenses must be borne by their family.
**Fact:** The initial treatment is entirely free. Furthermore, the court mandates that the fine imposed on the attacker must cover all ongoing medical expenses of the victim.
