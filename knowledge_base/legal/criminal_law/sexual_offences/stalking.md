# Stalking: Legal Rights, Procedures, and Protection under Indian Law

## Overview
**English:** Stalking is a serious criminal offense in India under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023. It involves unwanted, persistent following, contacting, or monitoring of a woman by a man, either physically or in cyberspace (cyberstalking), despite her clear disinterest. The law is designed to protect women's privacy, dignity, and physical safety, recognizing that unchecked stalking often escalates into more violent crimes.
**Hindi:** पीछा करना (Stalking) भारतीय न्याय संहिता (BNS), 2023 के तहत एक गंभीर आपराधिक अपराध है। इसमें किसी व्यक्ति (आमतौर पर एक पुरुष द्वारा महिला का) द्वारा उसकी स्पष्ट अनिच्छा के बावजूद शारीरिक रूप से या साइबर स्पेस में अनचाहा, लगातार पीछा करना, संपर्क करना या निगरानी करना शामिल है। यह कानून महिलाओं की निजता, गरिमा और शारीरिक सुरक्षा की रक्षा के लिए बनाया गया है।
**Hinglish:** Stalking ek serious criminal offense hai India mein Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023 ke under. Isme kisi aurat ka uski marzi ke khilaf baar-baar peecha karna, contact karna ya internet par uski activity monitor karna shamil hai. Yeh kanoon women ki privacy aur safety ko protect karne ke liye banaya gaya hai, kyunki online ya physical stalking aage chalkar dangerous crimes mein badal sakti hai.

Victims can report it via the National Cyber Crime portal, emergency number 112, or women's helpline 1091.

## Definition
**English:** As per Section 78 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023, stalking is defined as any man who follows a woman and contacts, or attempts to contact her to foster personal interaction repeatedly despite a clear indication of disinterest, OR monitors the use by a woman of the internet, email, or any other form of electronic communication.
**Hindi:** भारतीय न्याय संहिता (BNS), 2023 की धारा 78 के अनुसार, पीछा करने को इस प्रकार परिभाषित किया गया है कि कोई भी पुरुष जो किसी महिला का पीछा करता है और स्पष्ट अनिच्छा के बावजूद व्यक्तिगत बातचीत को बढ़ावा देने के लिए बार-बार संपर्क करता है या संपर्क करने का प्रयास करता है, या इंटरनेट, ईमेल या किसी अन्य इलेक्ट्रॉनिक संचार के उपयोग की निगरानी करता है।
**Hinglish:** BNS 2023 ke Section 78 ke hisaab se, stalking ka matlab hai koi bhi aadmi jo kisi aurat ka peecha karta hai aur uske mana karne ke baad bhi usse baar-baar contact karne ki koshish karta hai, YA internet, email, ya social media par uski activities par nazar rakhta hai (monitor karta hai).

## Why It Matters
**English:** Recognizing and reporting stalking early is crucial to prevent escalation to severe crimes like assault, acid attacks, or murder. It empowers victims to reclaim their privacy and ensures perpetrators face strict legal consequences before harm is done.

## Legal Background
The offense of stalking was originally introduced into the Indian Penal Code (IPC) as Section 354D following the Criminal Law (Amendment) Act, 2013 (often called the Nirbhaya Act). Under the new criminal framework implemented in 2024:
*   **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023 - Section 78:** Directly penalizes the act of stalking. It explicitly covers physical stalking and cyberstalking.
*   **Information Technology (IT) Act, 2000:** Sections 66E (violation of privacy), 67, and 67A (transmitting obscene material) may also apply concurrently if the stalking involves sending explicit images, morphing photos, or hacking into the victim's electronic devices.
*   **Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023:** Governs the procedural aspects, ensuring that victims have the right to record statements confidentially and safely.

## Applicability
*   **Who is covered:** The law specifically protects women. It explicitly uses the terminology "any man who... follows a woman". Therefore, under Section 78 BNS, the perpetrator is presumed to be male, and the victim is female.
*   **Who is exempt:** There are exemptions provided within the section (see Exceptions below).
*   **Special Conditions:** For online monitoring (cyberstalking), physical proximity is completely irrelevant. Tracking IP addresses, sending unsolicited messages from anonymous accounts, or using spyware constitutes an offense.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure
**English:**
1. **Preserve Evidence:** Document dates, times, screenshots, emails, or eyewitness accounts of the stalking incidents.
2. **First Response:** Inform trusted family members or friends. Call the emergency helpline 112 if in immediate physical danger.
3. **Register Complaint:** Visit the nearest police station or the Cyber Crime Cell (if cyberstalking) to file a written complaint. Alternatively, file online at cybercrime.gov.in.
4. **Filing the FIR:** The police will register an FIR under Section 78 of BNS. Insist on a copy of the FIR, which is free of cost.
5. **Statement Recording:** Under Section 183 of the BNSS (formerly 164 CrPC), your statement will be recorded before a Judicial Magistrate.
6. **Investigation & Charge Sheet:** Police will conduct an inquiry, confiscate electronic devices if needed, and file a charge sheet.
7. **Trial:** The matter proceeds to trial in a criminal court where evidence is presented, culminating in a verdict.

## Required Documents
*   A formal written complaint detailing the timeline of events.
*   Government-issued ID proof of the complainant (Aadhaar, Voter ID, Passport).
*   Printed screenshots of WhatsApp chats, text messages, emails, or social media DMs.
*   Call Detail Records (CDRs), audio recordings, or video recordings showing the stalker.
*   List of witnesses (if the stalking occurred in public spaces or workplace).

## Government Authorities
*   **Local Police / Mahila Thana (Women Police Stations):** For immediate physical reporting and FIR registration.
*   **Cyber Crime Cell:** Specialized units for tackling internet, email, and social media stalking.
*   **National Commission for Women (NCW):** Victims can directly approach the NCW online for intervention if local police fail to act.
*   **Judicial Magistrate Court:** For recording official statements and conducting the trial.

## Penalties & Punishments
Under Section 78 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023:
*   **First Conviction:** The stalker shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to **three years**, and shall also be liable to a fine. For the first offense, stalking is a **Bailable** and **Cognizable** offense.
*   **Second or Subsequent Conviction:** The stalker shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to **five years**, and shall also be liable to a fine. A subsequent offense is **Non-Bailable** and **Cognizable**.

*Note: In bailable offenses, bail is a matter of right at the police station itself. In non-bailable offenses, bail is at the discretion of the Court.*

## Exceptions
Section 78 BNS lists specific situations where following or monitoring a woman is **not** considered stalking. These include:
1.  **State Security & Law Enforcement:** Pursued for the purpose of preventing or detecting a crime, and the man accused was entrusted with the responsibility by the State (e.g., Police detectives, undercover agents).
2.  **Statutory Duty:** Pursued under any law or to comply with any condition or requirement imposed by a person under any law (e.g., a court-appointed bailiff).
3.  **Reasonable Conduct:** In particular circumstances, such conduct was reasonable and justified (e.g., a private investigator hired legally, though even this has strict privacy boundaries, or following someone to return a dropped wallet).

## Important Timelines
*   **Filing a Complaint:** There is no strict limitation period for heinous offenses against women, but filing the complaint as soon as possible is highly recommended to preserve digital footprints and CCTV footage.
*   **Zero FIR transfer:** If a Zero FIR is filed, the police must transfer it to the police station with actual jurisdiction immediately (usually within 24-48 hours).
*   **Investigation Completion:** Typically, police must complete the investigation and file the charge sheet within 60 to 90 days from the arrest of the accused, as per general criminal procedure.

## Common Legal Mistakes
*   **Deleting Evidence:** Blocking the stalker and deleting the chat history in anger/fear. (Correct action: Block, but save screenshots and logs first).
*   **Ignoring Early Signs:** Thinking "it's just a phase" or "he will stop". Stalking usually escalates.
*   **Confronting the Stalker:** Engaging with the stalker directly or through family members often gives the stalker the attention they seek and can lead to physical violence.
*   **Not Naming the Suspect:** Being afraid to name the stalker in the FIR due to societal pressure. A vague FIR severely weakens the legal case.
*   **Failing to Report to the Platform:** For cyberstalking, not reporting the abusive account to the social media platform (X, Instagram, Facebook) alongside the police complaint.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Is cyberstalking considered a real crime in India?**
Yes. Under Section 78 of the BNS, monitoring a woman's internet usage, emails, or social media activity is explicitly defined as stalking. The police take cyberstalking just as seriously as physical stalking.

**2. I don't know the real identity of the person stalking me online. Can I still file an FIR?**
Absolutely. You can file an FIR against an unknown person. The Cyber Crime Cell has the technical expertise to trace the IP address, device IDs, and ISP details to uncover the stalker's identity.

**3. Does the law protect men from being stalked by women?**
Under Section 78 of the BNS, stalking is gender-specific, addressing offenses committed by a "man" against a "woman". However, men facing harassment can seek legal recourse under other sections like criminal intimidation or defamation, though not specifically under the stalking statute.

**4. The stalker has only texted me twice. Is that enough to file a complaint?**
The law specifies that the contact must be done "repeatedly despite a clear indication of disinterest." If you have clearly told the person to stop and they contacted you again, it constitutes an offense. Two unwanted contacts after a clear "no" can be grounds for a complaint.

**5. What is a Zero FIR and how does it help in a stalking case?**
A Zero FIR allows you to file an FIR at any police station, irrespective of where the stalking occurred. This is especially helpful if you are traveling, shifted to a new city to escape the stalker, or if the stalker follows you across different states.

**6. The stalker is my ex-boyfriend. Can I still file a case?**
Yes. Previous romantic involvement does not give anyone the right to stalk, harass, or monitor you after the relationship has ended. A clear indication of disinterest is all that the law requires, regardless of past history.

**7. Can I request a female police officer to handle my case?**
Yes, it is your right. Under criminal procedural laws, offenses against women, including stalking, should ideally be recorded by a female police officer or any female officer.

**8. If I block the stalker, won't I lose all evidence?**
Before blocking the person, take screenshots of the entire chat history, including their profile picture and phone number or username. Once evidence is secured locally or on a cloud drive, proceed to block the stalker.

**9. Will my identity be revealed if I file a police complaint?**
In cases concerning offenses against women, the police and courts are obligated to maintain the confidentiality of the victim's identity. Media cannot publish your name without your written consent.

**10. What if the police refuse to register my FIR?**
If a police station refuses to register an FIR, you can send the substance of the complaint in writing by post to the Superintendent of Police (SP). You can also file a complaint directly with the Judicial Magistrate under BNSS, or approach the National Commission for Women (NCW).

**11. Does stalking only mean physically following someone?**
No. Stalking includes physical following, persistent phone calls, unwanted gifts, loitering near your home or workplace, and online monitoring (cyberstalking).

**12. Can a private detective be arrested for stalking?**
If a private detective's actions cross the line into harassment or violate a woman's privacy, they can be charged. The legal exemption only applies if the conduct was completely reasonable, justified, or authorized by the state.

**13. What if the stalker uses foreign phone numbers (like +1) to message me?**
Indian cyber cells coordinate with international agencies and telecom operators. Using virtual numbers or foreign numbers does not grant immunity; authorities can trace the root IP and arrest the offender.

**14. Is stalking a bailable offense?**
For the first conviction, stalking is a bailable offense, meaning the stalker can get bail from the police station. However, for a second or subsequent conviction, it becomes non-bailable, requiring a court hearing for bail.

**15. Can I withdraw a stalking complaint later?**
Since stalking is a cognizable criminal offense, withdrawing an FIR is a complex process. It usually requires permission from the High Court to quash the FIR. It is not as simple as taking back a statement.

**16. How does the court determine "clear indication of disinterest"?**
A verbal "No", ignored text messages, blocking the person, or a formally sent message asking them to cease contact all serve as evidence of a clear indication of disinterest in the court of law.

**17. Do I need to hire a private lawyer to fight a stalking case?**
Because stalking is a criminal offense, the State prosecutes the accused on your behalf through a Public Prosecutor. You do not strictly need to hire a private lawyer, though you can hire one to assist the prosecutor.

**18. How long does a stalking trial usually take?**
The timeframe varies significantly based on court pendency, ranging from a few months to a few years. Speedy trials are encouraged for crimes against women, but practical delays occur.

**19. What should I do if the stalker threatens me for filing a police complaint?**
Immediately inform the investigating officer or the police station where the FIR was filed. This constitutes a separate, severe offense of criminal intimidation, and the police can arrest the stalker and oppose their bail.

**20. Can I get a restraining order against the stalker?**
While Indian law doesn't have "restraining orders" exactly like the US or UK, the court can grant injunctions, and police can take bonds of good behavior under BNSS. You can also file under the Domestic Violence Act if the stalker is a relative or a live-in partner, which provides protection orders.

## Common Myths vs Facts

**Myth 1:** Stalking is romantic and shows true love, like in movies.
**Fact:** Stalking is a criminal act of aggression, obsession, and control. It induces fear and is punishable by up to 5 years in prison.

**Myth 2:** If you were friends before, it cannot be considered stalking.
**Fact:** Past friendship or relationship does not justify stalking. If a woman clearly says "no" to further contact, any persistent following is illegal.

**Myth 3:** Cyberstalking is harmless because there is no physical contact.
**Fact:** Cyberstalking is highly damaging, leading to severe mental trauma, reputational harm, and often escalates to physical threats. It is heavily penalized under BNS Section 78.

**Myth 4:** Only women can be victims of stalkers.
**Fact:** While Section 78 BNS specifically covers women as victims, men also face stalking. However, men must use other legal provisions (like harassment, criminal intimidation) to seek justice.

**Myth 5:** The police will not take it seriously if no physical harm was done.
**Fact:** Stalking laws are specifically designed as preventive measures. The police are mandated to register an FIR based purely on unwanted following or monitoring, without requiring physical harm.

**Myth 6:** Changing your phone number solves the problem.
**Fact:** While it may provide temporary relief, stalkers often find new ways to contact victims. Legal intervention is the only permanent solution.

**Myth 7:** You need a mountain of evidence to prove stalking.
**Fact:** Even your statement to a Magistrate (Section 183 BNSS) is a powerful piece of evidence. Screenshots, a few call logs, or testimonies from friends strongly support your case.

**Myth 8:** If a woman wears certain clothes or goes to clubs, she is "asking for it."
**Fact:** The victim's lifestyle, clothing, or habits have zero bearing on the legality of the crime. Stalking is solely the fault and criminal choice of the perpetrator.

**Myth 9:** Stalking is a non-cognizable offense, so police cannot arrest without a warrant.
**Fact:** Stalking is a Cognizable offense. The police have the full authority to arrest the accused without a warrant from a magistrate.

**Myth 10:** Stalkers only target strangers.
**Fact:** Statistical data shows that the vast majority of stalkers are known to the victim—often ex-partners, acquaintances, colleagues, or neighbors.

**Example 1: Recognizing the Crime**
*Citizen:* Ek ladka pichle ek hafte se mere college ke bahar khada rehta hai aur mujhe ghoorta hai. Usne mujhe roka nahi hai. Kya yeh stalking hai?
*Saarthi:* Haan, agar woh aapka peecha karta hai aur aapki marzi ke khilaf aap par nazar rakh raha hai, toh yeh BNS Section 78 ke tehat stalking hai. Aap police ko 112 par call kar sakti hain ya nazdiki police station mein complain de sakti hain.

**Example 2: Cyberstalking via Fake Accounts**
*Citizen:* Someone is making fake Instagram accounts to send me abusive messages after I blocked their main account. What should I do?
*Saarthi:* This is a clear case of cyberstalking. Do not delete the messages. Take screenshots of the chats and the profile URLs. Report this immediately on the National Cyber Crime portal (cybercrime.gov.in) or call 1930. The Cyber Cell can trace the IP address of the creator.

**Example 3: Ex-Partner Stalking**
*Citizen:* Mera ex-boyfriend mujhe baar baar call karta hai aur dhamki deta hai. Main usse mana kar chuki hu. Kya main case kar sakti hu?
*Saarthi:* Bilkul. Past relationship stalk karne ka koi right nahi deti. Agar aapne clear "No" bol diya hai, aur woh phir bhi contact kar raha hai, toh yeh legal offense hai. Aap apne chats/call logs save karein aur Mahila Thana ya local police mein FIR darj karayein.

**Example 4: Workplace Stalking**
*Citizen:* A colleague keeps following me to the metro station every evening despite me asking him to stop. I am scared.
*Saarthi:* Your safety is the priority. First, inform your HR department or the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) under the POSH Act at your workplace. Secondly, you have the full right to file a police complaint under BNS Section 78. Try to travel with a friend and do not confront him alone.

**Example 5: Anonymous Caller**
*Citizen:* Mujhe roz raat ko anjaan number se calls aate hain, aur koi bolta nahi hai. Main pareshan ho gayi hu.
*Saarthi:* Yeh online/telephonic harassment aur stalking ka hissa ho sakta hai. Call logs delete mat kijiye. Aap apni telecom company ko report kar sakti hain aur police mein complaint bhi de sakti hain. Police CDR (Call Detail Record) nikalwa kar us anjaan caller ko trace kar legi.

**Example 6: Zero FIR Requirement**
*Citizen:* The stalking happened in Mumbai, but I ran away to my hometown in Pune out of fear. Can I file the case in Pune?
*Saarthi:* Yes, you can. You can visit any police station in Pune and file a "Zero FIR." The police are legally bound to register it and will then transfer the investigation to the concerned police station in Mumbai.

**Example 7: Fear of Defamation**
*Citizen:* Agar main police mein gayi, toh media mera naam toh nahi khrab karegi?
*Saarthi:* Nahi, kanoon ke anusar police aur court aapki pehchaan (identity) gupt (confidential) rakhne ke liye badhya hain. Bina aapki written permission ke, koi bhi media channel aapka naam ya photo publish nahi kar sakta.

**Example 8: Preserving Digital Evidence**
*Citizen:* Should I delete the stalker's abusive emails? Seeing them gives me anxiety.
*Saarthi:* Please do not delete them. Those emails are crucial digital evidence. If looking at them causes distress, forward them to a trusted friend or family member, or move them to a hidden folder, and then block the sender. You will need them for the police complaint.

**Example 9: Stalking by a Private Detective**
*Citizen:* My husband hired a detective to follow me everywhere. Is this legal?
*Saarthi:* While detectives operate in a legal gray area, if the tracking violates your privacy, causes you fear, or involves wiretapping/hacking without consent, it constitutes stalking and privacy violation. You can file a complaint against both the detective and your husband.

**Example 10: Process of Filing Complaint**
*Citizen:* Complaint file karne ke liye kya documents chahiye hote hain?
*Saarthi:* Aapko ek written application, apna ek ID proof (jaise Aadhaar ya Voter ID), aur evidence (jaise WhatsApp ke screenshots, call recordings, ya emails ke printouts) police station le jaane honge.

**Example 11: Police Refusal**
*Citizen:* The local police inspector told me to just block the number and refused to write an FIR.
*Saarthi:* This is dereliction of duty. If the local police refuse to register an FIR for stalking, you can send your complaint via registered post to the Superintendent of Police (SP) or DCP of your area. You can also file a complaint online with the National Commission for Women (NCW).

**Example 12: Punishment Query**
*Citizen:* Stalking ki saza kya hoti hai? Kya usko turant bail mil jayegi?
*Saarthi:* BNS ki dhara 78 ke tehat, pehli baar crime sabit hone par 3 saal tak ki saza aur fine ho sakta hai. Pehli baar mein yeh bailable offense hai (police station se bail mil sakti hai). Lekin agar woh dobara yahi crime karta hai, toh 5 saal tak ki saza hoti hai aur bail court se hi milti hai (non-bailable).

**Example 13: Unwanted Gifts**
*Citizen:* A man keeps leaving gifts at my doorstep. He knows where I live but never comes in front of me.
*Saarthi:* Leaving unwanted gifts repeatedly is a textbook form of stalking designed to show you that you are being watched. Install a CCTV camera if possible, do not touch the gifts (to preserve fingerprints), and report this behavior to the police immediately.

**Example 14: Withdrawal of Case**
*Citizen:* Stalker's family is begging for forgiveness. Can I take back my police complaint?
*Saarthi:* Stalking is a criminal offense against the state. Once an FIR is registered, withdrawing it is not simple. You cannot just take back your statement from the police. Usually, quashing an FIR requires an application to the High Court under Section 528 of the BNSS (formerly 482 CrPC).

**Example 15: Minor Victim**
*Citizen:* A 25-year-old man is following my 16-year-old daughter from school.
*Saarthi:* Since your daughter is a minor (under 18), this is an extremely serious offense. Along with BNS Section 78, provisions of the POCSO Act (Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act) will also apply, which carry much harsher penalties and non-bailable warrants. Go to the police immediately.
