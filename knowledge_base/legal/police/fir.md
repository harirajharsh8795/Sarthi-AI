# First Information Report (FIR)

## Overview
**English:** A First Information Report (FIR) is a critical legal document prepared by the police when they receive information about the commission of a cognizable offence. It sets the criminal law in motion, serving as the foundation for the subsequent police investigation and trial. Under Section 173 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023 (formerly Section 154 of the CrPC, 1973), every citizen has the right to report a crime, and the police are legally bound to register it.

## Quick Summary
An FIR is the official record of a cognizable offence registered under Section 173 of the BNSS, 2023. It registers the date, time, location, and description of the crime, naming suspects and witnesses. It initiates the police investigation and must be provided to the informant free of cost.

## Definition
A **First Information Report (FIR)** is the earliest report of a cognizable offence given to the officer-in-charge of a police station. Although the term "FIR" is not explicitly defined in the BNSS or CrPC, it legally denotes the written recording of oral or written information received under Section 173(1) of the BNSS, 2023.

A **Cognizable Offence** is an offence for which a police officer may, in accordance with the first schedule of the BNSS or under any other law for the time being in force, arrest the accused without a warrant and initiate an investigation without a magistrate's order. Examples include murder, rape, robbery, kidnapping, and theft.

## Why It Matters
Filing an FIR is the mandatory first step to secure justice for victims of serious crimes. It officially establishes that a crime has occurred, forces the police to investigate, secure evidence, and arrest suspects. Delaying or failing to file an FIR can weaken the prosecution's case in court, as the delay might be viewed as an afterthought or fabrication. Knowing how to register an FIR protects citizens from police inaction, corruption, and systemic delays.

## Legal Background
The registration of FIRs has evolved from colonial laws to a citizen-centric digital-era framework:
- **Historical Framework:** Formerly governed by Section 154 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), 1973.
- **Current Legislation:** Governed by Section 173 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023, which came into effect on July 1, 2024.
- **Section 173(1) BNSS:** Permits oral, written, or electronic (e-FIR) submissions. For electronic information, the informant must sign the report within 3 days before it is officially registered.
- **Section 173(2) BNSS:** Mandates that a copy of the FIR be given to the informant free of cost immediately.
- **Lalita Kumari vs. Govt of UP (2014):** A landmark Supreme Court judgment that made the registration of an FIR mandatory for the police if the information discloses the commission of a cognizable offence. No preliminary inquiry is allowed in such cases.

## Applicability
- **Covered:** All individuals (Indian citizens, foreign nationals, victims, witnesses, or even the accused themselves) who have information about a cognizable offence.
- **Exempt:** Non-cognizable offences (minor crimes like simple defamation or cheating without gravity), where police cannot register an FIR without a Magistrate's order. Instead, a "Non-Cognizable Report" (NCR) is recorded.
- **Special Conditions:** For crimes against women (such as stalking, rape, sexual harassment), the FIR must be recorded by a woman police officer. If the victim is temporarily or permanently physically or mentally disabled, the police must record the FIR at the victim's residence in the presence of a special educator or interpreter.

## Rights of Citizens
- **Right to Register:** The police cannot refuse to register an FIR if a cognizable offence has occurred.
- **Right to a Free Copy:** Under Section 173(2) of the BNSS, the informant has the right to receive a copy of the registered FIR immediately and completely free of cost.
- **Right to Zero FIR:** If the incident occurred outside the jurisdiction of the police station you approach, the police must register a "Zero FIR" and subsequently transfer it to the appropriate police station.
- **Right to E-FIR:** Informants can submit reports online, provided they sign it within 3 days.

## Duties & Responsibilities
- **Truthful Information:** The informant is legally responsible for the truthfulness of the report. Providing false information is punishable under Section 217 or 248 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023.
- **Prompt Reporting:** Citizens have a duty to report crimes promptly to prevent destruction of evidence and escape of suspects.
- **Non-Interference:** The informant must not interfere with the police investigation or fabricate evidence to mislead the officers.

## Step-by-Step Legal Procedure

### How to File a Standard FIR
1. **Visit the Police Station:** Go to the nearest police station (preferably the one having jurisdiction over the crime scene).
2. **Present the Information:** Meet the Officer-in-Charge (SHO) or Duty Officer. You can give your statement orally or in writing.
3. **Recording by Police:** If given orally, the officer must write it down and read it back to you to ensure accuracy.
4. **Review and Sign:** Read the written report carefully. If satisfied, sign it. If you cannot write, use a thumb impression.
5. **Collect Copy:** Demand your free physical copy of the FIR with the official stamp and GD (General Diary) entry number.

```text
[Citizen reports crime] ---> [Officer writes & reads back] ---> [Citizen signs report]
                                                                        |
                                                                        v
[Investigation Starts] <--- [Police enter in GD & assign number] <--- [Free copy given to citizen]
```

### What to Do if the Police Refuse to File an FIR
If the local police station refuses to file your FIR, follow these remedies:
1. **Approach the Superintendent of Police (SP):** Send the substance of the information in writing by registered post to the SP of the district under Section 173(4) of the BNSS. If satisfied that a cognizable offence is disclosed, the SP will either investigate the case or direct an officer to do so.
2. **File a Magistrate Complaint:** If the SP fails to act, you can file a private complaint before the Judicial Magistrate under Section 175 of the BNSS (formerly Section 156(3) of CrPC) to direct the police to register the FIR and investigate.
3. **Approach the High Court:** File a Writ Petition under Article 226 of the Constitution of India for directions to the police to register the FIR.

## Required Documents
No formal documentation is mandatory to register an FIR. However, carrying these can expedite the process:
- **Written Complaint:** A drafted letter detailing the incident (keep 2 copies: one for submission, one for your acknowledgment).
- **Identity Proof:** Aadhaar Card, PAN Card, or Voter ID.
- **Evidence Support:** Photos, videos, doctor's prescription (in assault cases), or threat messages on phone (if applicable).

## Government Authorities
- **Police Station (SHO & Investigation Officers):** Primary body responsible for registration and investigation.
- **Superintendent of Police (SP / Commissioner):** Appellate authority in case of local police refusal.
- **Judicial Magistrate Court:** Directs police registration under Section 175 of BNSS and monitors progress.
- **National / State Human Rights Commission:** For complaining against police abuse or refusal.
- **Legal Services Authority (DLSA/SLSA):** Provides free legal counsel to draft complaints.

## Penalties & Punishments
- **BNS Section 248 (False Information):** If a person deliberately gives false information to a public servant to cause them to use their lawful power to injure another person, they can be punished with imprisonment up to 1 year, a fine, or both.
- **BNS Section 217 (Giving false evidence):** Intentionally fabricating false charges can lead to criminal prosecution.
- **BNSS Section 173 Violation by Police:** If a police officer refuses to register an FIR for specific serious offences against women or children, they can face disciplinary action and prosecution under Section 198 of BNS (punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years).

## Exceptions
- **Non-Cognizable Offences:** Minor disputes or civil issues (like rent disputes, family bickering) are recorded in the Non-Cognizable Register, not as an FIR.
- **Preliminary Inquiry Cases:** The Supreme Court allowed a limited preliminary inquiry before registering an FIR in specific categories: matrimonial disputes, commercial offences, medical negligence, corruption cases, or where there is abnormal delay in reporting. This inquiry must be completed within 14 days.

## Important Timelines
- **Registration of FIR:** Must be done immediately upon receipt of cognizable offence details.
- **e-FIR Signature:** Electronic FIRs must be signed by the informant within **3 days** to be registered.
- **Supply of Free Copy:** Must be given **immediately** to the informant.
- **Preliminary Inquiry (if allowed):** Must be completed within **14 days**.
- **Forwarding to Magistrate:** The police must send a copy of the FIR to the magistrate within **24 hours** of registration.

## Common Legal Mistakes
- **Delaying the Filing:** Waiting days or weeks after the crime. If delayed, you must explain the reason in the FIR (e.g., medical treatment, trauma).
- **Providing Vague Details:** Not specifying the exact time, location, or sequence of events.
- **Signing Blank Papers:** Never sign a blank paper or an incomplete FIR form.
- **Including Exaggerations:** Adding false names or escalating minor arguments into major crimes, which can destroy the credibility of your case during court trials.

## Safety Tips
- **Keep Copies Safe:** Save the physical copy of the FIR. Scan it and store a backup on Google Drive or DigiLocker.
- **Note the GD Entry:** Always ensure that your complaint is recorded in the General Diary (Station Diary) if the FIR is delayed.
- **Witness Protection:** If you feel threatened after filing the FIR, immediately notify the SP or magistrate under the Witness Protection Scheme.

## Frequently Asked Questions
1. **What is an FIR?**
   It is a written document prepared by the police containing information about a cognizable (serious) crime.

2. **Who can file an FIR?**
   Any person who is a victim, witness, or has knowledge of a cognizable offence can file it.

3. **Can police refuse to file an FIR?**
   Legally no, if it is a cognizable offence. If they do, it is a violation of Supreme Court guidelines.

4. **What is a Zero FIR?**
   An FIR registered at any police station irrespective of its jurisdiction, which is later transferred to the appropriate station.

5. **Is there any fee for filing an FIR?**
   No, filing an FIR and obtaining its copy is completely free of charge.

6. **What is the difference between a Complaint and an FIR?**
   A complaint is made to a police officer or magistrate for any offence (cognizable or not). An FIR is registered by the police only for cognizable offences.

7. **Can I file an FIR online?**
   Yes, via state police portals (e-FIR), but you must sign it physically or digitally within 3 days for serious crimes.

8. **What happens if I sign a false FIR?**
   You can be prosecuted under BNS Section 248 and face up to 1 year in jail.

9. **Can a phone call to the police be treated as an FIR?**
   A phone call is generally considered information to the police. If it is clear and detailed, it can be recorded as an FIR, but usually requires a signed statement.

10. **Do I have to go to court after filing an FIR?**
    Yes, if the police file a charge sheet, you may be summoned as a witness to testify.

11. **Can an FIR be cancelled once filed?**
    Only the High Court can quash an FIR under Section 528 of BNSS (formerly Section 482 of CrPC). Police cannot cancel it on their own without magistrate approval.

12. **What is a Cognizable Offence?**
    A serious crime (like murder or theft) where police can arrest the accused without a court warrant.

13. **What is a Non-Cognizable Offence?**
    A less serious crime (like simple cheating) where police need a warrant to arrest and cannot investigate without a magistrate's order.

14. **What if the accused is unknown?**
    An FIR can be filed against "unknown persons." The police will investigate to identify them.

15. **Does filing an FIR mean the accused is guilty?**
    No, it only starts an investigation. Guilt is decided only by the court after trial.

16. **Can police arrest someone immediately after an FIR is filed?**
    Yes, if it is a cognizable offence and there is sufficient suspicion or evidence.

17. **Can a minor file an FIR?**
    Yes, a minor can report a crime, though it is usually done with a parent or guardian.

18. **What is a Charge Sheet?**
    The final report prepared by the police after completing the investigation, detailing the evidence against the accused.

19. **What is a Final Report (Closure Report)?**
    A report filed by the police in court when they find no evidence to support the charges in the FIR.

20. **Can I challenge a closure report filed by the police?**
    Yes, you can file a Protest Petition before the magistrate to contest the closure and request further investigation.

## Common Myths vs Facts
1. **Myth:** You must file an FIR only at the police station near the crime scene.
   **Fact:** You can file a Zero FIR at any police station in India.
2. **Myth:** Police can charge money to give you a copy of the FIR.
   **Fact:** The copy must be provided immediately and free of cost.
3. **Myth:** You can be arrested for filing a delayed FIR.
   **Fact:** Delays are common due to trauma. You only need to explain the reason for the delay.
4. **Myth:** Police can investigate any case without an FIR.
   **Fact:** For cognizable cases, registering the FIR is the legal trigger to start the formal investigation.
5. **Myth:** Filing an FIR guarantees the recovery of stolen goods.
   **Fact:** It only initiates the search; recovery depends on the investigation outcome.
6. **Myth:** The police can change the details of an FIR after you sign it.
   **Fact:** Once signed, the FIR cannot be altered. Any subsequent information is recorded separately.
7. **Myth:** A written complaint on plain paper is not valid as an FIR.
   **Fact:** A written complaint is fully valid and often preferred.
8. **Myth:** Only the victim can file an FIR.
   **Fact:** Any witness, neighbor, or person with knowledge of the crime can file it.
9. **Myth:** An FIR can be filed for property boundary disputes.
   **Fact:** Purely civil disputes are handled by civil courts, not via police FIR.
10. **Myth:** E-FIR is valid for murder or rape without physical signatures.
    **Fact:** Serious crimes require verification and signatures within 3 days to become a valid FIR.
